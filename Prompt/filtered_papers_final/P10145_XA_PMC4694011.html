Abstract

Infection with enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli (EHEC) can result in severe disease, including hemorrhagic colitis and the hemolytic uremic syndrome. Shiga toxins (Stx) are the key EHEC virulence determinant contributing to severe disease. Despite inhibiting protein synthesis, Shiga toxins paradoxically induce the expression of proinflammatory cytokines from various cell types in vitro, including intestinal epithelial cells (IECs). This effect is mediated in large part by the ribotoxic stress response (RSR). The Shiga toxin-induced RSR is known to involve the activation of the stress-activated protein kinases (SAPKs) p38 and JNK. In some cell types, Stx also can induce the classical mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs) or ERK1/2, but the mechanism(s) by which this activation occurs is unknown. In this study, we investigated the mechanism by which Stx activates ERK1/2s in IECs and the contribution of ERK1/2 activation to interleukin-8 (IL-8) expression. We demonstrate that Stx1 activates ERK1/2 in a biphasic manner: the first phase occurs in response to StxB1 subunit, while the second phase requires StxA1 subunit activity. We show that the A subunit-dependent ERK1/2 activation is mediated through ZAK-dependent signaling, and inhibition of ERK1/2 activation via the MEK1/2 inhibitors U0126 and PD98059 results in decreased Stx1-mediated IL-8 mRNA. Finally, we demonstrate that ERK1/2 are activated in vivo in the colon of Stx2-intoxicated infant rabbits, a model in which Stx2 induces a primarily neutrophilic inflammatory response. Together, our data support a role for ERK1/2 activation in the development of Stx-mediated intestinal inflammation.

INTRODUCTION

Infection of humans with enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli (EHEC) can cause severe disease, including hemorrhagic colitis and the hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS) (1). As EHEC-associated disease usually is not accompanied by bacteremia (2), EHEC strains are thought to be noninvasive, with infection limited to the intestinal lumen and mucosa. Therefore, the systemic uptake of Shiga toxin (Stx) from the intestine during the diarrheal phase of illness may by the key event promoting systemic disease, including HUS. However, our understanding of exactly how and where Stx exerts its pathogenic effects in the human host is incomplete.

Shiga toxins are ribosome-inactivating proteins (RIPs) that damage 28S rRNA, thereby inhibiting new protein synthesis (reviewed in Vanaja et al. [3]). Stxs are approximately 70-kDa protein complexes comprised of one enzymatically active (ribosome depurinating) A subunit (∼32 kDa) and five B subunits (∼7.7 kDa each). The holotoxin binds via a B subunit pentamer to the glycosphingolipid receptor globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) located on the plasma membrane of target cells, resulting in toxin internalization, retrograde trafficking through the Golgi apparatus to the endoplasmic reticulum, and translocation of the enzymatically active A subunit to the cytoplasm, where it cleaves a specific adenine from the 28S rRNA, thus inactivating the ribosome.

Stx and other toxins that damage the 28S rRNA also can induce the ribotoxic stress response, or RSR. The ribotoxic stress response originally was defined as the activation of c-jun N-terminal kinases (JNK) by certain agents that damage the 28S rRNA, including ricin, anisomycin, and alpha-sarcin (4), but the RSR also may include the activation of other mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs), including p38 and the extracellular receptor kinases 1 and 2 (ERK1/2) (5,–7). It should be noted that protein synthesis itself does not appear to be the key stimulus, as not all protein synthesis inhibitors activate the RSR. Furthermore, in order for the ribotoxic stress response to occur, ribosomes must be actively engaged in protein synthesis, as pretreatment with protein synthesis inhibitors such as emetine, a protein synthesis inhibitor that inactivates ribosomes by stabilizing the 80S polysome (8), or pactamycin, which blocks translational initiation, prevents the RSR (4, 9). Concomitant with the Stx-induced RSR is an upregulation of proinflammatory mRNAs, as well as a paradoxical expression of proinflammatory cytokine proteins, and it is well established that blocking host MAPKs blocks Stx-induced proinflammatory cytokine expression (6, 10,–14).

Different groups (including ours) have observed Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation (12,–15), but the mechanism(s) by which this occurs has not been studied in depth, and the potential contribution of ERK1/2 signaling to disease has not been well studied. Some cell lines respond to Stx with ERK1/2 activation, but others do not (D. M. Jandhyala and C. M. Thorpe, unpublished data) (6, 15, 16). The extent to which Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation contributes to proinflammatory cytokine expression in the intestinal epithelia, or whether Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation is even part of the ribotoxic stress response, is not known but may be relevant in assessing the benefit of specific anti-inflammatory strategies in HUS prevention. Here, we investigate the roles of Stx binding and catalytic activity in ERK1/2 activation in vitro and in vivo and evaluate the contribution of Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation to interleukin-8 (IL-8) mRNA expression in intestinal epithelial cells (IECs). We also explore upstream signaling events leading to Stx-associated ERK1/2 activation, including protein synthesis inhibition, activation of mTOR (mammalian target of rapamycin), and signaling through the mitogen activated protein 3-kinase (MAP3K) ZAK.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Cell culture, materials, and solutions.

The human colonic epithelium-derived HCT-8 cell line was obtained from the American Type Culture Collection and cultured at 37°C in 5% CO2 in RPMI 1640 medium with l-glutamine, 10% fetal bovine serum, 100 U of penicillin G sodium per ml, 100 μg of streptomycin sulfate per ml, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, and 10 mM HEPES, as previously described (11). Cell culture media and additives were from Invitrogen/Life Technologies (Grand Island, NY). QIAshredder cell homogenization spin columns and RNeasy kits were from Qiagen (Valencia, CA). Northern blotting glyoxal gel preparation, transfer reagents, and nylon membranes were from Ambion, Inc. (Austin, TX). [3H]leucine and [α-32P]dCTP were from New England Nuclear (Boston, MA). Bio-Rad protein assay reagent was from Bio-Rad (Hercules, CA). Immobilon-P transfer membranes used for Western blotting were from Millipore (Billerica, MA). Dithiothreitol (DTT), β-glycerol phosphate, leupeptin, phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF), and sodium orthovanadate were obtained from Sigma Chemical (St. Louis, MO). The MEK1/2 inhibitors PD98059 and U0126 were from Calbiochem (La Jolla, CA), and rapamycin was from Cell Signaling Technology (Beverly, MA). DHP-2 was synthesized by Albany Molecular Research Inc. (Albany, NY).

Production and use of wild-type and mutant Shiga toxins.

Wild-type Stx1, StxAY77S,E167QB1, Stx2, StxAY77S,E167QB2, and StxB1 were prepared by the Phoenix Laboratory at Tufts Medical Center (Boston, MA) using receptor analog affinity chromatography as previously described (17, 18). To heat inactivate wild-type holotoxin, an aliquot of active reconstituted toxin was set aside and a paired sample was inactivated by boiling in a water bath for 8 h. Boiled toxin was checked for biological activity and did not inhibit [3H]leucine incorporation in HCT-8 cells (11). The expression plasmids encoding StxAY77S,E167QB1 (pSW09) and StxAY77S,E167QB2 (pNR100) were provided by Tom Obrig of the University of Maryland (Baltimore, MD); the plasmids were constructed in the laboratory of Alison O'Brien at the Uniformed Services University of the Health Sciences (Bethesda, MD) as previously described (18). All toxin/toxoid-producing bacterial strains were deposited in the Phoenix Laboratory. The concentration of Shiga toxins employed in HCT-8 studies (i.e., 1 μg/ml) is based on demonstrated effects of this concentration on [3H]leucine incorporation assays, 3-(4,5-dimethyl-2-thiazolyl)-2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazolium bromide (MTT) cell viability assays, and IL-8 enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) as previously published (11, 19).

RNA isolation and Northern blotting.

Following cell treatments, HCT-8 cells were disrupted using QIAshredders, and total RNA was prepared using RNeasy RNA purification kits according to the manufacturer's instructions (Qiagen, Valencia, CA). For Northern blotting, 10 to 30 μg of total RNA was separated on a glyoxyl agarose gel and transferred to a nylon membrane according to the manufacturer's instructions (Ambion). [α-32P]dCTP-labeled il-8 and gapdh probes were generated and used as previously described (11). The blots were hybridized overnight at 65°C and washed using phosphate-buffered hybridization solutions and wash solutions (20), and hybridized probe was detected via exposure to film.

Preparation of whole-cell extracts, MAPK immunoprecipitation assays, and Western blotting.

Following treatment and removal of cell culture media, cell culture plates were transferred to ice, and cells were washed with cold phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and then scraped off the culture dishes using cold PBS containing 10 μg/ml leupeptin, 1 mM PMSF, and 0.5 mM DTT. Cells were pelleted by centrifugation at 4°C, the supernatant was removed, and pelleted cells were resuspended in lysis buffer consisting of 0.1% Triton X-100, 25 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 300 mM NaCl, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 200 μM EDTA (pH 8.0), 0.1 mM Na3VO4, 20 mM β-glycerol phosphate, 10 μg/ml leupeptin, 1 mM PMSF, and 0.5 mM DTT. The resuspended cells were gently rocked at 4°C for 30 min; cellular debris was removed by centrifugation at 4°C, and supernatants were collected and frozen at −80°C. Protein concentrations of cell extracts were estimated using Bio-Rad (Hercules, CA) protein assay reagent according to the manufacturer's instructions.

For loading whole-cell lysates onto SDS-PAGE gels for Western blotting, equal amounts of protein were added to each lane. The nonradioactive MAPK precipitation assay kit 8794s or 9800 (Cell Signaling Technology, Beverley, MA) was used to assay phosphorylated p44/42 (phospho-ERK1/2) or phosphorylated JNK (phospho-JNK), respectively. Assays were performed per the manufacturer's protocol, except that we used the previously described lysis buffer instead of that supplied by the kit. Briefly, for each sample, phospho-ERK1/2 or phospho-JNK were immunoprecipitated from whole-cell lysate equivalent to 200 μg total protein. Once washed, the samples were treated with kinase buffer containing 0.5 μg kinase substrate (Elk-1 fusion protein for phospho-ERK1/2 or a c-jun fusion protein for phospho-JNK) and 200 μM ATP, followed by incubation at 30°C for 30 min. Western blots were used to visualize relative amounts of phosphorylated substrate, an indicator of phospho-ERK1/2 or phospho-JNK activity. Antibodies used for other Western blot experiments or as loading controls included phosphorylated p44/42 (no. 9101), p44/42 (no. 9102), and phosphorylated mTOR (no. 2971) from Cell Signaling Technology, SC25778 anti-glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) antibody from Santa Cruz Biotechnology Inc. (Dallas, TX), and A2228-200UL anti-β-actin from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO).

Stx2 intoxication of infant rabbits and immunohistochemistry for phospho-ERK1/2.

All animal experiments were preapproved by our institutional animal care and use committee. The treatment of infant rabbits was performed as previously described (21). Briefly, 3-day-old rabbits were given Stx2, StxAY77S, E167QB2, or heat-inactivated Stx2 intragastrically at a dose of 1.0 mg/kg of body weight daily for 2 consecutive days. Initial treatment was noted as day 0, and on day 2, the infant rabbits underwent euthanasia and necropsy. Distal colon tissue was fixed in 10% buffered formalin followed by embedding in paraffin. Five-micrometer-thick sections were deparaffinized and then hydrated. Microwave antigen retrieval was performed at pH 10.00 using an antigen retrieval solution (Dako, Carpinteria, CA). Sections were blocked for endogenous peroxidase activity using 3% H2O2-H2O. To prevent nonspecific protein binding, a serum-free protein block (Dako) was used. Sections then were incubated overnight at 4°C with antibody against phosphorylated ERK1/2 (no. 7383; Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Dallas, TX), followed by incubation with biotinylated anti-mouse secondary antibody. Following treatment with peroxidase-conjugated streptavidin (Dako), antibody binding was detected by using the 3-amino-9-ethylcarazole peroxidase substrate chromogen. Sections then were counterstained with Mayer's hematoxylin. The specificity of staining was confirmed by omitting the primary antibody (negative controls).

RESULTS

Shiga toxin 1 induces ERK1/2 in a biphasic manner in IECs.

Previously, we reported that Stx1-induced ERK1/2 activation in HCT-8 cells contributed to the phosphorylation of the eukaryotic initiation factor eIF4E and, as a result, translational reprogramming (12). However, in that study we did not further characterize the ERK1/2 activation by Stx1. Here, we show the effects of Stx1 holotoxin on ERK1/2 phosphorylation for a period of up to 6 h posttreatment. Whole-cell lysates from untreated HCT-8 intestinal epithelial cells, or cells treated with Stx1 for 15 min to 6 h, were immunoblotted to detect phosphorylated ERK1/2. Results of a representative experiment are shown in Fig. 1. Stx1 induced ERK1/2 in a biphasic manner. ERK1/2 activation was seen as early as 15 min following Stx1 treatment (Fig. 1). This early induction was transient, returning to baseline levels by 45 min, and was not consistently observed in all experiments. ERK1/2 activation consistently recurred at approximately 1 h postexposure and was sustained for a longer period. We next tested the StxB1 subunit and the catalytically inactive mutant StxAY77S,E167QB1 to determine the relative contributions of binding and N-glycosidase activity to Stx1-induced ERK1/2 activation. In this experiment, as an alternative to probing immunoblots directly to detect phosphorylated ERK1/2, we immunoprecipitated phosphorylated ERK1/2 and then used it to phosphorylate ERK1/2 substrate Elk-1 fusion protein. We then performed a Western blot on the reaction mixture and probed for phosphorylated Elk-1 fusion protein as an indicator of ERK1/2 activation. This indirect method is a more sensitive indicator of ERK1/2 activation. As with Stx1 holotoxin treatment, an early, transient ERK1/2 activation also was detected following treatment with only purified StxB1 subunit (Fig. 2A). However, the later and sustained ERK1/2 activation required Stx1 catalytic activity: unlike Stx1 wild-type toxin, the N-glycosidase mutant StxAY77S,E167QB1 (18) did not induce ERK1/2 phosphorylation at 6 h (Fig. 2B). As an additional positive control, cells were treated for 30 min with fresh media containing 60% FBS to induce ERK1/2 activation, or as a negative control, cells were treated for 30 min with fresh media containing 60% FBS plus the MEK1/2 inhibitor U0126 (Fig. 2B, lanes labeled GF30 and U0126 + GF30, respectively).

Other protein synthesis inhibitors, some with differing modes of action, also induce ERK1/2 in IECs.

The stimulus resulting in the RSR has been demonstrated to not be protein synthesis inhibition specifically but instead disruption or damage to the 28S rRNA of actively translating ribosome (4). To determine whether Stx was activating ERK1/2 through the RSR, we compared ERK1/2 activation by ricin, puromycin, and emetine to assess whether ERK1/2 activation is restricted to protein synthesis inhibitors causing 28S rRNA damage or, in contrast, could result from protein synthesis inhibition per se, irrespective of the mechanism by which protein synthesis is abrogated. We compared Stx1, ricin (both of which are known ribotoxic stressors depurinating 28S rRNA at the same site), puromycin (which promotes the premature release of the nascent polypeptide chain [9] and does not induce a ribotoxic stress response in Rat-1 cells [4]), and emetine (which halts protein elongation, possibly through interactions with the 40S ribosomal subunit at the E site) (8, 22). Concentrations used are noted in the legend to Fig. 3, and all were above the approximate 50% inhibitory concentration (IC50) as determined by measuring protein synthesis via [3H]leucine incorporation (data not shown). Interestingly, as with Stx1, puromycin and ricin demonstrated sustained ERK1/2 activation (Fig. 3A), but emetine at 100 ng/ml showed no sustained ERK1/2 activation (see Fig. S1 in the supplemental material). However, very strong ERK1/2 activation was noted following treatment with a 1,000-fold higher dose (100 μg/ml) of emetine (data not shown). Thus, the ability to activate ERK1/2 in HCT-8 cells appeared not to be limited to only classic ribotoxic stressors.

Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation is unaffected by mTOR.

Regulation of Stx1-induced cytokine production by phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase/Akt/mTOR signaling has been observed in macrophage-like THP-1 cells (23). However, direct activation (phosphorylation) of mTOR by Stx1 treatment was not investigated. Furthermore, cross talk between ERK1/2- and mTOR-associated signaling pathways may result in one pathway inhibiting or activating the other (24). Since puromycin and emetine, nonribotoxic stressors (4), are able to induce ERK1/2 activation (Fig. 3A and data not shown) and mTOR can act as a sensor of amino acid accumulation (25), we hypothesized that Stx1-induced protein synthesis inhibition results in amino acid accumulation with subsequent mTOR phosphorylation and possible cross talk with ERK1/2 pathways. Figure 3B shows that Stx1 treatment results in mTOR phosphorylation, and that the inhibition of ERK1/2 activation with the MEK1/2 inhibitor U0126 or PD98059 did not prevent Stx1-mediated activation of mTOR. Furthermore, blocking the mTOR pathway with rapamycin had no effect on Stx1-induced activation of ERK1/2 (Fig. 3C). Pretreatment of cells with U0126 (Fig. 3C) was used as an additional negative control to demonstrate blocking of Stx1-induced ERK1/2 phosphorylation. Together, these data suggest that Stx1-mediated ERK1/2 activation and mTOR activation are distinct in this model system.

Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation is dependent on the MAP3K ZAK.

We previously identified the mixed-lineage kinase ZAK as a MAP3K that transduces the Stx- and ricin-induced ribotoxic stress responses, resulting in p38 and JNK activation (19). Therefore, we tested the hypothesis that Stx1- and ricin-induced sustained ERK1/2 activation is mediated by ZAK and can be considered part of the ribotoxic stress response to these agents. After pretreatment with the ZAK inhibitor DHP-2, cells were exposed to Stx1 or ricin for 4 h. DHP-2 blocked both Stx1- and ricin-induced ERK1/2 activation (Fig. 4A). As Stx2 is more often associated with severe EHEC-associated disease than Stx1 and, as is the case for ricin and Stx1, Stx2 damages ribosomes via depurination of the same site on the sarcin/ricin loop of the 28S rRNA, we tested (i) whether Stx2 activates ERK1/2 and, if so, (ii) whether inhibition of ZAK blocks this ERK1/2 activation. As with Stx1 and ricin, Stx2 activated ERK1/2 (Fig. 4B), and DHP-2 pretreatment blocked this activation by Stx2.

DHP-2 has been shown to have a high specificity for ZAK (26). However, we sought to verify that ZAK was indeed the signaling intermediary required for ERK1/2 activation by Stx. While we have been able to detect ZAK by Western blotting in the Vero cell line, we have been unable to detect it with confidence in HCT-8 cells, possibly due to low-level expression in this cell line. Therefore, we employed a pGipZ lentiviral shRNA delivery system (GE Healthcare/Dharmacon) to generate a stable cell line that could be validated for zak knockdown by qRT-PCR. HCT-8 cells transduced with pGipZ expressing a zak shRNA (Fig. 5A) had decreased zak expression compared to that of HCT-8 cells expressing a gapdh shRNA or nontransduced HCT-8 cells. We evaluated whether the zak knockdown cell line showed a predicted decrease in the ribotoxic stress response, as measured by Stx2-mediated JNK activation (4, 19). Indeed, compared to HCT-8 cells expressing the gapdh shRNA, those expressing the pGipZ zak shRNA construct showed decreased JNK activation (Fig. 5B). As our zak shRNA-expressing pGipZ construct showed decreased zak expression resulting in a decreased Stx2-induced RSR, we evaluated the cell lysates for ERK1/2 activation. As with DHP-2-treated cells, HCT-8s expressing the zak shRNA construct showed decreased Stx2-induced ERK1/2 activation (Fig. 5C). Therefore, like Stx-induced JNK and p38 activation, Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation is part of the ribotoxic stress response and is mediated through ZAK signaling.

Stx1 induces IL-8 mRNA in an MEK1/2-dependent manner in vitro.

We have previously shown that Stx induces IL-8, and that blocking ZAK or p38 decreases Stx-induced IL-8 gene expression and protein production in HCT-8 cells (11, 19, 27). Therefore, we assessed the contribution of Stx1-induced ERK1/2 activation to IL-8 mRNA expression. We used the small chemical inhibitors of MEK1/2, U0126 and PD98059, to block ERK1/2 activation. MEK1/2 inhibition with either U0126 or PD98059 resulted in the abrogation of Stx1-induced IL-8 mRNA expression (Fig. 6A).

DISCUSSION

MAPKs are evolutionarily conserved signaling molecules that influence eukaryotic cellular regulation in response to diverse stimuli, including that associated with environmental stress (e.g., osmotic stress and oxidative stress), with growth and differentiation (hormones and growth factors), and with exposure to pathogens. However, the majority of MAPK pathways identified are associated with stress and inflammatory stimuli as opposed to mitogens (30). MAPK activation can result in a variety of downstream processes, including transcriptional activation, chromatin remodeling, translational reprogramming, and cytoskeletal rearrangement (31). We have previously demonstrated that Stx-mediated activation of p38 and JNK in intestinal epithelial cells is mediated as part of the RSR (27) and is dependent on activation of the MAP3K ZAK (19).

While ERK1/2 activation by Stx1 previously has been demonstrated in macrophage-like THP-1 cells (14), in the ACHN renal tubule-derived cell line (32), and by members of our group in HCT-8 cells (12), the mechanism by which ERK1/2 activation occurred in these studies was not investigated further. In vitro studies have suggested that intestinal inflammation promotes the systemic uptake of Stx through disruption of the epithelial tight junction during neutrophil influx into the lumen (33). Since Stx has been shown to promote IL-8 production, a potent neutrophil chemoattractant, and MAPK activation, including that of ERK1/2, may effect IL-8 production through transcriptional and posttranscriptional mechanisms, we investigated the ERK1/2 activation by Stx to determine if it is mediated as part of the RSR and if it is associated with Stx-induced inflammation. Knowing if classical MAPKs also contribute to the proinflammatory effects of Stx may be important, as therapeutics selectively targeting only one subset of MAPKs may not be fully effective in limiting inflammation.

Based on serological profiling, Shiga toxins are classified into two main types, Stx1 and Stx2 (34). Within each type there are several subtypes, and for reasons not completely understood, EHEC strains that express only Stx2 are more likely to be associated with HUS and severe disease in humans than strains encoding only Stx1 or both Stx1 and Stx2 (1). Supporting this epidemiology are studies that show Stx2 is more cytotoxic in vitro (35) and is associated with more severe pathology in vivo (36, 37) than Stx1.

We demonstrate that both StxA1 and StxB1 subunits can contribute to ERK1/2 activation in intestinal epithelial cells in vitro. In response to holotoxin, there is a biphasic activation of ERK1/2. Early ERK1/2 activation can be observed within minutes of toxin treatment but is not sustained and can be reproduced by treatment with the StxB1 subunit alone, suggesting that this early ERK1/2 activation is triggered by binding of the toxin to its cell surface receptor, Gb3, and/or subsequent retrotranslocation to the Golgi apparatus. That the StxB subunit can promote cellular signaling is supported by studies of other cell types, demonstrating that the StxB subunit is sufficient for the activation of Syk tyrosine kinase in HeLa cells (38), increasing intracellular Ca2+ in BL-Ramos cells (39), activation of protein kinase Cδ in HeLa cells (40), transiently activating p38 in HeLa and HEp-2 cells (41), and promoting von Willebrand factor secretion by human umbilical vein endothelial cells and inducing platelet adhesion following treatment of human glomerular microendothelial cells (42). While earlier studies have suggested that StxB1 subunit, StxB2 subunit, or anti-Gb3 antibody can induce apoptotic signaling in BL-Ramos cells (39, 43), previous in vivo studies by our group suggest that N-glycosidase activity is critical to Stx2-mediated intestinal pathology in infant rabbits, although we have not investigated Stx1 in this model (21).

We also observed a later sustained activation of ERK1/2 by Stx1. Unlike early ERK1/2 activation, late activation (beginning 1 to 2 h posttreatment and sustained up to 6 h) required StxA subunit activity and was dependent on ZAK signaling. It should be noted that the timing of ERK1/2 activation parallels the timing of Stx-induced p38 and JNK activation (27) and protein synthesis inhibition (11). A similar ZAK-dependent ERK1/2 activation also was observed for Stx2 and ricin. Since we have previously shown that ZAK MAP3K activity occurs upstream of Stx2- and ricin-induced p38 and JNK activation (19), our current data suggest that, together with p38 and JNK activation, the late sustained ERK1/2 activation by Stx1, Stx2, and ricin are components of the ribotoxic stress response.

ZAK, also called AZK, MLK7, MRK, or MLTK, belongs to the family of mixed-lineage serine/threonine kinases. The kinase domains of this family contain subdomains, some of which resemble those of serine/threonine kinases, and some of which resemble those of receptor tyrosine kinases (44). The zak gene encodes two splice variants resulting in ∼91-kDa and ∼55-kDa isoforms termed ZAK-α and ZAK-β, respectively. Both isoforms share an identical N-terminal 329 amino acids which contain the kinase domain. Although we are able to do so in Vero cells (1), we have not been able to reliably detect either ZAK isoform in HCT-8 cells by Western blotting, possibly due to low zak expression in HCT-8 cells. As we do not know which isoform(s) is required for the ribotoxic stress response, we used shRNA to target both isoforms.

In addition to being activated by Stx- and ricin-mediated ribotoxic stress, ZAK signaling and/or expression also has been associated with cardiac hypertrophy (45, 46); G2/M cell cycle arrest in response to gamma irradiation (47); metastasis (48); gastric, colon, and bladder cancers (49, 50); doxorubicin toxicity (51); anisomycin treatment; exposure to UV irradiation (26); osmotic shock (52); and oxidative stress (53), suggesting that ZAK transduces a number of different stress responses.

As EHEC strains are thought to be noninvasive (2), the onset of extraintestinal disease (e.g., HUS) requires the systemic uptake of Stx from the intestinal lumen. In vitro studies have demonstrated that the recruitment of neutrophils across epithelial tight junctions can promote the basolateral uptake of Stx (33). Members of our group have previously demonstrated in vitro in HCT-8 cells that despite the inhibition of protein synthesis, Stx paradoxically induces IL-8 production. Furthermore, oral Stx intoxication results in neutrophil influx in the colon of infant rabbits (10,–12, 21). We demonstrate here that late RSR-dependent ERK1/2 activation is required for Stx1-mediated IL-8 expression. Stx previously has been shown to promote synergistic MAPK activation and superinduction of IL-8 when coadministered with other proinflammatory stimuli, such as tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) (11) and flagellin (54). Therefore, through the increased production of neutrophil chemoattractants such as IL-8, Stx-mediated ERK1/2 activation may act to promote the systemic uptake of Stx, leading to the onset of HUS.

That ERK1/2 also are activated in vivo in the infant rabbit further validates a role for this pathway in Stx-associated disease. However, a direct role for Stx in mediating damage to the human colonic epithelium remains controversial, as some reports have suggested that both normal and inflamed human colonic epithelium are devoid of the Stx receptor Gb3 (55, 56). The colon of infant rabbits has been shown to contain Gb3 (21), and in a more recent report, both Gb3 and another Stx receptor, Gb4, were detected on human colonic epithelium and HCT-8 cells (57). In this study, however, Gb3 levels were found to be very low in both human colonic epithelium and in HCT-8 cells, with only ∼0.01% of HCT-8 cells being positive for Gb3. Conversely, Gb4 was found to be abundant in both human tissue and HCT-8 cell culture. Together, these data lend support to the use of HCT-8 cells as a model for Stx-associated disease.

While Stx can induce IL-8 in vitro in HCT-8 cells (11), Miyamoto et al. demonstrated that the major EHEC determinant contributing to chemokine production in human intestinal epithelium was flagellin (58), suggesting that Stx is not a major participant in promoting intestinal inflammation. However, Stx has been shown to act in synergy with other proinflammatory stimuli (11, 14, 54, 59, 60), so it is plausible that Stx exacerbates epithelial inflammation induced by other more potent EHEC virulence factors. Alternatively, Stx may not play a major role in damaging epithelial cells directly during EHEC infection; instead, Stx may intoxicate and activate ERK1/2 signaling in other cells of the human colon, including stromal cells, neuroendocrine cells, lamina propria endothelium, and pericryptal fibroblasts, on which Gb3 surface expression has been demonstrated (55, 57).

In summary, Stx can activate ERK1/2 in vitro in human-derived intestinal epithelial cells, leading to the increased expression of IL-8 mRNA, and ERK1/2 activation can be detected in vivo in the infant rabbit colon following Stx2 exposure. In the infant rabbit model, Stx2 exposure alone results in diarrhea with a proinflammatory histopathology (21), and Stx2 contributes to the inflammation and diarrhea observed following exposure to EHEC (21, 29). Taken together, these data suggest a role for Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation in promoting inflammation. Mechanistically, sustained late ERK1/2 activation is StxA subunit dependent and requires MAP3K ZAK. Thus, along with the activation of the stress-activated protein kinases (SAPKs) p38 and JNK (Fig. 7), Stx-induced ERK1/2 activation can be considered part of the ribotoxic stress response. Blocking the effects of Stx on proinflammatory pathways may be beneficial in preventing the late sequelae of EHEC-associated infection by preventing inflammation-induced intestinal damage and subsequent systemic uptake of Stx. Finally, therapeutic modalities aimed at inhibiting the proinflammatory effects of Stx through targeting ZAK may be more efficacious than those targeting individual downstream MAPK/SAPKs.
