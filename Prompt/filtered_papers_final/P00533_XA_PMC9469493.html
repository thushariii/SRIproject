Abstract

Trefoil factor 3 (TFF3) is a secreted protein with an established oncogenic function and a highly significant association with clinical progression of various human malignancies. Herein, a novel small molecule that specifically targets TFF3 homodimeric functions was identified. Utilizing the concept of reversible covalent interaction, 2-amino-4-(4-(6-fluoro-5-methylpyridin-3-yl)phenyl)-5-oxo-4H,5H-pyrano[3,2-c]chromene-3-carbonitrile (AMPC) was identified as a molecule that interacted with TFF3. AMPC monomerized the cellular and secreted TFF3 homodimer at the cysteine (Cys)57-Cys57 residue with subsequent more rapid degradation of the generated TFF3 monomers. Hence, AMPC treatment also resulted in cellular depletion of TFF3 with consequent decreased cell viability in various human carcinoma-derived TFF3 expressing cell lines, including estrogen receptor positive (ER+) mammary carcinoma (MC). AMPC treatment of TFF3 expressing ER+ MC cells significantly suppressed total cell number in a dose-dependent manner. Consistently, exposure of TFF3 expressing ER+ MC cells to AMPC decreased soft agar colony formation, foci formation, and growth in suspension culture and inhibited growth of preformed colonies in 3D Matrigel. AMPC increased apoptosis in TFF3 expressing ER+ MC cells associated with decreased activity of EGFR, p38, STAT3, AKT, and ERK, decreased protein levels of CCND1, CCNE1, BCL2, and BCL-XL, and increased protein levels of TP53, CDKN1A, CASP7, and CASP9. siRNA-mediated depletion of TFF3 expression in ER+ MC cells efficiently abrogated AMPC-stimulated loss of cell viability and CASPASE 3/7 activities. Furthermore, in mice bearing ER+ MC cell-generated xenografts, AMPC treatment significantly impeded xenograft growth. Hence, AMPC exemplifies a novel mechanism by which small molecule drugs may inhibit a dimeric oncogenic protein and provides a strategy to impede TFF3-dependent cancer progression.

Introduction

The trefoil factor (TFF) family of proteins are identified by the presence of at least one trefoil domain, a conserved sequence of 42–43 amino acid residues containing 6 cysteine residues that form 3 disulfide bonds giving the protein a clover-like trefoil shape.1 Structural analysis has shown that the formation of a stable trefoil structure via disulfide bonding confers protease resistance2 necessary for the proteins to function within the harsh environment of the gastrointestinal tract. The third member of the trefoil factor family of proteins (TFF3) contains 59 amino acids and 1 trefoil domain. It is transcribed as a monomer that may form a homodimer via the presence of a seventh cysteine (Cys57) near the carboxyl terminus.3 TFF3 has been reported to form a more compact and defined homodimer as compared to TFF1, which also contains a single trefoil domain.1 The TFF3 homodimer presents specific clusters of conserved surface hydrophobic residues which have been suggested to be critical for interaction with other proteins.1 Several studies have indicated a significant difference in the activity of the monomeric and dimeric forms of TFF3, suggestive of functional differences between the two forms.4 For example, in inflammatory bowel disease, the deleted in malignant brain tumor 1 (DMBT1) protein exhibits dose-dependent binding to homodimeric TFF3 but not to monomeric TFF3.5 Also, an animal model of experimental colitis showed improvement with administration of homodimeric TFF3 dimer, whereas the monomeric form was without effect.4 Moreover, TFF3 monomer interacted with the CXCR4/7 heterodimer to promote cellular migration whereas, independent of CXCR4/7, the dimeric form of TFF3 activated p44/42 MAPK and increased cell survival.6 Therefore, the dimeric form of TFF3 is functionally competent concordant with other extracellular signaling mechanisms, where dimerization/aggregation of effector molecules by dimeric (or dimerizing) ligands is required for function in a similar manner as human growth hormone,7−9 platelet-derived growth factors,10 and epidermal growth factor11 among others.12

Increased expression of TFF3 has been observed in cervical,13 colorectal,14 endometrial,15,16 gastric,17,18 hepatocellular,19,20 lung (adeno),21 prostate,22 mammary,23−26 and pancreatic27,28 carcinoma, and it is generally associated with poor clinicopathologic and survival outcomes for patients. To date, TFF3 has been reported to bind to CXCR4/7,6 CD147,29 CRP-ductin/DMBT1gp340,30 PAR2,30 and LINGO2.31 As an example, TFF3 binding to LINGO2 prevents LINGO2 repression of EGFR (HER1) signaling.31 In addition, TFF3 has also been reported to enhance the activation of multiple other receptor and cytoplasmic kinase pathways including HER2–4, MET, IGF-1R, and SRC and hence promotes the downstream activity of p44/42 MAPK, NF-κB, PI3K-AKT, and STAT3.20,32−40 Consequently, TFF3 potently promotes cancer cell survival,24−26 anchorage independent growth, migration, invasion, cancer stem cell-like behavior20,24,25 and tumor angiogenesis, growth, and metastasis in vivo.16,24,26,41 Furthermore, TFF3 decreases the sensitivity of various carcinomas to ionizing radiation42 and chemo- and targeted-therapeutics20,25,40,43,44 and mediates acquired resistance to the same. Hence, TFF3 constitutes a validated target in oncology and is a potent driver of oncogenic progression.

Herein, the concept of reversible covalent interaction was exploited using chromene-based organo-nitriles as templates to identify reversible covalent inhibitors that antagonize the TFF3 dimer and its functions. The library of lead structures was synthesized using sequential Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling45 and Knoevenagel condensation reactions,46 and chemical screening was employed to determine apoptotic efficacy in TFF3 positive ER+ MC cells. Among the organonitriles, AMPC emerged as a lead candidate. The specificity and efficacy of AMPC to inhibit oncogenic progression was examined in TFF3 positive carcinoma cell lines and xenograft models. Hence, it is reported that AMPC inhibits TFF3-mediated functions in TFF3-positive carcinoma cells.

Materials and Methods

Cell Culture and Reagents

Detailed information of human normal (immortalized) or cancer-derived cell lines are described in Supporting Information. All cell lines were maintained as per ATCC propagation instructions. All cell-based functional experiments were performed in 2% FBS with the respective media. Construction of wild-type or mutant TFF3 cDNA (cysteine to phenylalanine at cysteine 57 residue) and siRNA-TFF3 constructs was previously described.16,20,24,39 Cells were stably transfected using FuGENE HD (Promega, Singapore) for 24 h, and antibiotic selection was performed as previously described.24N-Acetyl cysteine (NAC), 1,4-dithiothreitol (DTT), and cycloheximide (CHX) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, Singapore.

In Silico Bioinformatic Approaches and Compound Library Synthesis

In silico chemoinformatic approaches and molecular docking analyses were performed as described previously.47 Using Suzuki–Miyaura cross-coupling45 and Knoevenagel condensation reactions46 sequentially, the synthesis of the novel synthetic small molecule library was generated, and their physicochemical characterizations are described in the Supporting Information.

Oncogenic and SPR Analyses

AlamarBlue cell viability, total cell count, foci formation, suspension culture growth, and growth in 3D Matrigel culture were performed as previously described.26,47 Whole cell viability, apoptosis, and cytotoxicity were performed using ApoTox-Glo Triplex Assay Kit, Promega (Singapore) as previously described.47 Phosphatidylserine exposure and cell death were assessed by a BD FACSAria Cell Sorter (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA) using Annexin-V-FLUOS and PI-stained cells as described previously.48 Molecular interactions were analyzed by SPR using a BIAcore-2000 system (BIAcore AB, Uppsala, Sweden). Recombinant human TFF3 (catalog no. SRP3169, Sigma-Aldrich, USA) was immobilized on a sensor chip and analyzed as per the manufacturer’s protocol and as previously described.47

Immunoblot Analysis

Western blot analysis was performed as previously described.38,49,50 Briefly, cells were lysed in RIPA buffer and proteins in the cell lysate were resolved using SDS polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis. The primary antibodies used are tabulated in Table S1. The secondary antirabbit, antimouse, or antigoat horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated antibodies were obtained from Cell Signaling Technology (USA).

Xenograft Analysis

In vivo studies were performed according to guidelines for the care and use of laboratory animals, approved by the Laboratory Animal Ethics Committee, at Jinan University, Guangzhou. Xenografts and histological analysis were performed as previously described20,21,38,47 and briefly described in the Supporting Information.

Pharmacokinetic Studies

Pharmacokinetic analyses were performed by TheraIndx Life sciences Pvt Ltd., Bangalore, India. The animal work followed ethical practices as laid down in the guidelines for animal care (registration number no. 1852/PO/Rc/S/16/CPCSEA). Approval was obtained from the Institutional Animals Ethics Committee (IAEC) of the test facility (protocol no. IAEC/01/2016/024). The pharmacological methodology was performed as described previously47 and briefly described in Supporting Information.

Statistical Analysis

For in vitro assays, the statistical differences among subgroups analyses were compared using an unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-test. All analyses used GraphPad Prism software (Version 5.0). P < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

Identification of a Small Molecule Interacting with TFF3

Homodimeric TFF3 has been reported to promote cell survival in various cell types, whereas monomeric TFF3 does not and, indeed, is reported to be antagonistic.18−27 To verify that the TFF3 monomer functions antagonistically, the cDNA encoding TFF3 in which Cys57 was mutated (TFF3-C57F) was transfected into an ER+ MC cell line with TFF3 expression (MCF-7) and in which depletion of TFF3 reduces cell viability.25,26 Consistently, transfection of TFF3-C57F mutant decreased ER+ MC cell viability (Figure S1A).

As Cys57 of TFF3 is responsible for homodimerization,1 and therefore a potentially druggable site, the concept of covalent reversible inhibition51 was exploited, where heteroaromatic nitriles reversibly react with biological thiols producing a thiomidate intermediate. Covalent reversible inhibition has been previously confirmed by NMR spectroscopic techniques and also resembles the Pinner chemical reaction process52 (Figure S1B).

In this regard, previously reported chromene-based organonitriles53 were utilized as templates, using both ligand (MOLPRINT-2D) and structural bioinformatic methods53 to design potential compounds. Hence, a library of compounds potentially targeting the dimeric form of TFF3 was generated (Figure S1C). These compounds could act as electrophiles and potentially interact with the dimeric form of TFF3.

First, the effect of the synthesized small molecule compound library (ADC series) against two immortalized but otherwise normal human mammary epithelial cell (HMEC) lines (MCF-10A, MCF-12A) and four ER+MC cell lines that express TFF3 (MCF-7, T47D, BT474, MDA-MB-361) was determined by examining IC50 using an AlamarBlue cell viability assay (Figure S1D). Among the series of small molecule compounds, ADC7/AMPC (Figure ​Figure11A) was identified as an efficacious compound reducing the viability of ER+ MC cells compared to vehicle (DMSO) treated cells. The cell viability studies revealed that the 4-fluoro-5-methyl-5-pyridine moiety attached to the phenyl group of the pyrano[3,2-c]chromene ring was essential for the efficient bioactivity of AMPC. DFT calculations of the ADC compounds were performed, which revealed that molecules such as ADC1, ADC3, ADC4, ADC7 (AMPC), and ADC10 exhibit HOMO and LUMO eigen functions at the same end irrespective of their substituent (Supporting Information). All ADC-series molecules possess similar HOMO–LUMO gaps, hardness, electronegativity, and electrophilicity values, suggesting the similar reactivity of the molecules. This group of molecules is more highly reactive than the compounds ADC5, ADC6, ADC8, ADC 9, ADC13, and ADC14, which bear methoxy units at the chromone moiety (Figure S1E). The presence of the methoxy units in these molecules localizes the Fermi (HOMO and LUMO) orbitals at different ends by forming donor–acceptor linkage leading to molecule stability.

Further, molecular docking studies were further performed to explore the direct molecular interactions between AMPC and TFF3. The compounds were docked to the TFF3 dimer (PDB: 1PE3) after identifying the potentially druggable pocket in the interface of the symmetric Cys57-linked dimer.1 Docking was performed using MOE’s default settings and protein preparation tools, using the first conformer of the NMR ensemble and setting Cys57 as cavity center.54 AMPC was predicted to occupy the interfacial cavity between TFF3 monomers, showing a pose with high shape complementarity (Figure ​Figure11B). In the predicted pose, AMPC exhibits mostly hydrophobic contacts across the dimeric interface with Val-19 and Phe-59 of one TFF3 subunit and Gly-35 of the other (Figure S1F). Interestingly, the nitrile group of AMPC is predicted to locate in close proximity to the Cys57-Cys57 disulfide bond in TFF3.

Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) was performed with immobilized recombinant dimeric TFF3 protein using AMPC as the analyte. The overlaid sensorgrams were analyzed using the BIAcore system, and the kinetic parameters were specified in Figure ​Figure11C. The yielded dissociation equilibrium constant (KD) of 1.265 × 10–8 M demonstrated physical and reversible interaction of AMPC with dimeric TFF3 protein.

The effect of AMPC on the dimerization state of TFF3 was examined using recombinant dimeric TFF3 in a cell free system followed by nonreducing SDS-PAGE. Dimeric TFF3 and dimeric TFF1 (the most closely homologous protein to TFF3)1 was monomerized by reduction with DTT, but only TFF3 was monomerized by AMPC (Figure ​Figure11D). Preincubation of AMPC with NAC for 4 h before mixing with dimeric TFF3 protein prevented monomerization of TFF3. Hence, evidence suggests that the AMPC forms a reversible covalent adduct with the TFF3 homodimer, resulting in monomerization of TFF3.

AMPC Requires Cys57 in TFF3

To explore the mechanistic basis of the AMPC effects on cell viability, TFF3 expressing MC cells (MCF-7, BT474) were treated with 10 μM AMPC for 24 h, and TFF3 expression was examined. Surprisingly, AMPC treatment of these cells significantly suppressed both the total cellular and secreted levels of TFF3 (Figure ​Figure11E). To determine the specificity of this effect, we transfected the TFF3-negative ER-negative MC cell line (MDA-MB-231) with either wild-type TFF3 or the TFF3-C57F mutant. AMPC treatment of the respective transfected cells significantly reduced the expression of wild-type TFF3 but did not reduce expression of TFF3-C57F (Figure ​Figure11F). To determine the possible involvement of protein degradation in AMPC-mediated reduction of TFF3 expression, the proteasome inhibitor, MG132, and the lysosome inhibitor, chloroquine, were utilized in vehicle and AMPC treated MDA-MB-231 cells transfected with TFF3 and TFF3-C57F. Both MG132 and chloroquine enhanced TFF3 and TFF3-C57F expression in vehicle treated cells and rescued the AMPC suppression of wild-type TFF3 expression (Figure ​Figure11G). Hence, AMPC promoted degradation of wild-type TFF3 but not TFF3-C57F.

The protein half-life of wild-type TFF3 compared to TFF3-C57F using a cycloheximide chase in MDA-MB-231 cells was next examined. TFF3-C57F exhibited a significantly shorter half-life of 1.6 h compared to wild-type TFF3 (t1/2 of 5.3 h (Figure ​Figure11H) suggestive that monomeric TFF3 is degraded more rapidly than dimeric TFF3. Given that AMPC monomerizes dimeric TFF3 (as above), the effect of AMPC on the half-life of TFF3 in MCF7 and BT474 cells was therefore examined. AMPC treatment significantly reduced the half-life of TFF3 from 5.30 to 1.91 h and 4.58 to 1.59 h in MCF7 and BT474 cells, respectively (Figure ​Figure11I). Hence, AMPC promotes protein degradation of TFF3.

AMPC Stimulates Apoptotic Cell Death in MC Cells with TFF3 Expression

The dose-dependent effect of AMPC on MC cells using a total cell number assay was next evaluated. Treatment of four different ER and TFF3-positive cell lines (MCF-7, T47D, BT-474, MB-MDA-361) with AMPC produced dose-dependent decreases in total cell number with sub- or single digit micromolar IC50 values (Figure ​Figure22A). AMPC treatment of these cells also suppressed the growth of colonies in soft agar and inhibited growth in suspension culture and foci formation compared to vehicle exposed cells (Figure ​Figure22B–D). AMPC also significantly suppressed the growth of pregrown colonies of MC cells in 3D Matrigel as indicated by cell viability and bright-field microscopy visualization (Figure ​Figure22E).

Next, cell viability and CASPASE 3/7 activity of these MC cells after exposure to AMPC or vehicle were examined. Exposure to AMPC significantly (*P < 0.05) decreased cell viability and increased CASPASE 3/7 activities (apoptosis) compared to vehicle-treated cells (Figure ​Figure33A,B). The effect of AMPC on the expression of genes involved in carcinoma cell proliferation and survival were examined in MCF-7 and BT474 cells by qPCR (Figure S3). The mRNA levels of genes commonly downregulated by AMPC in both cell lines include those involved in cell cycle progression such as CCDN1, CDK4, and CCNE1 and those involved in cell survival such as BCL2, BCL2L1, and hTERT. The mRNA levels of genes commonly upregulated by AMPC in both cell lines include those involved in cell cycle arrest such as CDKN1A and those promoting apoptosis such as TP53, BAX, and CASP7. The effect of AMPC on various signaling pathways utilized by TFF3 to promote cell survival and proliferation was further determined by Western blot analysis in the four ER+ MC cell lines and two HMEC lines. The total protein levels of EGFR, p38 MAPK, p44/42 MAPK, STAT3, or AKT were not significantly altered by AMPC treatment (10 μM) in either cell line. In contrast, phosphorylation of all examined proteins exhibited a substantial decrease in MC cells after treatment with AMPC as compared to vehicle controls (Figure ​Figure33D). The expression of proteins involved in cell cycle progression and survival were also examined. CCND1 and CCNE1, cyclin proteins involved in controlling cell cycle progression, were decreased in ER+ MC cells after AMPC exposure. Expression of BCL-2 and BCL-XL, promoters of cell survival, was also decreased by AMPC in ER+ MC cells. In contrast, expression of pro-apoptotic BAX was increased by AMPC exposure in ER+ MC cells along with the tumor suppressor protein TP53, cell cycle regulator CDKN1A, and pro-apoptotic CASP9 (Figure ​Figure33D). Thus, AMPC treatment of ER+ MC cells with TFF3 expression produces apoptotic cell death.

siRNA-Mediated Depletion of TFF3 Diminishes AMPC Effects in ER+ MC Cells

To further determine the functional specificity of AMPC, the effect of AMPC exposure after siRNA-mediated depletion of TFF3 expression in MC cell lines either with (as previously) or without (BT20, MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-436, MDA-MB-468) TFF3 expression was examined, as represented in Figure ​Figure44. In ER+ MC cells with expression of TFF3, CASPASE 3/7 activity was significantly increased and cell viability was decreased in control transfected cells treated with AMPC as compared to vehicle treated cells. Transfection of TFF3 expressing ER+ MC cells with siRNA directed to the TFF3 transcript decreased TFF3 expression (Figure ​Figure55A), increased CASPASE 3/7 activity, and decreased cell viability compared to control cells (transfected with scrambled oligo). In TFF3 expressing ER+ MC cells with siRNA-mediated depletion of TFF3 and in ER- MC cells without TFF3 expression, no appreciable effect of AMPC on CASPASE 3/7 activity nor cell viability was observed (Figure ​Figure44B,C). Hence, AMPC exhibits functional specificity to TFF3 expression.

Next, the IC50 of AMPC in a wide-range of carcinoma cell lines including those derived from endometrial, lung, hepatocellular, colon, prostate, pancreatic, thyroid, gastric, and mammary carcinoma were determined. That AMPC did not affect immortalized cell lines and only generated high IC50 values (approximately 400 μM) in TFF3 negative ER- MC cell lines indicates the relative selectivity of the compound for cells with TFF3 expression.

Pharmacological Features of AMPC

The pharmacological features of AMPC were determined. The solubility of AMPC was determined as 67.7 μM (30.8 μg/mL, at 3 h) and 77.1 μM (35.1 μg/mL, at 24 h) at ambient temperature in Universal Buffer pH 7.4. The permeability of AMPC was determined to be 0.6 × 10–6 cm/s (16 h), which falls between the effective permeabilities (Pe) values of caffeine and quinidine. AMPC also demonstrated a moderate degree of retention to the membranes used in these assays (29% at 16 h). AMPC demonstrated good permeability in Caco2 assays and high metabolic stability after incubation with rat liver microsomal membranes (Figure S4A–C). The pharmacokinetic characteristics of AMPC were determined via intravenous (IV) and oral administration in Wistar rats (Figure S4E,F). Following a single 2 mg/kg IV dose, AMPC showed biexponential disposition with moderate clearance [∼50% of RLBF] and a high volume of distribution with a t1/2 = 2h. Following a single oral dose of 10 mg/kg, AMPC showed rapid absorption followed by a biexponential disposition with tmax = 0.25 h, Cmax = 467 ng/mL, AUCinf = 1372 ng·h/ml, t1/2 = 4.3 h, and a moderate bioavailability of 23%.

AMPC Inhibits Xenograft Growth In Vivo

The in vivo efficacy of AMPC was examined in a xenograft model of ER+ MC. Randomly grouped mice with preformed xenografts (volume ∼150–200 mm3) were injected every second day i.p. with vehicle, AMPC 5 mg/kg, or AMPC 20 mg/kg. A significant dose-dependent reduction in xenograft volume was observed in AMPC-treated mice as compared to their vehicle-treated counterparts (Figure ​Figure55A). The animal weight was unchanged between the groups, indicating a general favorable tolerance of the drug (Figure ​Figure55A, lower). The xenograft weight of AMPC-treated animals was reduced in a dose-dependent manner compared to vehicle-treated mice (Figure ​Figure55B). Immunohistochemical analyses of the AMPC-treated xenograft specimens resected from the animals showed significantly decreased TFF3 expression compared to vehicle-treated xenograft specimens (Figure ​Figure55C). Congruently, AMPC-treated xenograft specimens also showed a decreased percentage of Ki67 positive cells and significantly increased TUNEL positivity compared to vehicle-treated xenograft specimens (Figure ​Figure55D,E).

Discussion

Herein, the discovery and characterization of an aminonitrile, AMPC, which monomerizes the TFF3 homodimer is reported. Other examples of nitrile-based drugs such as the gliptins (anagliptin, vildagliptin, and saxagliptin), inhibitors of dipeptidyl peptidase 4 used for glycaemic control,55 and clopidogrel which inhibits ADP-dependent platelet aggregation56 are generally efficacious and well tolerated. Hence, AMPC is a potential lead candidate to inhibit the pivotal functions of TFF3 driving cancer progression and poor patient prognosis.23,26

It is demonstrated herein that monomeric TFF3 reduces cell viability and the generation of monomeric TFF3 by AMPC also antagonized the homodimeric TFF3 stimulation of cell survival (Figure ​Figure33). The antagonistic effect of TFF3 monomers has also been reported previously in various models17,20,22,24,43 and is consistent with dimeric or dimerizing ligand interactions responsible for receptor activation and downstream signaling events.20,32−40 One advantage of this approach of using a small molecule to generate a biologic antagonist (monomeric TFF3) is that AMPC would only generate TFF3 monomers at sites of TFF3 expression, such as occurs in various carcinomas.13−28 This effect would presumably generate high concentrations of the monomer within the tumor mass, thereby improving targeting specificity. Furthermore, generation of TFF3 monomer within the cancer would inhibit both autocrine effects of TFF3 and paracrine interactions with the tumor microenvironment. In addition, a second potential effector mechanism of AMPC is that the generation of monomeric TFF3 subsequently promotes monomeric TFF3 degradation through proteolytic pathways, resulting in decreased total TFF3 levels in cells and the extracellular space. Small molecule promoted monomerization of Survivin has also been demonstrated to promote proteolytic degradation of Survivin.57 Exposure of the dimeric interface of TFF3 by AMPC may produce conformational changes as reported for other proteins58,59 with resulting instability promoting proteolytic degradation.60 Thus, concordant with a multitude of si/shRNA studies16,20,21,24,40 the depletion of TFF3 from various cancer models produces the loss of carcinoma cell viability through apoptosis. This target degrading feature of AMPC is reminiscent of PROTAC-based (proteolysis-targeting chimeras) molecules.61 However, to date PROTACs are limited by larger molecule size generally resulting in poor oral availability and hence are administered intravenously,62 whereas AMPC exhibits acceptable bioavailability. One further advantage is that AMPC also generates the antagonistic monomer from the cell survival promoting dimeric TFF3. However, which of the two potential mechanisms of AMPC action is primarily responsible for AMPC efficacy is not determined. Hence, AMPC disruption of dimeric TFF3 provides a dual and effective mechanism to inhibit TFF3 promotion of cell survival in cancer.

There are multiple clinical indications for use of a TFF3 inhibitor in oncology. TFF3 is a validated target in oncology with TFF3 expression associated with poor survival outcomes in a range of cancers2,26 and functionally drives cancer progression.13,20,24 Increased expression of TFF3 protein is also functionally associated with decreased sensitivity and acquired resistance to both chemo- and targeted therapies in hepatocellular, gastric, lung, colorectal, and mammary carcinoma.6,20,39,40,43,44 AMPC or derivatives would therefore find utility in various cancers in combination with existing chemotherapeutics such as doxorubicin20,40 and 5-FU44 and targeted therapeutics such as tamoxifen,16 trastuzumab,39 and MEK inhibitors43 among others. AMPC may also be useful as a single agent in resistance situations where the cancer has acquired dependence on TFF3 for cellular survival. This appears to the case with doxorubicin-resistant ER+ breast cancer which is highly sensitive to TFF3 inhibition and wherein AMPC produces regression in xenograft models.40

TFF3 has been reported to enhance the activation of multiple receptor and cytoplasmic kinase pathways,63 and hence the cellular effects of TFF3 are likely to be broad, as observed.2,6,13,20,24,26,39,40,43,44 Additionally, TFF3 was recently demonstrated to also utilize WNT signaling pathways by enhancing expression of multiple WNT ligands,64 and hence the effector pathways of TFF3 may not yet be fully described. Herein, the effect of AMPC on a limited set of TFF3 effector pathways and genes was examined (Figure S3). However, the cellular effects of AMPC are presumed to be wider than this limited set of pathways/genes examined and will be the focus of future endeavors. Given that AMPC possesses a reactive functional group (aminonitrile group) capable of forming covalent links to target proteins, there exists the issue of nonspecific and off-target effects especially at higher concentrations. Delineating TFF3 regulated genes in a specific disease model and determining if AMPC regulates these genes, or a subset of these genes, or exerts effects outside of this gene set, may be useful to determine potential off-target effects of AMPC. Such an approach will, however, be complicated by the observation that both monomeric and dimeric TFF3 exert independent cellular functions,4,65 and the approach may be further complicated by heterodimerization of TFF3 with other proteins.63 It should be noted that AMPC administration would presumably need to be biomarker driven with companion diagnostics to determine which patient would respond to AMPC treatment. Tumor expression of TFF3 itself would seem suitable in this regard,21,43,44 although there may be a need to account for downstream mutations in effector pathways utilized by TFF3, akin to loss of EGFR inhibitor efficacy in KRAS mutant colon carcinoma.66 Furthermore, as above, the existence of monomeric TFF3 or heterodimeric TFF3, in addition to homodimeric TFF3 targeted herein, may complicate using TFF3 expression as a companion diagnostic to AMPC.

In conclusion, AMPC is a novel small molecule inhibitor of TFF3 dimerization that effectively inhibits TFF3 driven pro-survival mechanisms and induces apoptosis in cancer cells.
