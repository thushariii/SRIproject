Abstract

Background

The purpose of this study was to explore the influencing factors of serum uric acid (SUA) level and related gene polymorphisms in the healthy population.

Methods

A total of 346 healthy individuals screened from different areas in Shenyang City and 195 patients with high SUA levels were included.

Results

The levels of TC (total cholesterol), HDL-C (high-density lipoprotein cholesterol), LDL-C (low-density lipoprotein cholesterol), TG (triglycerides), GLU (blood glucose) ALT (alanine aminotransferase), TBA (total bile acid), TBIL (total bilirubin), CR (creatinine) and CYSC (Cystatin C) were statistically different between the healthy and hyperuricemia population (P<0.05). However, there was no statistical difference in the UA level between the two groups (P>0.05). After adjusting for UA, TC, HDL-C, LDL-C, GLU, TBIL and CYSC, the additive and recessive models of rs2231142 were statistically significant in females (P<0.05). For males, haplotypes of A-C-A-A-G-G, A-C-G-C-G-G and A-T-G-A-A-G had significant difference between the healthy and hyperuricemia population (P<0.05). For females, the haplotypes of A-C-G-C-G-G and A-T-A-C-A-T had significant differences (P<0.05).

Conclusion

The distributions of SLC2A9 (solute carrier family 2 and facilitated glucose transporter member 9), ABCG2 (ATP-binding cassette G2), GCKR (glucokinase regulatory protein), KCNQ1, IGFIR (Insulin-like growth factor-I receptor) and VEGFR (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor Receptor) were balanced in the population in Shenyang City. The haplotypes of A-C-A-A-G-G, A-C-G-C-G-G and A-T-G-A-A-G were the influencing factors of high SUA in the population in Shenyang City.

Keywords: gene polymorphisms, hypemricemic population, serum uric acid

Introduction

Gout is a common and treatable disease caused by the deposition of monosodium urate crystals in joint and non-joint structures (1). Gout is mainly caused by the disorders of purine metabolism and uric acid (UA) excretion, which can invade bones, joints, kidneys, and cardiovascular systems and seriously threaten human life and health (2). In clinical practice and research, hyperuricaemia (blood urate concentration over the saturation threshold) is typically reported when serum urate is higher or equal to 0.42 mmol/L (7 mg/dL) (3). Hyperuricemia often causes the deposition of urate crystals in different parts of the body, causing corresponding damage, such as gouty arthritis, tophi, urolithiasis, etc. (4,5). Current studies have found that increased urate production in the liver and decreased urate excretion in the kidney and intestine are the main mechanisms leading to the increase of SUA levels. About two-thirds of the clearance of SUA is carried out through the kidney, while the rest is mainly through the intestine (6).

Genome-wide association study (GWAS) is the main research method that has been widely used in gout genetics in recent years (7,8). Köttgen et al. have conducted a GWAS in more than 140000 Europeans, and the results show that 28 different gene loci were statistically significantly associated with SUA level (8). They have confirmed several gene loci related to urate metabolism that have been previously identified by small-scale GWAS (9-11). The loci of SLC22A11 (organic anion transporter 4), SLC22A12( URAT1 urate transporter 1) SLC17A1(NPT1, dependent phosphate cotransporter type 1 ), PDKZ1(PDZ domain containing 1), SLC2A9(solute carrier family 2 and facilitated glucose transporter member 9, GLUT9), and ABCG2(ATP-binding cassette G2) are the major genes involved in UA transporters in the kidney and intestinaltissues. Among them, SLC2A9 and ABCG2 are the main mutation genes, which could explain 3% - 4% of the change of the UA level. Therefore, in recent years, researchers are interested in the molecular basis and clinical significance of SLC2A9 and ABCG2 in the control of urate levels.

At present, some progress has been made in the screening of susceptibility genes for gout and hyperuricemia (12-17). There are relatively many studies on SLC2A9 and ABCG2, and the polymorphisms of these two gene loci occur more frequently in Asians, Europeans, and Americans. In addition to the above two gene loci, we also included four other gene loci related to uric acid metabolism, including GCKR(glucokinase regulatory protein) (rs1260326), KCNQI (rs179785), IGFIR(Insulin-like growth factor-I receptor) (rs6598541), and VEGFA (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor A) (rs729761). Among them, GCKR and KCNQI have a higher incidence in Chinese, while IGFIR and VEGFA have a higher incidence in Europeans. In this study, we selected six loci related to gout and hyperuricemia as the research objects and compared the genotype carrying frequency and allele carrying frequency of these six loci in healthy and hyperuricemia populations in Shenyang City by detecting the genotypes of the subjects. We aimed to investigate the effect of high-risk genotypes of gout and hyperuricemia on genetic susceptibility in the population in Shenyang City, China.

PATIENTS and Methods

Materials

This study was approved by the ethics committee of the First Affiliated Hospital of China Medical University. All subjects provided informed consent. From January 2008 to December 2011, 346 healthy controls were selected from different communities in Shenyang as a control group. In this study, healthy controls were defined as physical and mental health, having normal learning and memory ability, having certain social communication ability, and adaptability. A total of 119 patients with hyperuricemia treated in the Department of general medicine, the First Affiliated Hospital of China Medical University from March 2016 to December 2016 were a disease group. Inclusion criteria: BUA > 420 mmol/L in male and > 360 mmol/L in female. Exclusion criteria: (1) patients with a malignant tumor, severe liver and kidney disease, thyroid disease, and other autoimmune diseases; (2) patients taking drugs that affect blood uric acid; (3) patients in acute stress state; (4) patients with increased blood uric acid caused by secondary kidney diseases.

Biochemical index detection

All subjects fasted for at least 10 h before blood draw. Fasting venous blood was taken the next morning. The blood samples were centrifugated at 12000 rpm for 10 min to collect the supernatant. The levels of TC(total cholesterol), HDL-C(high-density lipoprotein cholesterol), LDL-C(low-density lipoprotein cholesterol), TG(triglycerides), GLU(blood glucose) ALT(alanine aminotransferase), TBA(total bile acid), TBIL(total bilirubin), CR(creatinine) and CYSC(Cystatin C) were tested by Hitachi 7600 automatic biochemical analyzer with corresponding matching kits (Beckman Company, USA).

DNA extraction

The DNA was extracted with a blood genomic DNA extraction kit or 0.1-1ml blood clot genomic DNA extraction kit (Tiangen Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Nanodrop 2000 full wavelength UV / Vis scanning spectrophotometer (Thermo, Germany) was used to determine the content of DNA. The concentration of extracted DNA was more than 10 ng/µl.

Genetic testing

Primers were designed according to the SNP sequences of SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCKR, KCNQ1, IGFIR, and VEGFR (Table 1). The PCR system was constructed with KASP commercial kits (KBD primer company). The reaction parameters of PCR are as follows: Activation for 4 min at 94℃; Denaturation for 20 s at 94℃ and Annealing/Elongation for 60 s at 55-61℃ for 10 cycles; Denaturation for 20 s at 94℃ and Annealing/Elongation for 60 s at 55℃ for 26 cycles.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis was done by IBM SPSS 25.0 software. The counting data were expressed by frequency. The EpiData software was used to establish the database and input the data. The SNPs of SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCKR, KCNQI, IGFIR and VEGFR in healthy and hyperuricemia populations were analyzed by gender stratification. The Kolmogorov-Smirnov method was used to test whether it conformed to the normal distribution. The distribution difference was detected by the X2 test. If the theoretical frequency T < 5 or < 1, Fisher’s exact probability method was used. Additive, dominant and recessive models were used to calculate the risk of hyperuricemia. Haploid analysis was performed by using she SHEsis software to analyze whether the polymorphisms of SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCKR, KCNQI, IGFIR, and VEGFR genes were independent risk factors for hyperuricemia.

Linkage disequilibrium and haplotype analysis in male

Table 9 showed that SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCKR, KCNQ1, IGFIR, and VEGFA genes formed 13 haplotypes with a combined frequency of more than 3%. The highest frequency was A-T-G-C-G-G, accounting for 16% of males. However, there was no statistical difference in the distribution between hyperuricemia and a healthy population. The distribution of haplotypes of A-C-A-A-G-G, A-C-G-C-G-G, and A-T-G-A-A-G in hyperuricemia and the healthy population was statistically different (P<0.05). The haplotypes of A-C-A-A-G-G and A-T-G-A-A-G showed the risks of hyperuricemia were 2.446 and 5.483, respectively. A-T-G-A-A-G haplotype was a protective factor, which could reduce the occurrence of hyperuricemia.

Discussion

Gene polymorphisms are greatly related to the susceptibility of diseases. In this study, we found that the haplotypes of A-C-A-A-G-G and A-T-G-A-A-G showed the risks of hyperuricemia, while A-T-G-A-A-G haplotype was a protective factor, which could reduce the occurrence of hyperuricemia.As an inflammatory disease, gout is mainly manifested as acute recurrent inflammatory arthritis. Gout not only invades the bone and joint system but also easily affects the kidney and cardiovascular system, posing a serious threat to human life and health. Elevated SUA levels occur in about a quarter of patients with gout (hyperuricemia: SUA ≥ 0.42 mmol/L) (18).

In recent years, with the widespread application of GWAS, especially the continuous advancement of common genome genetic variation and phenotypic changes, 28 gene loci are closely related to the pathogenesis of gout and hyperuricemia. At present, the most studied genes are solute carrier family 2 member 9 (SLC2A9) and ABCG2, which are related to uric acid excretion in the kidney and intestine. SLC2A9, also known as glucose transporter 9 (GLUT9), is known to be a voltage-dependent uric acid transporter, which is related to the glucose metabolism and transport in the liver, kidney, pancreas, and β cells of pancreatic islets, and is closely related to the occurrence of hyperuricemia (19-21).

SLC2A9 can be used as a transporter to transport carbohydrates and uric acid at the same time. Individuals homozygous for alleles at significantly related sites are more likely to cause increased blood urate (22). Dehghan et al. found the first single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) related to hyperuricemia and gout in a small region on chromosome 4 through GWAS research, and this region is located in the same region as the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter gene (ABCG2). ABCG2 protein was mainly expressed in the parietal membrane of the small intestine and colon epithelium, the parietal membrane of renal proximal convoluted tubule epithelium, and the bile duct membrane of liver cells (23,24). However, ABCG2 expression in the kidney is lower than that in the small intestine and liver (25). For the SNP, rs2231142, which is most closely related to hyperuricemia, the studies show that it encodes an amino acid substitution at position 141 (Q is replaced by K) (26-28). This kind of mutation is more common. The allele frequency is 0-35%, and the homozygous Q141K mutation has a lower expression of the transporter than the heterozygous. Therefore, the stability of the mutant ABCG2 is significantly reduced. The genetic basis of ABCG2 is simpler than that of SLC2A9, and its related signals are reported to be completely driven by the gene mutation of rs2231142 (Q141K) (29). Interestingly, the allele rs2231142 (q141k), which increases uric acid levels, is associated with uric acid output in urine (30, 31).

Nakayoma et al. calculated the percentage of population attributable risk (PAR%) of known major risk factors for hyperuricemia, including obesity, alcoholism, age, and ABCG2 dysfunction. The PAR of ABCG2 dysfunction calculated by them was 29.2%, which was almost twice as high as that of the secondary risk factor obesity (18.7%) (32). ABCG2 dysfunction may increase the risk of hyperuricemia, gout, hypertension, stroke, and metabolic diseases in hundreds of millions of individuals (33), given the high frequency of mutation alleles (0.11 in European, 0.31 (34) in Japanese and 0.31 (35) in Han population).

In this study, the gene distribution of healthy and hyperuricemia populations is balanced, in line with the law of genetic balance. There are significant differences in HDL, LDL, TG, GLU, ALT, TBA, and CYSC between male healthy and hyperuricemia populations, while there are significant differences in UA, TC, HDL, LDL, GLU, TBIL, and CYSC between female healthy and hyperuricemia population. After adjusting for the above factors according to gender, the additive model and recessive model of rs2231142 in the ABCG2 gene are statistically significant in females. Among the six loci, ABCG2 may be a susceptible gene.

GCK is the first key enzyme in the glycolysis pathway, which maintains the stability of glucose in the body by promoting glucose metabolism in the liver and regulating insulin release. GCK is associated with DM. The activity of GCK is affected by glucokinase regulatory protein (GCKR), which is an endogenous inhibitor of GCK. GCKR plays an important role in both glucose regulation and uric acid metabolism. Whether the effect of GCKR on uric acid is mediated by glucose regulation needs further study. The T allele of rs1260326 is associated with a lower level of fasting blood glucose and INS, HOMA-IR index and T2DM risk, and higher TG level. rs1260326 polymorphism is the strongest association signal in the GCKR gene region (36, 37).

The incidence of igfirrs6598541 and vegfars729761 is higher in Europeans than in Chinese. KCNQ1 is an important gene locus in recent 2-3 years. In 2015, Li et al. have identified three new loci (38), and have recognized that the loss of KCNQ1 function leads to changes in innate immune response (39), mainly involving islets β cell function. KCNQI may be a diabetes susceptible gene, and its risk allele can affect the secretion and response of islets β cells to insulin (40,41), thus affecting the excretion of uric acid (7, 42).

Haplotype analysis shows that there are three frequencies of the six loci in males and two frequencies in females. In particular, haplotype A-C-G-C-G-G is a protective factor in both males and females. However, its incidence is very low, 4% in males and only 2% in females. A-C-A-A-G-G and A-T-G-A-A-G are risk factors in males, while A-T-G-A-A-G is a risk factor in females.

There are also some limitations to this study. For example, the sample size is small. In this study, if the exclusion criteria are stricter and the causes such as obesity and metabolic factors are excluded, the number of subjects with hyperuricemia will be less, and the gene incidence will not be high. If the sample size is further reduced, the detection rate will be lower. Therefore, this study is mainly to exclude the patients with increased blood uric acid levels caused by secondary diseases.

In the six loci related to the onset of gout and hyperuricemia, SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCKR, KCNQI, IGFIR, and VEGFR, the distribution of the genotypes is balanced. There are differences in the levels of HDL, LDL, TG, GLU, ALT, TBA, CYSC between male healthy and hyperuricemia populations. There are significant differences in UA, TC, HDL, LDL, GLU, TBIL, CYSC between female healthy and hyperuricemia populations. After adjusting the influencing factors of UA, TC, HDL, LDL, GLU, TBIL, and CYSC, the additive model and recessive model of rs2231142 are statistically significant in females.

In conclusion, the distributions of SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCKR, KCNQ1, IGFIRand VEGFRwere balanced in the population in Shenyang City. For males, haplotypes A-C-A-A-G-G and A-T-G-A-A-G are risk factors for hyperuricemia, and haplotype A-C-G-C-G-G is a protective factor that can reduce the occurrence of hyperuricemia. For females, haplotype A-T-A-C-A-T is a risk factor, haplotype A-C-G-C-G-G is a protective factor.
