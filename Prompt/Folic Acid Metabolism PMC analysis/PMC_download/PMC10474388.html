Effect of 38% Silver Diamine Fluoride on Salivary Matrix Metalloproteinase-9 Levels in Children with Early Childhood Caries: A Clinical Study

ABSTRACT

Background

Early childhood caries is a major oral health problem in most industrialized countries, affecting the overall quality of life of children. It is caused by the action of bacterial acids on the enamel surface thereby demineralizing it and progressively destroying the tooth. It presents initially as smooth-surface carious lesions affecting the primary maxillary incisors. With the advancement of the lesion, decay progresses further to involve other primary teeth as well and if not treated early, the caries may involve the pulp leading to irreversible pulpitis. Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) are a group of endopeptidases that degrade almost all the proteins of the extracellular matrix (ECM). Among the different types of MMP, MMP-9 (gelatinase B) is one of the chief MMPs responsible for the breakdown of the organic matrix. Traces of MMP-9 can be found in the human carious lesions, saliva, and gingival crevicular fluid. They are activated by the release of acids by cariogenic bacteria and once activated they are able to digest demineralized dentin matrix. Thus, host-derived MMPs, which are activated by bacterial acids have a crucial role in the destruction of dentine by caries. Thus, this study was undertaken to investigate the effect of 38% silver diamine fluoride (SDF) solution on MMP-9 levels.

Aim

This clinical trial investigated the MMP-9 levels before and after the application of 38% SDF in children with early childhood caries.

Materials and methods

About 15 children were selected and were subjected to clinical and radiographic assessment before the commencement of the procedure. After the collection of saliva, the teeth with carious lesions were isolated and SDF was applied. Saliva samples were again collected after 1, 3, and 6 months postapplication of SDF. The collected saliva samples were then analyzed for their MMP-9 levels using a human salivary enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) kit. Finally, the data obtained were subjected to statistical analysis by repeated measures of analysis of variance (ANOVA).

Conclusion

The MMP-9 levels were found to be gradually increasing postapplication of 38% SDF and were significantly higher after 6 months and the highest mean difference in the MMP-9 levels was observed between baseline and 6th-month follow-up. Also, no new carious lesion appeared in the teeth during the experimental time period of 6 months and the decayed, extraction needed, filled teeth (deft) scores remained the same.

Introduction

Early childhood caries is one of the most common oral health-related problems in various parts of the world, affecting the overall well-being of children. It is caused by the action of bacterial acids on the enamel surface thereby demineralizing it and progressively destroying the tooth. In the event of a caries exposure, the mineralized portion of the dentin is dissolved and the demineralized organic matrix is susceptible to bacterial and host-driven enzymes such as matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs). MMPs derived from the host can originate both from dentin to saliva. They are activated by the release of acids by cariogenic bacteria and once activated they are capable of digesting the demineralized dentin matrix.1 Thus, host-derived MMPs, which are activated by bacterial acids have a crucial role in the destruction of dentine by caries.2

Minimal intervention dentistry is a concept that not only emphasizes the symptoms of the disease but is also concerned with the causes of dental diseases. The ideology of minimal intervention dentistry involves the prevention of disease and the preservation of the integrity of the tooth structure.3 With the increase in the understanding of caries progression and the introduction of adhesive restorative materials, the concepts of minimal intervention dentistry have evolved; the core being the earliest detection and prevention of disease, succeeded by less invasive treatments.

Remineralizing agents like topical fluorides and casein phosphopeptide-amorphous calcium phosphate (CPP-ACP) to the use of resin infiltration, minimal intervention dentistry has become an integral part of the dental practice in the current era. Recent research has found that treating carious lesions while taking into account their clinical, ultrastructural, microbiological, and chemical characteristics yield good outcomes. It has been observed that there is a reduction in bacterial development, lack of germs, or inactivation after partial removal of carious dentine and hermetic closure of cavities. As a result, the carious lesion does not advance deeper into the tooth.

Silver diamine fluoride (SDF) is a solution that is comprised of silver, ammonia, and fluoride which when applied to the decaying tooth surface, significantly arrests cavitated as well as incipient carious lesions. It is indicated in cases when restorative treatment for primary teeth is not an option. The United States Food and Drug Administration approved SDF in 2014 for hypersensitivity.4 A nonsurgical alternative to managing caries in communities where surgical management of decay is not an option is to utilize SDF as a preventive or therapeutic modality. This can delay dental treatment until a child is more understanding and cooperative.5 Silver and fluoride, in an alkaline environment have a concurring effect in arresting dentinal caries as a result of which the collagen enzyme activation fails to succeed.

Denaturation of matrix proteins occurs in inflammation of the pulp and it is believed that turnover of the matrix requires the activity of many different endopeptidases that act on multiple proteins of different composition. These extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins are degraded by a family of endopeptidases called MMPs.6 They are involved in the normal turnover of the ECM. ECM proteolysis appears to be a major triggering event for the progression of inflammatory processes such as rheumatoid arthritis and periodontal disease.7 In addition, gelatin zymography shows that MMP-9 is upregulated in inflamed pulp tissue. The data obtained suggest an important role for MMP-9 in the destruction of human pulp tissue.8 Therefore, inhibition in the MMP-9 levels may contribute to halting caries progression.

Hence, the purpose of this study was to evaluate and compare the salivary levels of MMP-9 before and after the application of SDF in children with early childhood caries at different time intervals.

Materials and Methods

The study was conducted in the Department of Pediatric and Preventive Dentistry, ITS Dental College, Hospital & Research Centre, Greater Noida, Uttar Pradesh, India. After approval from Institutional Ethical Committee, a clinical trial was planned. A minimum total sample size of 15 at each time point, was found to be sufficient for an α of 0.05, power of 80%. Hence, fifteen children in the age-group of 3–6 years without any underlying systemic disease and no history of any long-term medication were selected for this study. The parents/guardians of the patients were explained and informed about the nature of the study and were provided with a patient information sheet along with an informed consent form or assent form wherever applicable, in English or Hindi, to read and sign upon agreement to participate.

All the procedures were carried out by a single trained investigator who was trained and had received standardization in the clinical setting with similar patients. The parent/caregiver was given the consent form before the start of the procedure and the decayed, extraction needed, filled teeth (deft) index of the patient was noted. A minimum deft score of ”2” was required for the fulfillment of the study. The patient was also subjected to clinical and radiological assessment before the procedure was carried out (Fig. 1). First, the patient was asked to pool saliva (unstimulated) in his/her mouth and then it was drawn out using a syringe (Fig. ​(Fig.2).2). After that, it was transferred to an Eppendorf tube and labeled accordingly (Fig. 3). The debris from cavity was removed using a spoon excavator and then isolated with cotton rolls. Petroleum jelly was also applied to the lips and oral mucosa so as to minimize the risk of SDF tattoos.

After proper isolation, a drop of 38% SDF was put in a dappen dish for easy access. An applicator tip was then dipped into the drop and the excess liquid was drawn out. It was then applied to the carious lesions and was held in place for 1 minute (Fig. 4). After application, the excess liquid in the lesion was drawn out using cotton rolls and dried. The patient was asked not to eat or drink for at least half an hour after the procedure. The patients were then recalled after 1, 3, and 6 months for the collection of the saliva samples (Figs 5​566​67).7). After each sample was collected, the Eppendorf tubes containing saliva were placed in a cuvette rack and stored at −20° C in the freezer. Finally, the saliva samples were analyzed for their MMP-9 levels using a human salivary enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ESSAY) kit.

Statistical Analysis

Data were analyzed using Statistical Package for the Social Sciences version 21. As the study outcome variable (MMP-9 levels) is a continuous variable, it was summarized as mean and standard deviation (SD). Graphs were prepared in Microsoft Excel.

Intragroup comparison of MMP-9 levels was done by using the repeated measures of analysis of variance (ANOVA) test. The level of statistical significance was set at 0.05.

Results

A total of 15 children participated in the study however, one patient was lost in the follow-up period due to the countrywide lockdown. The study population comprised 57.1% males and the remaining 42.9% females. Mean salivary MMP levels increased continuously from baseline (0.241) to preoperative 1 month where it was found to be 0.292. It was found to be further increased in the 3rd month to 0.330 as well as in the 6th month to a level of 0.614. This increase in salivary MMP levels was found to be statistically significant in the 6th month (Table 1 and Fig. 8). Post hoc pairwise comparison was done by the Bonferroni test. The mean salivary MMP levels at postoperative 6 months were found to be significantly higher than that at baseline, 1st, and 3rd-month follow-up. The baseline vs 6th-month levels were 0.051 and 0.373 which was found to be statistically significant (Table 2 and Fig. 9). A correlation of mean deft score with salivary MMP-9 levels was also tested for in this study using Spearman's correlation test. However, MMP levels at baseline and 6 months follow-up failed to show any significant correlation with deft score (Table 3).

Discussion

Traditional treatment of early childhood caries in young children involves drilling and filling which evokes many challenging behavioral issues and a lack of cooperation. Therefore, the majority of patients go untreated, which ultimately leads to early tooth loss.4 Primary tooth loss, especially of the upper anterior, is unsightly and might lead to phonation issues or psychological anguish for the patient. Primary teeth also play a vital role in the normal development and growth of jaws along with prompt eruption of the permanent teeth. However, the introduction of minimally invasive dentistry has changed the whole concept of dealing with carious lesions from “extension for prevention” to “prevention of extension.” As a result, less invasive nonsurgical approaches to manage dental caries have emerged thereby promoting various chemotherapeutic procedures.

In this era of atraumatic dentistry, SDF has come up as a game changer in the management of dental caries. SDF not only halts the progression of caries but also helps in remineralizing the lost tooth structure over time, making it one of the most efficient tools in modern times. Fung et al.9 have reported that the fluoride ions in SDF act predominantly on tooth structure while the silver phosphate component acts against the cariogenic bacteria inhibiting dentinal collagen degradation. In an acidic environment, it combines with the enamel's hydroxyapatite to form fluorapatite, which has been found to be more resistant to caries than hydroxyapatite. Also, Mei et al.,10 in an in vitro study concluded that dental caries are inhibited by the use of 38% SDF by slowing down the process of demineralization. It reduces mineral content loss and delays the breakdown of collagen.11 In addition, it has significant levels of silver and fluoride ions, both of which can stop the development of multispecies cariogenic biofilms.

One of the most commonly used concentrations in clinical trials of SDF solution is 38%. Gotjamanos12 used a 40% aqueous silver fluoride solution in an in vitro experiment, researchers in Australia hypothesized that such a high fluoride concentration may enable significant amounts of fluoride to enter the systemic circulation and perhaps lead to dental fluorosis in young children.6 Hence, researchers have recommended the use of a lower concentration of SDF to minimize this risk. However, a low concentration of 12% SDF was not effective in arresting dental caries. Fung et al. studied the effect of high and low concentrations of SDF and whether a 6 or 12-month application differed in the treatment outcome. The authors concluded that a concentration of 38% SDF with the biannual application is the most effective therapeutic regime for arresting dentin caries.9 Also, studies have shown that application of SDF at a concentration of 38% inhibits MMP-9 levels up to 80%. Furthermore, it has been shown to be safe for the pulp by not inciting pulpal inflammation or necrosis and promoting the formation of tertiary dentin.

The MMPs have been reported from dentin, pulp tissue, and odontoblasts, wherein they play a vital role alongside the formation of the dentin matrix, the modulation of caries progression, and the formation of secondary dentin. MMP-9 is the predominant MMP found in carious lesions and in saliva. It is of prime importance as the active form of this enzyme was detected by gelatin zymography in a study conducted by Tjäderhane et al.2 on several carious lesions. Thus, MMP-9 was chosen for the study and collected from unstimulated saliva. In dental carious lesions, both pre and active forms of MMP-9 are found thus, suggesting their role in the caries process (Sulkala et al.)13 Acidic pH of the carious lesion activates the endogenous MMPs and in turn, they have a crucial role in collagen breakdown leading to the progression of caries. Tooth surfaces treated with SDF show a large amount of intact collagen which increases dentin bond strength. Matrix metalloproteins, such as MMP-2, MMP-8, and MMP-9, which have a degradative effect on the dentin surfaces, are observed to be blocked by 38% SDF, according to Mei et al.14 Additionally, it significantly inhibits MMPs more than solutions containing 10% sodium fluoride and 42% silver nitrate, which have equal amounts of fluoride and silver ions, respectively. It works by preventing these enzymes’ proteolytic activity, which prevents the degradation of collagen. However, in the present study mean salivary MMP levels increased continuously from baseline (0.241) to 1st month (0.291), and then from 1st (0.291) to 3rd month (0.330) which were statistically insignificant. Recent studies suggest that the activation of MMP-9 has a crucial role in the destruction of dentin collagen in carious lesions. Tjäderhane et al.2 stated that MMPs, despite being active, are incapable of degrading the organic matrix of dentin at acidic pH. The pH reduction in the caries process, on the other hand, is followed by a pH neutralization because of the salivary buffer systems. As a result, a momentary spike in pH that occurs at the sites of demineralized dentin allows pH-activated MMPs to work. Furthermore, the phosphorylated proteins released during dentin matrix demineralization by bacterial acids could interact with tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases-inhibited host MMPs within the lesion and reactivate them, hence enhancing the degrading activity. The change in the dietary pattern of children like an increase in snacking during the lockdown period could have resulted in the dropping of pH mimicking the environment of a carious lesion leading to the production of bacterial acids which ultimately causes a rise in the MMP-9 levels which is in accordance with the study conducted by Tjäderhane et al.2 This could have contributed to the increase in MMP-9 levels after 1 and 3-months’ time period. The results showed that after the application of 38% SDF, there was a gradual increase in levels of MMP-9 from baseline to 3rd month (0.241–0.330) which was statistically nonsignificant, though at 6th-month follow-up the levels were significantly higher (0.614) indicating the need for reapplication of 38% SDF biannually (Fung et al.). Also, Llodra et al.,15 concluded that the monthly application of a 38% SDF solution is efficacious to control caries in primary teeth. Secondly, the mean deft scores at the start of the study were 6 and at the end of the 6th-month follow-up, the deft scores did not change that is, no new carious lesions were detected emphasizing the preventive role of SDF in caries control. This is in accordance with a systematic review by Oliveira et al. which also states that when SDF is used for arresting carious lesions in the teeth, it also exerts its anticaries action on the entire dentition.16 The MMP levels and deft scores were assessed for possible correlation and results showed that there is no significant correlation between MMP levels and deft scores at baseline and 6 months. This is in support of the study conducted by Llorda et al., which concluded that 38% SDF is effective in reducing caries in primary teeth and first permanent molars.15 However, a very limited number of studies are published on the effects of SDF on MMP-9 levels and hence, more extensive research has to be carried out in this field.

Limitations of the Study

The limitations of the study were as follows:

  • The MMP-9 levels were not evaluated immediately after application of 38% SDF which could have shown a decrease in their levels as observed in previous studies.
  • There is a scarcity of research regarding the effect of 38% SDF on salivary MMP-9 levels and hence requires more studies.
  • A longer follow-up with a larger sample size may give a better understanding of the regulation of the MMP-9 level.

Conclusion

The conclusions derived were as follows:

  • The MMP-9 levels were found to be gradually increasing postapplication of 38% SDF and were significantly higher after 6 months.
  • The highest mean difference in the MMP-9 levels was observed between baseline and 6th-month follow-up.
  • No new carious lesion appeared in the teeth during the experimental time period of 6 and the deft scores remained the same.

Clinical Significance

The present study had the following clinical significance:

  • It was observed that the MMP-9 levels were increasing postapplication of 38% SDF and it was significantly high at the 6th-month follow-up. Also, the highest mean difference in their levels was observed between the baseline and the 6th-month follow-up. Therefore, a biannual application of 38% SDF would be beneficial for children with early childhood caries.
  • A correlation was also checked for the mean deft scores with MMP-9 levels. However, at the end of the 6th-month follow-up period no significant correlation was found between the two, and no new lesions were seen in the patients.
