Teprotumumab Divergently Alters Fibrocyte Gene Expression: Implications for Thyroid-associated Ophthalmopathy

Abstract

Context

Teprotumumab, an IGF-I receptor (IGF-IR) inhibitor, is effective in thyroid-associated ophthalmopathy (TAO). The drug can modulate induction by TSH of IL-6 and IL-8 in CD34+ fibrocytes and their putative derivatives, CD34+ orbital fibroblasts (CD34+ OF). Fibrocytes express multiple thyroid autoantigens and cytokines implicated in TAO, which are downregulated by Slit2. Inflammation and disordered hyaluronan (HA) accumulation occur in TAO. Whether teprotumumab alters these processes directly in fibrocytes/CD34+ OF remains uncertain.

Objective

Determine teprotumumab effects on expression/synthesis of several TAO-relevant molecules in fibrocytes and GD-OF.

Design/Setting/Participants

Patients with TAO and healthy donors were recruited from an academic endocrine and oculoplastic practice.

Main outcome measures

Real-time PCR, specific immunoassays.

Results

Teprotumumab attenuates basal and TSH-inducible autoimmune regulator protein, thyroglobulin, sodium iodide symporter, thyroperoxidase, IL-10, and B-cell activating factor levels in fibrocytes. It downregulates IL-23p19 expression/induction while enhancing IL-12p35, intracellular and secreted IL-1 receptor antagonists, and Slit2. These effects are mirrored by linsitinib. HA production is marginally enhanced by teprotumumab, the consequence of enhanced HAS2 expression.

Conclusion

Teprotumumab affects specific gene expression in fibrocytes and GD-OF in a target-specific, nonmonolithic manner, whereas IGF-IR control of these cells appears complex. The current results suggest that the drug may act on cytokine expression and HA production systemically and locally, within the TAO orbit. These findings extend our insights into the mechanisms through which IGF-IR inhibition might elicit clinical responses in TAO, including a potential role of Slit2 in attenuating inflammation and tissue remodeling.

Keywords: Graves’ disease, orbit, fibroblasts, cytokine, ophthalmopathy, hyaluronan

Thyroid-associated ophthalmopathy (TAO), an autoimmune component of fully expressed Graves’ disease (GD), remains a vexing, disfiguring, and potentially sight-threatening disorder (1). Historically, management of TAO has remained suboptimal, resulting from an absence of safe and effective medications. The disease involves anatomic-site-selective activation of orbital and upper facial tissues, which become inflamed, congested, and remodeled. The central, disease-specific autoantigen in GD, TSH receptor (TSHR), is targeted by thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin (TSI), frequently resulting in thyroid dysfunction (2). Evidence for TSHR playing important roles in TAO rests largely on its expression in orbital connective tissue (3) and orbital fibroblasts (GD-OF) (4). GD-OF respond robustly to TSH and TSI in vitro (5-7). Further, circulating TSI levels broadly correlate with the clinical behavior of TAO (8, 9). Activity of another, more recently identified pathogenic component of TAO, the IGF-I receptor (IGF-IR), is required for many actions of TSH and TSI (10). Patients with GD and TAO appear to generate autoantibodies against IGF-IR (11, 12); however, whether these autoantibodies might directly and independently initiate signaling remains a topic of considerable debate (13). Confirmation that IGF-IR plays a critical role in TAO was provided by dramatic and unprecedented clinical responses to teprotumumab, a highly specific IGF-IR inhibitor, observed in 2 therapeutic trials (14, 15). The drug reduces proptosis and inflammation and improves diplopia in active, moderate, to severe TAO. Results from those trials led to the recent US Food and Drug Administration approval of teprotumumab for TAO (16). Importantly, the mechanism(s) underlying the effectiveness of teprotumumab has yet to be fully characterized. We have very recently reported that the monoclonal antibody alters the immune phenotype of fibrocytes, both in vitro and in vivo, suggesting that systemic actions may be involved (17).

Among hallmarks of tissue remodeling associated with TAO are elevated expression levels of specific cytokines, inflammation, and abnormal accumulation of hyaluronan (HA) in the orbit and upper face (18). This pathology results from increased activities of GD-OF, heterogeneous cells that differ markedly from orbital fibroblasts found in healthy individuals (6, 19, 20). Relatively recent insights have identified the presence of CD34+ CXCR4+ Collagen I+ TSHRbright cells among GD-OF (6, 21). These fibroblasts are currently thought to represent derivatives of bone marrow-derived CD34+ fibrocytes. Fibrocytes represent myeloid precursors of the monocyte lineage that actively participate in wound healing and scar formation (22). They synthesize several proteins, the expression of which was thought historically to be limited to the thyroid gland (23,24). TSHR/IGF-IR interactions occurring in fibrocytes and GD-OF lead to cell activation and enhanced biosynthetic phenotypes (5, 10-12). We have demonstrated recently the dramatically divergent capacity of fibrocytes and GD-OF to generate HA (25). Although GD-OF generate copious amounts of HA, fibrocytes synthesize relatively little. These differences result from a cell-type specific pattern of hyaluronan synthase (HAS) isoenzyme and UDP-glucose dehydrogenase expression (25-28).

Earlier reports from our group demonstrated that TSH-dependent IL-6 and IL-8 expression in fibrocytes is substantially downregulated by teprotumumab in vitro (29). The current studies were undertaken to explore further the effects of this drug on fibrocytes and GD-OF and whether these effects could be implicated in the therapeutic impact of teprotumumab in TAO. They were also designed to allow comparisons between the actions of teprotumumab and Slit2, a neural axon guidance glycoprotein that can attenuate the expression of several fibrocyte proteins implicated in TAO (17, 30-32). We have proposed that Slit2, by its actions mediated through roundabout 1 (ROBO1), its cognate receptor, may represent a key endogenous determent of TAO activity and severity (30). Here, we demonstrate that teprotumumab inhibits the expression and in some cases, induction by bovine TSH (bTSH) of several “thyroid-specific” proteins, including TSHR, thyroglobulin (Tg), thyroperoxidase (TPO), and sodium-iodide symporter (NIS). In addition, basal expression and bTSH induction of autoimmune regulator protein (AIRE), HAS1, B cell-activating factor (BAFF), soluble IL-1 receptor antagonist (sIL-1RA), IL-23, and IL-10 in both cell types are attenuated either partially or completely by teprotumumab. In contrast, the monoclonal antibody enhances IL-12p35, intracellular IL-1RA (icIL-1RA), and HAS2 expression. Thus, it would appear that several potentially important therapeutic responses to IGF-IR inhibition might be mediated, at least in part, through direct effects on these cells. In contrast, HA synthesis by GD-OF is not inhibited directly and may be somewhat increased by teprotumumab, suggesting that an intermediate cell type(s) may be involved in these effects in vivo.

Materials and Methods

Materials

DMEM (cat. #11965) containing 4.5 g/mL D-glucose and L-glutamine, penicillin-streptomycin mixture (cat. #15140), and fetal bovine serum (FBS) (cat. #16000-044) were purchased from Life Technologies (Grand Island, NY). Bovine TSH (cat. #T1614) was from Scripps Laboratories, San Diego, CA. Human CD34+ nucleofection kit (cat. #VPA-1003) was supplied by Lonza, Allendale, NJ. An HA ELISA kit (K-1200, RRID:AB_2916099) was purchased from Echelon Biosciences Inc. (Salt Lake City, UT). ELISAs for human BAFF (cat. #DBLYSOB, RRID:AB_2916100), IL-10 (cat. #D1000B, RRID:AB_2916101), human IL-12 (cat. #D1200, RID:AB_2916102), human IL-23 (cat. #D2300B, RRID:AB_2916103), and sIL-1RA (cat.#DRA00B, RRID:AB_2916104) were from R & D Systems, Minneapolis, MN. Slit2 (cat. #ab100637, RRID:AB_2916105) and icIL-1RA (ab211650, RRID:AB_10846099?) were from Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA. and PE mouse anti-human CD34 (cat. #555822, RRID:AB_396151) and mouse IgG1 isotype control-PE (cat. #555749, RRID:AB_396091) came from BD Biosciences, San Diego, CA. Accutase (cat. #SCR005) was from Millipore, Billerica, MA. Teprotumumab (RRID:AB_2911058) was kindly provided by River Vision and Horizon Therapeutics.

Methods

Research participant recruitment

A total of 40 individuals with stable TAO and 10 healthy control subjects were recruited from academic endocrine and oculoplastic practices at the University of Michigan Health System providing peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs). Patients undergoing surgical orbit decompressions with stable, severe TAO (n = 4) provided strains of GD-OF comprising CD34+ OF and CD34– OF, as reported previously (23). Verbal and written informed consent was obtained from all subjects before their study participation. These activities have been approved by the University of Michigan institutional review board. Additional blood samples were supplied by the American Red Cross.

Fibrocyte and GD-OF cultivation

Fibrocytes were prepared as described by Bucala et al (22) with slight modification (6) in that they are not routinely cultivated on fibronectin-coated substratum. PBMCs were isolated by Ficoll Histopaque (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) density gradient centrifugation. Six-well plates were inoculated with 107 PBMCs and covered with DMEM containing 10% FBS and penicillin/streptomycin. After 10 to 12 days in culture, adherent monolayers (<5% of the starting population) were rinsed and removed from substratum by mechanical scraping following Accutase treatment. Culture purity was confirmed routinely by FACS analysis to be >90% fibrocytes based on their consistent CD31–CD45+ CD34+ CXCR4+ Col I+ TSHRhigh phenotype.

GD-OF were cultivated from deep fat removed during surgical orbital decompression as described (20, 33). The fraction of CD34+ OF in GD-OF strains varies somewhat but usually approximates 50%. Early passages used in these studies ranged from 2 to 8, an interval during which the cells exhibited a stable phenotype (34). In those studies examining pure CD34+ and CD34– subsets, GD-OF were subjected to cytometric cell sorting using a FACSAria III (BD Biosciences) instrument. Cell monolayers were stained for 30 minutes at 4°C with fluorescein isothiocyanate-conjugated anti-mouse CD34 or its isotype control. Rinsed cells were sorted under sterile conditions into CD34+ OF and CD34– OF subsets. These were then recultured for 48 to 72 hours and treated as indicated in the figure legends without or with bTSH (5 mU/mL) for 6 hours alone or with a test compound.

RNA preparation and real-time PCR

GD-OF strains were inoculated into 6-well plates in 10% FBS-supplemented DMEM. Confluent cultures were shifted to DMEM without or with bTSH (5 mIU/mL), teprotumumab (50 µg/mL) or linsitinib (1 µM) was added as single agents or the combinations and time intervals indicated. Cellular RNA was extracted with an Aurum TM Total RNA Mini Kit (Bio-RAD, Hercules, CA, cat. #732-6820). RNA (2 µg) was reverse-transcribed and real-time PCR was performed using an Applied Biosystems instrument and QuantiTect SybrGreen PCR kits (Qiagen, cat #204143). Primer sequences synthesized by Life Technologies were as follows: GAPDH forward, 5′-TTGCCATCAATGACCCCTTCA-3′, reverse, 5′-GCCC-CACTTGATTTTGGA; HAS1, CGATACTGGGTAGCCTTCAATG-3′, reverse, 5′-GGAGGTGTACTTGGTAGCATAA CC-3; HAS2, forward, 5′- GTGTTATACATGTC-GAGTTTACTTCC -3′’ reverse, 5′- GTC- ATATTGTTGTCCCTTCTTCCGC-3′; BAFF forward, 5′-GAGAAGCTGCCAGCAGGA-3; reverse, 5′-TCCTGGAGCTGGTGGTTC-3′. Quantitect primer assays were from Qiagen (Frederick, MD). Sample values were generated against a standard curve and normalized to GAPDH signals.

IL-12, IL-23, IL-10, HA, and BAFF ELISAs. Confluent cultures were treated with nothing or teprotumumab (50 µg/mL) for 5 days, without or with bTSH (5 mU/mL) for the intervals indicated or 72 hours. Media and rinsed cell layers were homogenized in 0.1M NaCl and assayed for HA content using an ELISA kit (K-1200) from Echelon Biosciences Inc. (Salt Lake City, UT) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. A BAFF-specific ELISA, purchased from R & D Systems, was used in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions.

Statistics

Statistical significance was determined by a 2-tailed Student t test. Analyses were performed with SAS version 9.4 (Cary, NC).

Results

Effects of Teprotumumab on Thyroid Autoantigen Expression in Fibrocytes

Fibrocytes express relatively high levels of functional TSHR (6), Tg, NIS, and TPO (23, 24). Expression of those proteins is dependent on the AIRE protein, which in fibrocytes is inducible by bTSH (24). Further, Slit2, a neural axon repellent generated by CD34– OF, attenuates the expression of these proteins (30). Here, we find that teprotumumab (50 μg/mL) for 7 to 9 days inhibits the basal expression and induction by bTSH (5 mIU/mL for 6 hours) of AIRE (Fig. 1A), Tg (Fig. 1B), TPO (Fig. 1C), and NIS (Fig. 1D) in cultured fibrocytes compared with an isotype control monoclonal antibody. The extent of inhibition, both regarding basal expression of the transcripts and the induction by bTSH, varies among the targets. In some cases, the effects of teprotumumab may result from its inhibition mainly of bTSH-independent mRNA levels.

Teprotumumab Attenuates IL-23p19 Expression While Enhancing That of IL-12p35

IL-12 and IL-23 are 2 key cytokines determining immune responses, in part through the polarization and expansion of T cells to the Th1 and Th17 phenotypes, respectively (35). They are differentially expressed in fibrocytes and GD-OF, apparently the consequence of divergent actions of endogenously expressed Slit2 in the CD34– OF subset (31). In GD-OF, the down-regulatory effects of CD34– OF have been shown to be the consequence, at least in part, of Slit2. Here, we find that teprotumumab inhibits basal IL-23p19 expression and induction by bTSH in both fibrocytes (Fig. 2A) and GD-OF (Fig. 2B). In contrast, the IGF-IR inhibitor enhances IL-12p35 expression and induction by bTSH in both cell types. The effects of teprotumumab are dose-dependent (Supplemental Fig. 1) (25). Impact of linsitinib (1 µM), a small molecule IGF-IR inhibitor, is congruent with that of teprotumumab, and similarly discordant with regard to IL-23p19 and IL-12p35 in these cell types (Fig. 2C and ​and2D).2D). These findings suggest that IGF-IR may play an important role in maintaining the balance between IL-23 and IL-12 generation in both fibrocytes and GD-OF, effects that resemble those of the Slit2/ROBO1 pathway (31).

In contrast to IL-12 and IL-23, cytokines generally enhancing immune responses, IL-10 most frequently inhibits inflammation (36). IL-10 attenuates immune responses as a component of the T-cell regulatory mechanism (37). Fibrocytes express IL-10 mRNA, as demonstrated in Fig. 3A. bTSH induces IL-10 by 11-fold, whereas both teprotumumab (Fig. 3A) and Slit2 (Fig. 3B) downregulate both basal and bTSH-provoked expression. In contrast, teprotumumab (Fig. 3C) and Slit2 (Fig. 3D) upregulate basal and bTSH-dependent icIL-1RA mRNA levels in fibrocytes. The effects of teprotumumab on icIL-1RA protein levels are minimal (Fig. 3E), whereas Slit2 attenuates sIL-1RA mRNA expression in fibrocytes (Fig. 3F). Teprotumumab upregulates both icIL-1RA (Fig. 3G) and sIL-1RA (Fig. 3H) protein levels in GD-OF. Thus, the impact of teprotumumab on cytokines known to influence immune responses is not uniform. Some of its actions reduce whereas others enhance levels of modulating cytokines in fibrocytes and GD-OF.

Teprotumumab Inhibits Basal BAFF Expression and Induction by bTSH

BAFF and its pathway exert an important influence on B-cell survival (38, 39). Since its discovery, BAFF has been the focus of developmental therapeutic targeting of autoreactive B cells. Because autoantibody generation appears integral to GD and TAO, we determined whether fibrocytes and GD-OF generate BAFF. Constitutive BAFF mRNA levels are manifold higher in fibrocytes than in GD-OF (Fig. 4A). The transcript is time-dependently induced by bTSH in both cell types, peaking at 12 hours and then declining. By 96 hours, levels in fibrocytes have returned to baseline, whereas those in GD-OF remain elevated. BAFF protein levels are similarly induced in fibrocytes, peaking at 24 hours and returning to baseline levels by 72 hours (Fig. 4B). A comparison of the basal and bTSH-dependent expression of BAFF mRNA (treated for 12 hours), in multiple strains of fibrocytes and GD-OF demonstrates a consistent pattern, although absolute levels of the transcript vary among the strains (Fig. 4C). Teprotumumab partially attenuates bTSH induction of BAFF in fibrocytes (Fig. 4D), compared with a more complete inhibition by Slit2 (Fig. 4E). To determine whether the relatively low BAFF expression levels in GD-OF compared with fibrocytes were the consequence of factors from CD34– OF, parental strains of GD-OF containing a mixture of CD34+ OF and CD34– OF were subjected to cytometric cell-sorting. As the data in Fig. 4F indicate, both basal and bTSH-induced (12-hour treatment) BAFF expression in the pure CD34– OF subset are considerably higher than that in either parental cultures or pure CD34+  OF.

Teprotumumab Enhances HA Synthesis in GD-OF and Fibrocytes

A major component of tissue remodeling in TAO is HA accumulation in the orbit and upper face (40). Among the clinical responses to teprotumumab in TAO is the dramatic reduction in proptosis accompanied by reductions in orbital connective tissue volumes (14, 15). Because this increased volume can be attributed, at least in part, to disordered HA accumulation, effects of teprotumumab on HA generation were assessed in fibrocytes and GD-OF. Consistent with our earlier observations, HA synthesis was substantially greater in GD-OF than in fibrocytes (25). Surprisingly, teprotumumab enhances the effects of bTSH on HA synthesis in GD-OF over 96 hours, the duration of the study (Fig. 5A). The synergism between bTSH and teprotumumab was also apparent in fibrocytes (Fig. 5B). Teprotumumab inhibits both basal and bTSH-provoked expression of HAS1 in fibrocytes and GD-OF (Fig. 5C and ​and5D,5D, respectively). In contrast, teprotumumab induces HAS2 expression, either as a single agent or in combination with bTSH in both cell types. The effects of teprotumumab on HAS1 and HAS2 expression are nearly identical to those of linsitinib (1 µM) (Fig. 5E and ​and5F).5F). We have previously demonstrated that HA production in fibrocytes and GD-OF is primarily the consequence of HAS2 expression and activity (25, 26). Thus, teprotumumab exerts a small, direct, stimulatory effect on HA production in both fibrocytes and GD-OF through modest HAS2 induction. These effects are similar to those of linsitinib. It would appear that IGF-IR inhibition per se can increase in HAS2 levels and HA production in these cells. These findings therefore suggest that any inhibition of HA accumulation occurring in vivo by teprotumumab may be mediated through indirect actions, perhaps involving other cell types.

Could Effects of Teprotumumab Be Mediated Through the Slit2/ROBO1 Pathway?

Slit2 exerts a powerful regulatory influence on fibrocyte differentiation (41) and phenotype (25, 30, 31). Moreover, these effects are mediated through its cognate membrane receptor, ROBO1. Because many of the effects exhibited by teprotumumab on fibrocytes resemble those of Slit2, we examined whether the monoclonal antibody might be influencing Slit2 and ROBO1 expression levels in GD-OF. Teprotumumab induces Slit2 protein synthesis in these cells (Fig. 6A). Further, it induces expression of both Slit2 mRNA and ROBO1 mRNA in 3 strains of GD-OF, each from a different donor (Fig. 6B).

Discussion

Results from the current studies indicate that teprotumumab exerts several actions in vitro in fibrocytes and GD-OF. These findings may have revealed, at least in part, potentially important therapeutic mechanisms underlying the drug’s clinical effectiveness in TAO. Several responses to TSHR activation, potentially related to disease pathogenesis, are attenuated by teprotumumab and another IGF-IR inhibitor, linsitinib. Further, the IGF-IR inhibitors also reduce basal (bTSH independent) expression of several proteins in fibrocytes and GD-OF. We previously found that teprotumumab reduces TSHR levels, but the effects are partial and transient (29). Thus, in some cases, the effects of TSHR-initiated signaling are smaller in magnitude (ie, incomplete) than on others, potentially revealing the complex characteristics of the IGF-IR/TSHR signaling to specific gene targets. Of note subcellular IGF-IR distribution is markedly altered in lung fibroblasts from TSHR-/- mice (42), demonstrating the reciprocal dependence of IGF-IR and TSHR pathways. We recently reported that teprotumumab and Slit2 downregulate expression of MHC II and multiple B7 costimulatory molecules in circulating fibrocytes, in vivo and in vitro (17). Here, we find that teprotumumab attenuates basal expression of thyroid autoantigens including Tg, TPO, and NIS. In addition, the noncanonical transcription factor, AIRE, is also downregulated in these cells (Fig.1). In contrast, effects of teprotumumab on induction by bTSH of these proteins are variable. The monoclonal antibody exerted small effects on TPO and NIS inductions while those of AIRE and Tg were strongly blunted. As suggested previously, TSHR signaling may comprise 2 components exhibiting IGF-IR dependence or independence (43). Thus, susceptibility of specific bTSH-inducible proteins to therapeutic IGF-IR inhibition may differ, and therefore determine the patterns of clinical response. We have recently opined on the complex interplay between IGF-IR and TSHR that might underpin the diversity of IGF-IR-inhibitory effects on TSHR-initiated signaling (44).

It is possible that the autoantigenic burden within the TAO orbit and in other tissues may be lessened by teprotumumab. Expression of these ectopically expressed thyroid proteins in circulating fibrocytes is under the positive control of AIRE (23, 24). In contrast, lower levels of these antigens can be detected in CD34+ OF (30). Their divergent levels result, at least in part, from repression of gene activity in CD34+ OF exerted by Slit2, emanating from residential CD34– OF in situ in the TAO orbit. Thus, the impact of teprotumumab on fibrocyte and GD-OF phenotypes shares similarities with that of Slit2 (30). Further, it is possible that the drug may be eliciting its clinical effects through reducing AIRE expression, perhaps within the thymus.

Besides its inhibition of AIRE and “thyroid-specific” proteins, teprotumumab also attenuates the expression and in some cases, the induction by bTSH of IL-23p19, IL-10, and BAFF in fibrocytes while enhancing that of IL-12p35, sIL-1RA, and icIL-1RA. The effects on IL-23 and IL-12 mirror those of Slit2 (31). The inhibitory impact on BAFF is notable in light of recent evidence that serum BAFF levels and those of BAFF receptor 3 are elevated in GD (45). Thus, in multiple regards, teprotumumab effects resemble those of Slit2 (30, 31). The therapeutic monoclonal antibody’s effects on HA synthesis mirror those of Slit2. This upregulation is mediated through increased HAS2 expression, the dominant isoenzyme expressed in GD-OF (25, 26). At the same time, both Slit2 and teprotumumab downregulate HAS1 expression. From these findings, any impact of teprotumumab in reducing HA synthesis in vivo in TAO may be mediated indirectly, through actions in another cell-type(s), such as T cells. Earlier studies from our laboratory group have identified a potential for bidirectional interplay between T cells and fibrocytes, mediated through an extensive array of cytokines (18, 29). It remains uncertain what effects, if any, teprotumumab might have on leukorgulin, CD40 ligand, IL-1β, and TNF-α synthesis or actions, cytokines known to drive HA synthesis in GD-OF (46-48). Clearly, additional studies will be required to identify the mechanisms involved in the apparent clinical reduction by teprotumumab of HA-dependent water content within orbital tissues (15). It remains possible that teprotumumab is directly affecting cytokine synthesis in T and B cells, in addition to its inhibition of IL-17a and interferon γ, which is mediated indirectly through fibrocytes (17).

Phenotypic differences between PBMC-derived CD34+ fibrocytes and CD34+ OF from TAO orbital tissue result in part from the actions of Slit2, generated primarily by CD34– OF (30, 31). Slit2 can downregulate specific genes while upregulating others in CD34+ OF. Given the capacity for teprotumumab to induce both Slit2 and ROBO1 expression (Fig. 6), we now question whether at least some clinical responses attributed to teprotumumab might be mediated through intermediate induction of Slit2/ROBO1. We find the close alignment between teprotumumab and Slit2 effects on IL-23p19/IL-12p35 balance particularly interesting. Those findings suggest that IGF-IR-mediated signaling may influence T-cell polarization and expansion, particularly because Th1 and Th17 cytokines appear relevant to GD and TAO (49, 50). The enhancing effects of teprotumumab on basal and TSH-provoked sIL-1RA and icIL-1RA suggest the potential for the drug to modulate consequences of inflammasome activation, a pathway we have previously implicated in TAO (51-53). A recent study compared gene expression profiles in GD-OF and healthy orbital fibroblasts (54). That report failed to include studies in purified CD34+ OF and CD34– OF, an approach that might have revealed additional divergence between gene expression repertoires of fibroblast subsets coming from healthy and TAO tissues.

Our current findings reveal additional, potentially important effects underlying responses to teprotumumab in TAO. It is possible that these may be mediated through Slit2, sIL-1RA, and icIL-1RA generation in GD-OF. Our studies have not included examination of other tissues and cell types but these are planned, conducted in vitro and in vivo. Despite these limitations, our results disclose potentially relevant insights into other diseases where fibrocytes and IGF-IR appear involved, such as rheumatoid arthritis (55).
