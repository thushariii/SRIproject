Mycophenolate mofetil inhibits Merkel cell carcinoma growth

The direct antitumour effects of mTOR inhibitors against cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) have prompted a paradigm shift towards using sirolimus for allograft rejection prophylaxis in patients with high-risk SCC who have had a solid organ transplant (SOT). Patients who have had an SOT are at higher risk for Merkel cell carcinoma (MCC), yet there is little evidence concerning potential antitumour effects of immunosuppressive drugs against MCC. Screening seven immunosuppressive drugs in six MCC cell lines revealed that mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) had strong antitumour activity (surpassing mTOR inhibitors) and higher potency in MCC vs. other cancer types. MMF also inhibited MCC tumour growth in mice. Our preclinical findings strongly suggest the utility of MMF in patients with MCC who require immunosuppression.

Dear Editor, Merkel cell carcinoma (MCC) is a rare and aggressive neuroendocrine skin cancer driven by Merkel cell polyomavirus (MCPyV) in virus-positive MCC (VP-MCC) and ultraviolet radiation-induced mutations in virus-negative MCC (VN-MCC).1 Advanced MCC of both types are treated with immunotherapy, reflecting the importance of immune surveillance in controlling MCC. Consistent with this, the incidence of MCC is higher in immunocompromised populations,1 particularly in patients who have had a solid organ transplant (SOT) and are receiving immunosuppressive drugs.2 Patients who have had an SOT also have lower survival rates than immunocompetent patients with MCC.3,4

All patients who have had an SOT have an increased risk of MCC; however, those taking azathioprine (AZA), ciclosporin A (CsA) or mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) inhibitors have higher incidence rates of MCC than those on mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) or tacrolimus.2 This disparity in the risk of MCC may reflect differences in the immunosuppressive properties of these drug regimens. However, agents like MMF and mTOR inhibitors may also possess direct antitumour activity that could affect MCC risk.5,6

Because immunosuppression affects both MCC risk and outcomes, modifying immunosuppressive medications is often considered when MCC is diagnosed in patients who have had an SOT. The general goal is to minimize the amount of immunosuppression; however, there is limited evidence to guide the selection of what immunosuppressive drug to choose. Here, we assessed the effect of immunosuppressive medications on the growth of MCC cell lines and xenograft tumours, as selecting drugs with antitumour activity may be advisable.

We treated VP-MCC (WaGa, MKL-1, MKL-2) and VN-MCC (MCC13, MCC26, UISO) cell lines with seven immunosuppressive compounds, each at 11 escalating concentrations: MMF, AZA, temsirolimus, everolimus, CsA, methylprednisolone and cyclophosphamide. After 72 h, we assessed cell viability using CellTiter-Glo® (Promega, Madison, WI, USA), an assay that quantifies living cells based on ATP production. To evaluate the effect of each drug on MCC viability, we calculated the area under the dose–response curve (AUC). The AUC for a drug decreases as the potency and efficacy of its cytotoxic effect increase. We compared the mean AUC for each drug across the six MCC cell lines with the average AUC of 10 nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), which had no effect on MCC viability (100% AUC). Of the immunosuppressive drugs tested, MMF showed the most antitumour activity (70.4% AUC, P < 0.001) in MCC. The mTOR inhibitors everolimus (82.8% AUC, P = 0.018) and temsirolimus (84.7% AUC, P = 0.039) also showed moderate antitumour activity (Figure ​(Figure1a1a).

Having identified MMF as the immunosuppressive drug with the largest impact on MCC viability, we compared the average potency of MMF in MCC cell lines with its potency in other cancer types reported in the publicly accessible PRISM secondary repurposing dataset.7 Using average IC50 values calculated from dose–response curves, we found that MMF was more potent in MCC cell lines than in any other cancer type with more than two cell lines in the database, suggesting that MCC is particularly sensitive to MMF’s antitumour effects (Figure ​(Figure1b1b).

To assess the impact of MMF on MCC growth in vivo, we generated athymic nude mice with MCC13 xenograft tumours and treated them with oral MMF at a dosage of 120 mg kg–1 (n = 10) or a placebo (n = 10) twice daily, 5 days a week for a duration of 4 weeks (Figure ​(Figure1c1c).8 MMF treatment significantly slowed MCC tumour growth (P = 0.019; Figure ​Figure1d1d).

Taken together, we found that MMF substantially reduced the viability of MCC cells in culture and inhibited MCC tumour growth in a preclinical mouse model. Although we did not specifically investigate the mechanism of MMF in MCC, previous research attributes its antitumour effects to cell cycle arrest, apoptosis induction in cancer cells and reduced angiogenesis in xenografts.4 MCC in patients who require immunosuppression is too rare to permit a prospective randomized clinical trial testing different immunosuppressive regimens. Similarly, retrospective data on MCC outcomes in patients on different immunosuppressive drugs are too sparse to allow definitive conclusions to be drawn. In the absence of clinical data, our preclinical studies highlight the potential of MMF in managing patients with MCC who require immunosuppression. As MCC is immunogenic, it is crucial to also consider minimizing the amount of immunosuppression when selecting MMF for graft rejection prophylaxis in patients who have had an SOT and are diagnosed with MCC.
