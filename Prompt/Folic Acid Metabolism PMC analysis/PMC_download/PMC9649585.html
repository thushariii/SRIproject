Abstract

Malaria has been a source of concern for humans for millennia; therefore in the present study we have utilized in-silico approach to generate diverse anti-malarial hit. Towards this, Molinspiration cheminformatics and Biovia Discovery Studio (DS) 2020 were used to conduct molecular modelling studies on 120 designed compounds. Furthermore, the TOPKAT module was used to evaluate the toxicity of the screened compounds. The CDOCKER docking technology was used to investigate protein–ligand docking against the Pf-DHFR-TS protein (PDB ID: 1J3I and 1J3K). These compounds were synthesized using a conventional and microwave-assisted nucleophilic substitution reaction, and they were characterized using a variety of physicochemical and spectroscopic methods. Among the ten compounds tested, Df3 had the highest antimalarial activity against the chloroquine-resistant (Dd2) strain, with an IC50 value of 9.54 μg mL−1 and further demonstrate, molecular dynamics (MD) simulation studies and estimation of MM-PBSA-based free binding energies of docked complexes with 1J3I and 1J3K were carried out. The discovery of a novel class of Pf-DHFR inhibitors can be accomplished using this hybrid scaffold.

Introduction

Malaria has been a source of concern for humans for millennia. The WHO has predicted 14 million more malaria cases and 47,000 more fatalities, and over the previous 20 years, the illness claimed more young lives (WMR 2020). There were 241 million malaria cases worldwide in 2020, up from 227 million in 2019, with 85 countries with endemic malaria, primarily in the WHO’s African region. Malaria mortality in the WHO Eastern Mediterranean region increased by 49% between 2016 and 2020, with Plasmodium falciparum (Pf) accounting for 80% of infections (WMR 2021).

The majority of malaria cases in Africa and Southeast Asia are caused by Pf, the most virulent of the protozoal species that cause human malaria. Resistance to currently used antimalarial medications, such as pyrimethamine, halofantrine, mefloquine, and quinine, has hampered the decade-long effort to curb the menace of the disease. Therefore, it is necessary to generate armamentarium of novel antimalarial agents endowed with unique chemical structure and mode of action (Nzila et al. 2005; Yuthavong et al. 2012).

A purposeful approach to selective chemotherapy can often be seen in studies of the metabolic and biochemical distinctions between parasite and host cells. It has been found advantageous to target specific metabolic processes necessary for parasite growth, to prevent damage to host cell. Studies have shown that malarial parasite synthesizes folate factor from scratch and that para-amino benzoic acid (PABA) is crucial in the parasite's folate metabolic pathway using PABA dependent auxotrophic mutants (Krátký et al. 2019). The amino acid PABA, which is frequently utilized in biochemistry and medical science, has a wide range of commercial applications. It is now understood that the majority of human illnesses have it as a key component (Lödige and Hiersch 2015).

1,3,5-Triazine is another pharmacophore, and its derivatives have generated interest for their biological activity against bacteria (Kashyap et al. 2018), cancer (Dansena et al. 2015), mycobacteria (Dubey et al. 2012), protozoa (Gahtori et al. 2012), fungi (Singh et al. 2012), and viruses (Singh et al. 2012; Sakakibara et al. 2015). The antimalarial activity of 1,3,5-triazine has been discovered to be highly impacted by a number of bioactive pharmacophores, including hybrid PABA-1,3,5-triazine (Adhikari et al. 2020), 4-aminoquinoline 1,3,5-triazine (Bhat et al. 2013a, b), thiazole-1,3,5-triazine (Singh et al. 2013), pyrazole-1,3,5-triazine (Gogoi et al. 2021), and pyrimidine-1,3,5-triazine derivatives (Choudhury et al. 2022).

A combinatorial library of 120 compounds was generated in this study, which included the two-pharmacophores 4-aminobenzoyl-l-glutamic acid and 1,3,5-triazine. Following that, ten compounds were selected using computational screening techniques such as molecular property analysis, toxicity assessment, and molecular docking. These screened compounds were synthesized using a microwave synthesizer. The compounds were tested for antimalarial activity in vitro against 3D7 (chloroquine-sensitive) and Dd2 (chloroquine-resistant) strains of P. falciparum as Pf-DHFR inhibitor.

MD simulation

Molecular dynamics simulation study was performed to understand the physical movements of atoms and molecules (Mahanta et al. 2020). As a part of the study, RMSD, RMSF, and ROG were calculated within the simulation period of 50 ns (50,000 ps) and compared with the control WRA609 to observe the stability of the complexes with both the protein 1J3I and 1J3K. The RMSD plots for the compound were analyzed and showed that 1J3I-WRA609 and 1J3I-Df3 had almost similar deviations within the simulation period. The fluctuations of the individual amino acid residues within the simulation period were plotted in RMSF. It was found that the residues for the 1J3I-WRA609 and 1J3I-Df3 had almost similar pattern with minimum deviations from each other. The ROG diagram indicated that the 1J3I-WRA609 and 1J3I-Df3 complexes were formed with almost equal compactness.

For the protein–ligand complex 1J3K-WRA609 and 1J3K-Df3, some deviations were observed which suggests that the presence of ligand influenced the stability of the protein 1J3K and changed its dynamic behavior. From RMSF analysis, it was found that 1J3K-Df3 showed minor fluctuations in interaction for some amino acid residue. These findings were also supported by calculated ROG for the 1J3K-Df3 and 1J3K-WRA609 complexes, but the differences between their deviations were found non-significant (Fig. ​(Fig.22).

MMPBSA-based binding free energy

After completing the MD simulation, the MM-PBSA based binding free energies were calculated for all the generated conformations, and the average binding free energy was then calculated for each protein–ligand complex (Fig. 4). The binding free energy of the test compound 1J3I-Df3 (− 128.13 kcal/mol) was found considerably lower than that of the co-crystallized ligand 1J3I-WRA609 (− 69.95 kcal/mol). This indicated that the test compound Df3 formed thermodynamically stable complex with the target protein 1J3I. Likewise, the binding free energy of the test compound 1J3K-Df3 (− 124.97 kcal/mol) was found considerably lower than that of the co-crystallized ligand 1J3K-WRA609 (− 74.09 kcal/mol) and hence the test compound Df3 formed thermodynamically stable complex with the target protein 1J3K.

Synthesis

The complete synthetic scheme is reported in Scheme ​Scheme1.1. According to the scheme, mono-substitution (B) of 2,4,6-trichloro-1,3,5-triazine (A) with X was achieved at 0–5 °C for 3 h. The 2-chloro-4,6-disubstituted 1,3,5-triazine derivatives (C) were synthesized by the reaction of (B) with different primary/secondary amines (Y) under microwave temperature of 27–31 °C in 1,4-Dioxane. The tri-substituted 1,3,5-triazine derivatives (D) with N-(4-aminobenzoyl)-l-glutamic acid were achieved in 15 min under microwave temperature 90 °C. The obtained products were recrystallized with suitable solvents and dried properly.

In vitro antimalarial screening

The antimalarial activity of all synthetic compounds against P. falciparum strains 3D7 (chloroquine-sensitive) and Dd2 (Chloroquine-resistant) is shown in Table ​Table5.5. The IC50 values for the compounds Df28, Df38, Df24, and Df22, ranged from 14.40 to 41.67 μg mL−1 against 3D7 (chloroquine-sensitive) of strain of P. falciparum along with resistance index (RI) 0.27 to 3.69 and compounds Df3, Df22, De38, and Df40 showed IC50 range from 9.54 to 23.24 μg mL−1 against Dd2 (chloroquine-resistant) strain of P. falciparum with resistance index (RI) 0.16 to 0.51. Among the ten synthesized compounds, compound Df3 with heterocyclic such as piperidine substitution demonstrated superior efficacy against chloroquine resistant strain (Dd2) with IC50 9.54 μg mL−1 and moderate antimalarial activity against 3D7 strains with IC50 57.85 μg mL−1 along with resistance index (RI) 0.16 low as comparable to chloroquine 1.71. Replacement of heterocyclic amine analogue piperidine with other heterocyclic amine analogue, such as piperazine in compound Df22, showed good antimalarial activity in Dd2 strain and moderate in 3D7 strain with IC50 11.55 and 41.67 μg mL−1. On replacing heterocyclic amine analogue such as piperazine with aliphatic amine such as diethylamine in compound De38 showed insignificant antimalarial activity in 3D7 strain with IC50 93.88 μg mL−1 but highest antimalarial activity in Dd2 strain with IC50 15.94 μg mL−1 along with resistance index (RI) 0.16 low than standard drug chloroquine. On replacing aliphatic analogue such as diethylamine with heterocyclic analogous as ethylpiperazine in compound Df24 showed good antimalarial activity in 3D7 strain with IC50 22.96 μg mL−1 but insignificant antimalarial activity in Dd2 strain with IC50 36.16 μg mL−1 along with resistance index (RI) 1.57 low than standard drug chloroquine. Compounds contain heterocyclic amine analogue, such as morpholine substituted derivative Df28 showed promising antimalarial activity against 3D7 strain with IC50 14.40 and moderate antimalarial activity Dd2 strain with IC50 43.22 μg mL−1. Furthermore, diethylamine substituted derivatives Df38 showed mild antimalarial activity against 3D7 strain of P. falciparum with IC50 21.35 μg mL−1 and negligible antimalarial activity against Dd2 strain with IC50 78.79 μg mL−1. Replacement of aliphatic diethylamine with dimethylamine in compounds Df40 showed negligible antimalarial activity against 3D7 strain of P. falciparum with IC50 44.82 μg mL−1 and moderate antimalarial activity against Dd2 strain with IC50 23.24 μg mL−1.

Structure–activity relationship study

The antimalarial activity result was used to generate a structure–activity relationship (SAR), as shown in Fig. 5. Compounds Df3 and Df28 with N-containing hetero alicyclic substitution such as morpholine and piperidine showed the highest antimalarial activity in chloroquine resistant Dd2 and chloroquine sensitive (3D7) strains of P. falciparum. Antimalarial activity is increased by substituting N-containing aliphatic groups, which is supported by previous research published by our group and other researchers (Bhat et al. 2013a, b). Compounds Df22 and Df24 containing a hetero alicyclic linkage with a 1,3,5-triazine ring, such as piperazine and ethylpiperazine, demonstrated good antimalarial activity against the Dd2 strain of P. falciparum and moderate antimalarial activity against the (3D7) strain of P. falciparum. Furthermore, substituting chain aliphatic group for a hetero alicyclic analogue in compounds Df38, Df40, De30, and De37 such as diethylamine, dimethylamine, tert-butylamine, and propylamine showed reduced antimalarial activity against both the strains. Furthermore, increasing the chain length of the substitution linkage with the 1,3,5-triazine ring reduces activity against both 3D7 and Dd2 strains due to steric hindrance of aliphatic chain analogous that may interfere with the active site of the receptor with ligand (Alven and Aderibigbe 2019).

Designed chemical library

A 1,3,5-triazine scaffold was employed in this study as the fundamental structural foundation for creating novel 1,3,5-triazine derivatives via molecular modification. Their antimalarial effectiveness may be significantly influenced by the presence N-(4-aminobenzoyl)-l-glutamic acid scaffold. The primary/ secondary amines with aliphatic chains or aromatic rings that make up the functional groups were set for the chemical library. A total of three series were created from all of the designed 120 compounds: (Dd1 to Dd40, De1 to De40 and Df1 to Df40). The substitutions in 1,3,5-triazine conjugated with N-(4-aminobenzoyl)-l-glutamic acid scaffold are tabulated in Table ​Table11.

Computational screening

In order to conduct computational screening and molecular modelling studies, Molinspiration Cheminformatics, and Biovia Discovery Studio (DS) 2020 was adopted. The Biovia Discovery Studio (DS) 2020 was from Dassault Systèmes BIOVIA, San Diego, USA on a Dell Precision workstation T3400 with an Intel Core 2 Duo processor, 4 GB of RAM, a 250 GB hard drive, and NVidia Quodro FX 4500 graphics card.

Molecular property

Molecular properties of the designed compounds such as partition co-efficient, total polar surface area, number of atoms, molecular weight, numbers of hydrogen bond acceptors and donors, and numbers of rotatable bonds were calculated with the help of Molinspiration cheminformatics software. Molinspiration Cheminformatics states that molecules that violate more than one of these rules may have bioavailability problems (http://www.molinspiration.com). The molecular weight, partition coefficient/MiLogP, and total polar surface area suggest good intestinal absorption, membrane permeability, and oral bioavailability, respectively. The remaining parameters, number of atoms, numbers of hydrogen bond acceptors and donors, and numbers of rotatable bonds, help to generate well-defined drug–receptor interactions for the best possible pharmacological action (Dahlgren and Lennernäs 2019).

Toxicity assessment

The TOPKAT component of the DS 2020 program was used to predict in silico toxicity on rodent models. To examine that, the following toxicity parameters were checked: Ames mutagenicity, mouse female carcinogenicity, mouse male carcinogenicity, and skin irritancy.

Molecular docking

According to the DS software’s standard approach, ligands and proteins were prepared before docking. Utilizing a smart minimizer built on the CHARMm (Chemistry at Harvard Macromolecular Mechanics) platform, three-dimensional structures of compounds (ligands) were produced and their energy was minimized.

From RCSB Protein Data Bank (http://www.rcsb.org/), the protein’s X-ray crystal structures (PDB ID: 1J3I and 1J3K) were obtained. The method made use of Chain A of both the proteins 1J3I and 1J3K. The standard process was followed to clean the protein molecules as needed, removed water molecules, and minimized energy. The CHARMm-based smart minimizer method with a maximum step size of 200 and an RMSD gradient of 0.1 kcal/mol was used to minimize the energy (Jain 2008).

By identifying the important amino acid residues, a receptor grid was created around the binding cavity (active sites) of the energy-minimized protein molecule. The amino acid residues were defined for the purpose of predicting the binding locations using the co-crystal ligand (active inhibitor) of the relevant protein molecule. The binding site sphere of the receptor grid box was created with a radius of 8.96 and x, y, and z dimensions of 28.741985, 5.926629, and 60.148934 for 1J3I. Again, the same was created with a radius of 8.98 and x, y, and z dimensions of 29.567696, 6.016929, and 60.120070 for 1J3K.

A simulation-based docking protocol, namely CDOCKER of DS 2020, was used for the docking. The CDOCKER is used to dock substances into the receptor molecule’s active site(s) with better accuracy. The estimated dock poses were ranked. By examining interactions between receptor–ligand complexes, the binding energies of docked ligands were predicted. The 2D diagram of receptor–ligand complexes was also used to examine various nonbonding interactions (hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic, etc.). To assess the binding mode of the test compounds, the co-crystal ligand’s docking energy was employed as reference ligand in each case.

Molecular dynamics simulation study

For the Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation investigation utilizing DS 2020, the compound Df3 with promising in vitro antimalarial activity against chloroquine-resistant (Dd2) strain was taken into consideration. The original X-ray crystal structure of the target proteins (1J3I and 1J3K) complexed with the co-crystal inhibitors was submitted to the MD simulation study together with the protein–ligand complexes produced by the flexible docking analysis. The macromolecule tool of DS 2020 was initially used to clean and prepare the protein–ligand complexes (Lin et al. 2011). In a water-filled cubic box, the complexes were solvated at a distance of 5 Å from the boundary. In the solvation procedure, the system was neutralized by the addition of 0.15 M NaCl. The solvated systems underwent energy minimization (5000 steps conjugate gradient with energy RMSD gradient 0.01 kcal/mol and 5000 steps steepest descent), heating (20 ps), and equilibration (500 ps) using the “Standard Dynamic Cascade” procedure of DS 2020. Analyses were replicated for 30 ns at 300 K in order to evaluate the convergence of the results following an MD simulation. The particle mesh ewald (PME) approach was employed for the electrostatics calculations. The SHAKE algorithm was applied with a 2-fs time step to limit hydrogen-containing bonds. To assess receptor–ligand conformation changes and their stability, root-mean-square deviation (RMSD), root mean square fluctuation (RMSF), and radius of gyration (ROG) were computed using the starting structure as a reference for the complete protein–ligand complex. The “Analyze Trajectory” protocol was used to calculate the RMSD, RMSF, and ROG values. The distance of the various hydrogen bonds that formed during the simulation was also examined. Finally, the average interaction of the receptor-ligand complexes was determined (Noha et al. 2017; Lai et al. 2011).

MM-PBSA based binding free energy calculation

One of the crucial parameters to calculate the binding affinity of a molecule to a target is the binding free energy (∆G), which was calculated using the MM-PBSA method (Genheden and Ryde 2015). Additionally, it offers quick and precise prediction of absolute binding affinity of a molecule within the active binding region of the target protein. This is crucial for the particular potency in terms of activation or inhibition, as well as its thermodynamic stability (Rastelli et al. 2010). The ‘Binding Free Energy-Single Trajectory’ protocol of DS v 2020 along with the MM-PBSA method was used in this investigation to compute the binding free energies for each protein–ligand combination. Finally, the average binding free energy (∆G) for each protein–ligand complex was calculated from the binding free energies of all the produced conformations.

General requirements

The synthesis used no extra purification of the commercial precursors and solvents that were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Merck KGaA), TCI chemicals (India) Pvt. Ltd., and Spectrochem Pvt. Ltd. Spectroscopic measurements

On Bruker Avance Neo 500 MHz NMR, the 1H and 13C spectra were recorded. With a UV/Visible Spectrophotometer (Lab India analytical: UV 3200), the maximum wavelength (λmax) for compounds was recorded. The LC–MS study was carried out using a Waters Q-TOF Micromass spectrometer (ESI–MS). The spectra have been displayed in the supplementary materials.

The general synthesis for monosubstituted 1,3,5-triazine derivatives

Utilizing the nucleophilic substitution mechanism shown in Scheme ​Scheme1,1, the intermediates and the final compounds were synthesized.

2,4,6-trichloro-1,3,5-triazine (0.02 mol) was stirred for 0.5 h in acetone (40 mL). Respective amine (0.02 mol) was gradually added to that stirred solution using a dropping funnel at a constant ice bath temperature of 0–5 °C. Reaction was carried out for 3 h. After completion of the reaction, the resulting mixture was filtered, dried, and recrystallized with suitable solvent.

Synthesis for disubstituted 1,3,5-triazine derivatives

A solution of first intermediate (0.002 mol), 5 mL 1,4-dioxane, 0.5 mL triethylamine and the appropriate amine (0.004 mol) was agitated together for 10 min at 27 to 31 °C under microwave irradiation at a pressure of 1 bar and power of 20 W. In a petridish, the reaction mixture was poured and allowed to dry. The residue was washed with distilled water before being recrystallized with the appropriate solvents to produce intermediates. TLC was used to monitor the reactions, with Methanol, Ethylacetate, and Chloroform (1:1:2) serving as the mobile phase.

Synthesis for title compounds

N-(4-aminobenzoyl)-l-glutamic acid (0.001 mol) was added to the solution of corresponding disubstituted 1,3,5-triazine derivatives intermediate, in 5 mL N,N-Dimethylformamide. In the presence of 0.5 mL triethylamine, the reaction mixture was agitated at 90 °C for 15 min in microwave-irradiated closed vessel at a pressure 5 bar and power 50 W. The reaction mixture was then dried in a hot air oven and recrystallized. TLC was used to monitor the reactions, using Methanol, Ethylacetate, and Chloroform as the mobile phase (1:1:2). The melting points of compounds were recorded.

In vitro antimalarial activity

Giemsa-stained slide technique was used to conduct in vitro antimalarial screening of the synthesized compounds against 3D7 (chloroquine-sensitive) and Dd2 (chloroquine-resistant) P. falciparum strains, respectively.

Preparation of parasite

The cryopreserved parasite strains of P. falciparum were brought from liquid nitrogen or (− 80 °C) to room temperature in order to thaw the 3D7 and Dd2 strains. 1.6% and 12% NaCl solutions were added progressively in 1-ml increments, and the mixture was centrifuged at 2000 rpm for 5 min. After discarding the supernatant, required fresh medium was added to wash the lysed parasitized RBCs away two to three times. The strains were then routinely maintained as continuous cultures in the malariology division of the ICMR-RMRC-North East Region, Dibrugarh, using a slightly modified version of Trager and Jensen's technique (Trager and Jensen 1978; Trager and Jensen 1997). The parasites were kept in RPMI-1640 medium with 25 mM HEPES, 0.2% d-glucose, 0.1% gentamycin sulfate, 0.1% amphotericin B, 0.5% Albumax-II, 0.01% hypoxanthine, and 1.77 mM NaHCO3 at a pH of 7.2 to maintain a hematocrit of 5%. The culture flask was incubated at 37 °C and 5% CO2 level in a CO2 incubator with regular parasitemia evaluation and medium change. The red blood cells used were O + . An incomplete medium preparation of the culture medium (without sodium bicarbonate) was made weekly and kept at 4 °C. Before using in a culture, sodium bicarbonate was freshly added after being warmed to 37 °C (Radfar et al. 2009).

In vitro antimalarial efficacy test

The synthetic compounds were subjected to screening against the 3D7 and Dd2 strains of P. falciparum, with modified micro assay procedures of (Rieckmann et al. 1978). A stock solution of 5000 µg/mL of each of the test samples was prepared in DMSO (1:200). By further dilution of the stock with incomplete media, two different doses of 5 and 50 µg/mL were utilized for preliminary screening (without NaHCO3). A total volume of 200 µL of medium RPMI-1640 is evenly maintained in a 96-well microtiter plate to conduct the experiment, with an initial ring-stage parasitemia of 1% at 3% hematocrit. Negative control wells were created by adding just complete medium and 0.5% DMSO. In duplicate wells, the test compound was introduced in 20 µL volume at the necessary concentrations (5 and 50 µg/mL). As a positive control, the reference chloroquine was used concurrently at a dosage that corresponded to its predefined IC50 (Basco et al. 1994; Bhat et al. 2013a, b). After that, the test plate was incubated in 5% CO2 and 37 °C. The blood smears from each well were prepared after 36–40 h of incubation, fixed with methanol, and stained with 10% Giemsa stain for microscopic observation. A total of 100 asexual parasites were counted, and the percentage of dead rings, trophozoites, and schizonts was determined based on the morphology of the parasites (Fig. 6). GraphPad Prism software was used to calculate IC50 values at different lower dosages.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the hybrid title N-(4-aminobenzoyl)-l-glutamic acid substituted 1,3,5-triazine derivatives were synthesized in good yield by facile and microwave synthesizer. Docking results indicated that compound Df3 revealed higher binding interaction with amino acids Ser108, Ser167, Thr107 (1J3I), and Arg122 (1J3K) and MD simulation study suggested that these compounds bound to the target proteins with comparatively better binding affinity than the co-crystallized ligand WRA609.

Furthermore, the antimalarial activity results revealed that compound Df3 had the highest activity against Dd2 (chloroquine-resistant) strain of P. falciparum. These compounds could lead to the discovery of a new class of Pf-DHFR inhibitors for antimalarial drug development.
