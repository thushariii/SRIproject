Abstract

Previous studies suggest that the reduction of SMAD3 (mothers against decapentaplegic homolog 3) has a great impact on tumor development, but its exact pathological function remains unclear. In this study, we found that the protein level of SMAD3 was greatly reduced in human-grade IV glioblastoma tissues, in which LAMP2A (lysosome-associated membrane protein type 2A) was significantly up-regulated. LAMP2A is a key rate-limiting protein of chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA), a lysosome pathway of protein degradation that is activated in glioma. We carefully analyzed the amino-acid sequence of SMAD3 and found that it contained a pentapeptide motif biochemically related to KFERQ, which has been proposed to be a targeting sequence for CMA. In vitro, we confirmed that SMAD3 was degraded in either serum-free or KFERQ motif deleted condition, which was regulated by LAMP2A and interacted with HSC70 (heat shock cognate 71 kDa protein). Using isolated lysosomes, amino-acid residues 75 and 128 of SMAD3 were found to be of importance for this process, which affected the CMA pathway in which SMAD3 was involved. Similarly, down-regulating SMAD3 or up-regulating LAMP2A in cultured glioma cells enhanced their proliferation and invasion. Taken together, these results suggest that excessive activation of CMA regulates glioma cell growth by promoting the degradation of SMAD3. Therefore, targeting the SMAD3–LAMP2A-mediated CMA-lysosome pathway may be a promising approach in anti-cancer therapy.

Introduction

Glioma, especially its most malignant form glioblastoma multiforme (GBM), is a highly vascularized brain tumor that displays rapid and invasive growth [1, 2]. According to the pathological classification of tumors of the central nervous system by the World Health Organization, gliomas are divided into four grades (I–IV) [3, 4]. These tumors cause significant mortality and morbidity. Even if surgery, concurrent chemoradiotherapy, and adjuvant chemotherapy are applied, the median survival of GBM patients is still only ~14.6 months, and the 5-year relative survival is ~5% [5]. Also, none of the traditional treatments is able to significantly prolong the survival of these patients [6]. So far, the pathogenesis of glioma remains unclear. Therefore, clarifying the pathological mechanisms and looking for potential effective therapeutic targets against glioma are important for clinical practice.

The SMAD (mothers against decapentaplegic) family is divided into three categories: receptor-regulated SMAD (R-SMAD, including SMAD1, SMAD2, SMAD3, SMAD5, and SMAD8), common-partner SMAD (Co-SMAD, also known as SMAD4), and inhibitory SMAD (I-SMAD, including SMAD6 and SMAD7) [7]. SMAD3 belongs to R-SMAD, which is an intracellular signal transducer and transcriptional modulator, comprising two functional domains: the MAD-homology 1 (MH1) domain which contains the DNA binding region, and the MH2 domain which bears a binding site for the cytoplasmic anchor SARA (SMAD anchor for receptor activation). In the cytoplasm, SMAD3 anchors to the TGF-β (transforming growth factor-beta) type I receptor at the membrane via recruitment and binding to SARA. When TGF-β activates the TGF-β type II receptor on the membrane, the TGF-β type I receptor and SMAD3 are phosphorylated. Then, the conformation of the MH2 domain of SMAD3 is changed by phosphorylation, which causes SMAD3 to dissociate from SARA and connect with SMAD4, forming the SMAD3/SMAD4 complex [8]. Afterwards, this complex binds to the TREs (Tas responsive elements) in the promoter region that is regulated by TGF-β, initiating or repressing gene transcription alone or in complex with other nuclear factors, which plays an important regulatory role in tumor pathogenesis [9]. There are three isoforms of TGF-β—β1, β2, and β3—that are differentially expressed in gliomas [10]. The mRNA levels of TGF-β1 and TGF-β2 in anemic astrocytoma and GBM are slightly increased, while the mRNA level of SMAD3 is somewhat decreased [11, 12]. The regression analysis has shown that as the malignant tumor increases, the expression of SMAD3 in the tumor tissue gradually decreases [13]. These studies suggest that the reduction of SMAD3 may have a great impact on tumor development, but its exact pathological function remains unclear.

Autophagy is a highly-conserved mechanism of self-digestion that removes damaged organelles and proteins from cells. Depending on how the cargo is delivered to the lysosome, three basic types of autophagy can be distinguished: macro-autophagy, chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA), and micro-autophagy [14]. CMA refers to autophagy selectively degrading client proteins bearing a KFERQ-like motif. These proteins are specifically recognized by HSC70 (heat shock cognate 71 kDa protein; also known as HSPA8) in a chaperone complex composed of several co-chaperones. After captured by HSC70, the CMA substrate proteins are transported to the lysosome surface and subsequently enter into the lysosomal lumen with the assistance of LAMP2A polymers for degradation [15, 16]. Thus, LAMP2A is regarded as the vital limiter of CMA, and it is one of the best readouts to determine whether protein cargo degradation occurs via CMA. Under physiological conditions, CMA maintains basic biological activity in cells and resists adverse conditions like nutrient deficiency, toxic component exposure, and oxidative stress. However, increasing evidence has pointed out an important role of CMA activation in the pathogenesis of different kinds of cancers including breast, gastric, lung, and hepatocellular tumors [17–25]. However, some studies showed that autophagy inhibits cancer development at the same time [24, 26, 27]. These contradictory results need to be further elucidated.

Abnormal CMA activity has been closely associated with tumor pathogenesis and progression [17]. However, the specific molecular mechanism on glioma pathology is still elusive. Our data showed that CMA activity was up-regulated in glioma tissue, while SMAD3 was notably down-regulated, especially in GBM, suggesting that SMAD3 may be a potential substrate of CMA. We speculated that SMAD3 may mediate the development of malignant tumors, especially glioma, by CMA.

Materials and Methods

Cell Lines and Culture Conditions

Human cervical cancer cells (HeLa) and human glioblastoma astrocytoma lines (U251) were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 100 U/mL penicillin/streptomycin (cat #: 15070063, Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, USA). Cells were maintained at 37°C in a 5% CO2 and 95% air incubator. To remove the serum, cells in a 60%–70% confluent condition were washed 3 times with Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline (PBS; Thermo Scientific™, Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and incubated in serum-free medium for the indicated times.

Antibodies and Chemical Compounds

The following antibodies were used: anti-LAMP2A (cat #: ab125068, Abcam, London, UK), anti-SMAD3 (cat #: 9523, Cell Signaling Technology, Danvers, USA), anti-SMAD3 (cat #: ab40854, Abcam), anti-SMAD3 Chip Grade (cat #: ab28379, Abcam), anti-HSC70 (cat #: ab51052, Abcam), anti-LAMP1 (lysosome-associated membrane glycoprotein 1) (cat #: ab25630, Abcam), anti-LAMP1 (cat #: ab24170, Abcam), anti-SQSTM1 (sequestosome-1) (cat #: 8025, Cell Signaling Technology), Di-methyl-histone H3 (cat #: 9728S, Cell Signaling Technology), anti-Flag (cat #: 20543-1-AP, Proteintech, Wuhan, China), anti-β-actin (cat #: AC037, ABclonal, Wuhan, China), anti-GST (glutathione S-transferases) (cat #: AE001, ABclonal), and normal rabbit IgG (cat #: 2729, Cell Signaling Technology). HRP (horseradish peroxidase)-labeled goat anti-mouse IgG (cat #: AS003) and goat anti-rabbit IgG (cat #: AS014) were from ABclonal. Alexa Fluor 488-adsorbed goat anti-rabbit IgG (cat #: A11034) and Alexa Fluor 647-adsorbed goat anti-mouse IgG (cat #: A21236) were from Invitrogen (New York, USA). The chemicals used were 3-methyladenine (3-MA) (cat #: S2767, Selleck, Houston, USA), BF (bafilomycin A1, cat #: S1413, Selleck), MG132 (cat #: S2619, Selleck), HCQ (hydroxychloroquine sulfate, cat #: S4430, Selleck), epoxomicin (cat #: S7038, Selleck), and DMSO (dimethyl sulfoxide, cat #: 219605580, MP Biomedicals). To remove serum, cells at ~60% confluence were washed twice with PBS (cat #: 14025076, Gibco Life Technologies, Grand Island, USA) and then cultured in serum-free DMEM for the indicated times.

Plasmids, siRNAs (Small-Interfering RNAs), and shRNA (Short-Hairpin RNA)

The Myc-Flag-tagged human LAMP2A construct was kindly provided by Dr. Zixu Mao (Departments of Pharmacology and Neurology, School of Medicine, Emory University). The Flag-tagged human plasmid, containing full-length human SMAD3 cDNA (NM-005902.4) was constructed by using its mRNA sequence and the pcDNA3.1(+) vector developed by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). Cells were also transfected using HiPerFect® Transfection Reagent (Qiagen, Frankfurt, Germany)-mediated transfer with siRNA pre-designed for human SMAD3 mRNA and LAMP2A mRNA according to the manufacturer’s Quick-Start protocol.

The relevant siRNA-targeting LAMP2A and SMAD3 mRNA were provided by Sangon Biotech. The sequences were as follows:

  • Human siLAMP2A sense: 5′-GCAGUGCAGAUGACGACAATT-3′;
  • Human siLAMP2A antisense: 5′-UUGUCGUCAUCUGCACUGCTT-3′;
  • Human siSMAD3 sense: 5′-GGAUGCAACCUGAAGAUCUTT-3′;
  • Human siSMAD3 antisense: 5′-AGAUCUUCAGGUUGCAUCCTT-3′.

Knockdown and overexpressing human-derived lentiviruses were purchased from Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). Both of the human LAMP2A- and SMAD3-overexpressing lentivirus were built on previous plasmids. Accordingly, the knockdown lentivirus sequences were designed as follows:

shLAMP2A: CCGGGTACGCTATGAAACTACAAATCTCGAGATTTGTAGTTTCATAGCGTACTTTTTTG;

shSMAD3: CCGGGAGCCTGGTCAAGAAACTCAACTCGAGTTGAGTTTCTTGACCAGGCTCTTTTTTG.

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

The wild-type (WT) SMAD3 plasmid described above was used as a template in the mutagenesis reaction using the QuickMutation™ Site-Directed Mutagenesis Kit (cat #: D0206, Beyotime, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. LQ/AA, QR/AA, and QW/AA were the three predicted mutation points in the SMAD3 gene sequence based on the classical KFERQ-like motif of CMA substrates. The mutagenic oligonucleotide primers were designed as follows:

  • 1-F: 5′-CTTCCGATGGGACACCGCCGCCCGGCCATCCAGGGAC-3;
  • 1-R: 5′-GTCCCTGGATGGCCGGGCGGCGGTGTCCCATCGGAAG-3′;
  • 2-F: 5′-GTAGAACTGGTGTCTCTACTGCCGCGTAGTGTAGGGATTCACGC-3′;
  • 2-R: 5′-GCGTGAATCCCTACCACTACGCGGCAGTAGAGACACCAGTTCTAC-3′;
  • 3-F: 5′-ATCTGGGTGAGGACCGCGTCAAGCCACGCCAAAGGCCCATTCAG-3′;
  • 3-R: 5′-CTGAATGGGCCTTTGGCGTGGCTTGACGCGGTCCTCACCCAGAT-3′.

According to the reaction system of site-specific mutation, various reagents were added and the parameters of the PCR instrument were set. The reaction products were directly transformed or stored at −20°C after being digested by DpnI (cat #: ER1701, Thermo Scientific™, Shanghai, China).

Co-immunoprecipitation

The cells were washed three times with pre-cooled PBS, and 1 mL PBS was added. The cells were quickly scraped into a centrifuge tube on ice, and centrifuged at a low speed for 5 min to remove the supernatant. After that, the cells were re-suspended with 1 mL lysate for 4 h [in mmol/L: 150 NaCl, 1 EGTA, 1 EDTA, 2 DTT (DL-dithiothreitol), 100 PMSF (phenylmethanesulfonyl fluoride), 50 Tris-HCl, and 1% Triton X-100, pH 7.5], containing protease inhibitor and phosphatase inhibitor and kept in a 4°C refrigerator for 4 h. Then the supernatant was retained after centrifugation at maximum speed. The primary antibody was added and incubated at 4°C overnight. Then A/G-plus agarose (cat #: sc-2003, Shanghai, China) beads were washed three times with lysate in advance and then incubated with the primary antibody mixture for 4 h at 4°C. After that, the beads were washed three times with cold lysate, heated at 95°C for 10 min, and the sample was centrifuged at low speed for subsequent immunoblot experiments.

Western Blotting

Brain tumor tissue samples were collected and washed three times with pre-cooled PBS to remove the remaining blood, and then segmented into appropriately-sized chunks on ice. As for cell samples, the treated cells were taken from a 37 °C culture incubator and placed on ice. After removing the medium, they were washed twice with pre-cooled PBS. Cells or tumor tissues were cleaved on ice for 30 min with ice-cold RIPA (radio-immunoprecipitation assay) lysis buffer (cat #: P0013C, Beyotime, Shanghai, China) mixed with protease inhibitors (cat #: 539134, Millipore, Shanghai, China) and phosphatase inhibitors (cat #: 524625, Millipore) in advance. Then, they were ultrasonicated on ice once or twice, then centrifuged at a maximum speed at 4°C for 15 min. The supernatant was retained, the precipitate was discarded, and the extracted protein concentration was determined by the BCA protein assay kit (cat #: 2322, Thermo Fisher Scientific). On polyacrylamide gels, equal amounts of protein samples were separated by electrophoresis, and transferred to PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride) membrane. The membrane was sealed with 0.5% skim milk for 2 h, then incubated with the primary antibody at 4°C overnight. The membrane was incubated with the secondary antibody at room temperature for 2 h, washed three times with TBST (Tris-HCl, phosphate saline buffer Tween) on a shaker, illuminated with ECL kits (cat #: 34094, Thermo Fisher Scientific), and finally, the results were analyzed with ImageJ software (National Institutes of Health, USA) for grayscale values.

Lysosome Isolation

Lysosomes were isolated by centrifugation of particles in a discontinuous density gradient of sucrose as previously described [28]. Lysosome enrichment kits (cat #: 89839, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and OptiPrep solution were used in this process. Briefly, male Sprague–Dawley rats purchased from the Experimental Animal Center of Air Force Medical University (200–250 g) were rapidly decapitated without anesthesia after fasting for two days, and their livers were removed and homogenized in extraction buffer (in mol/L: 0.25 sucrose, 1 EDTA, 20 HEPES, pH 7.4). The supernatant was centrifuged at 3000×g to remove the heavy mitochondrial fraction, and the lysosomes were finally isolated from a light mitochondrial–lysosomal fraction by centrifugation (Eppendorf centrifuge, 5415R, Hamburg, Germany) at 20,000×g on ice, and tested for LAMP1 levels by immunoblotting. Rough lysosomes were prepared and purified by ultracentrifugation at 14,000×g for 2 h. In general, active lysosomes enriched by HSC70 were in the first layer. Lysosomal integrity was assessed using Neutral Red (cat #: N7005, Sigma, St. Louis, USA).

Binding and Uptake Assays

HeLa cells overexpressing WT Flag-SMAD3 and corresponding site-directed mutagenesis were lysed for purification. GST-HSC70 was also purified in this process. The specific assay was implemented as described previously [16, 29]. Briefly, the purified Flag-SMAD3 or its corresponding mutants were incubated with freshly enriched HSC70 lysosomes, with or without the protease inhibitor cocktail (cat #: HY-K0010-1, MedChemExpress, Shanghai, China) in advance and purified GST-HSC70 in a reaction buffer (10 mmol/L 3-[N-morpholino] propane sulfonic acid (cat #: M1254, Sigma), 0.3 mol/L sucrose, pH 7.4) for 20 min at 37°C. After that incubation, all the samples were centrifuged at 16,000×g and washed with the reaction buffer. The final products were re-suspended in 1× loading buffer and denatured by heating for immunoblotting analysis.

Immunofluorescence and Confocal Microscopy

HeLa cells transfected with LAMP2A plasmids, or U251 cells treated with chemicals for the indicated times were washed three times with pre-cooled PBS, then fixed with 5% paraformaldehyde for 15 min. Then 0.5% Triton X-100 (PBS dissolution preparation) was used to permeate at room temperature for 20 min. PBS was dried with absorbent paper, and 1% bovine serum albumin was added to the slides and left at room temperature for 30 min. Each cell slide was covered with diluted primary antibodies and incubated overnight at 4°C in a wet box. On the second day, the diluted fluorescent secondary antibody was added in the absence of light, and incubated in the wet box for 2 h at room temperature. Under the same conditions, DAPI was added and left for 10 min to stain nuclei. After the use of mounting medium, images were captured with confocal microscopy (TCS SP8 STED 3X, Leica, Hamburg, Germany), and measured for statistical analysis as previously reported using ImageJ software.

Immunohistochemistry

The human glioma tissue samples for paraffin sectioning were kindly donated by the Tumor Center of Zhongshan Hospital. These samples were obtained in accordance with the Ethics Committee of the Tumor Center of Zhongshan Hospital. The animal experiments were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Guangzhou Medical University. The human glioma tissue chips were purchased from Shaanxi Kexin Biotechnology (Cat #: Bra-kx208b, Shaanxi, China). To be specific, paraffin sections or tissue chips were dewaxed and hydrated. After that, antigen retrieval was carried out with sodium citrate buffer solution (pH 6.0) for a short time in a microwave oven. Besides, 3% methanol hydrogen peroxide solution was dripped onto the slices in darkness at room temperature for 20 min to block endogenous peroxidase, which was incubated with the primary antibody, human LAMP2A (cat #: ab125068, Abcam), for 1 h at room temperature, followed by 30 min incubation with the secondary antibody (cat #: KIT530, Fuzhou Maixin Biotechnology, Fuzhou, China). The sections were developed in a 3,3′-diaminobenzidine solution under microscopic observation and counterstained with hematoxylin (Core Biological Technology, Shaanxi, China). Negative control slides, in which the primary antibodies were omitted, were included in all assays.

Cell Proliferation Assay, Migration, and Invasion Assays

Cell proliferation was assessed using a cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8; cat #: HY-KO301-12000T, MCE), according to the manufacturer's protocol. After lentivirus infection, cells (1 × 103) were seeded in 96-well plates, incubated for 24 h at 37°C, and then the absorbance was measured at 450 nm at 0, 24, 48, 72, and 96 h. Cell migration was evaluated using the scratch-wound assay. Cells were cultured for 2 days to form a tight monolayer and then serum-starved for 6 h. After starvation, the monolayer was wounded using a 10-µL plastic pipette tip. The remaining cells were washed twice with a culture medium to remove cell debris and incubated at 37°C with normal serum-containing culture medium. Migrating cells at the wound front were photographed at the indicated times, using an inverted microscope (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany). The percentage of the cleared area at each time-point compared with time zero was measured using Image-Pro Plus software v6.2 (Media Cybernetics, Baltimore, USA). Filters coated with Matrigel (BD Biosciences, New York, USA) in the upper compartment were loaded with 100 µL medium containing 1 × 105 U251 cells and the lower compartment was filled with conditioned culture medium mixed with DMEM and supplemented with 10% FBS. After 36 h, migrated cells on the bottom surface were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, counted after Crystal violet staining, and photographed using an inverted microscope.

Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed with SPSS software (version 16.0. Chicago, USA), and data were presented as the mean ± SEM from at least three independent experiments. Statistical differences between groups were compared using Student’s t-test or one-way ANOVA. P <0.05 was considered statistically significant. Graphs were generated by GraphPad Prism 6 software (La Jolla, USA).

Results

CMA and SMAD3 are Involved in the Pathology of Glioma

It has been reported that silencing SMAD3 accelerates the sensitization of malignant glioma death in radiotherapy [30, 31]. In order to explore the SMAD3-specific mechanism, we collected glioma samples from patients at different disease stages for Western blotting analysis. The results showed that, compared to the normal brain tissue and low-grade glioma tissue groups, the SMAD3 protein level was markedly lower in high-grade glioblastoma tissue (P <0.001; Fig. ​Fig.1A).1A). At the same time, the level of LAMP2A, a vital rate-limiting molecule in the CMA-lysosome pathway, skyrocketed in high-grade glioma, especially in grade IV glioma tissue (n = 24, 8 patients per group; P <0.001; Fig. ​Fig.1B).1B). As normal brain tissue samples were difficult to obtain, para-tumor samples were used as controls. LAMP2A expression was also higher in high-grade glioma tissue than in para-tumor tissue and grade II glioma tissue by immunohistochemical staining in glioma tissue samples and glioma tissue microchips (Fig. ​(Fig.1C).1C). Further, in case of paratumoral tissue contamination with tumor components, the above phenomenon was further verified in glioma tissue microarrays, including normal brain tissue. Furthermore, CCK-8 assays showed that LAMP2A overexpression promoted U251 cell viability, especially at 48 h, when compared to the vector group (positive control, PC, P <0.001; Fig. ​Fig.1D).1D). Conversely, the cell viability was significantly reduced 48 h after LAMP2A was knocked down (P <0.01, Fig. ​Fig.1E),1E), indicating that the level of LAMP2A does affect the growth of glioma cells. Undoubtedly, LAMP2A is important for the CMA-lysosomal pathway, which led us to speculate that SMAD3 and CMA are involved in glioma pathology, and there may be an interaction.

SMAD3 is Involved in the Lysosomal Degradation Pathway

To confirm whether SMAD3 is degraded via the lysosomal pathway, we cultured HeLa cells in serum-free conditions, which is well-known to enhance lysosomal activity [15, 32]. Western blotting results demonstrated that under serum-free conditions, SMAD3 protein levels began to decrease at 24 h, compared to the normal condition. This effect was greater at 36, 42, and 48 h, indicating that activation of the lysosomal pathway promotes the clearance of SMAD3 (Fig. ​(Fig.22A).

SMAD3, which exists in the cytoplasm and nucleus, is an important transcription factor involved in a variety of cellular functions [33, 34]. Meanwhile, the CMA-lysosome pathway mainly degrades the cytoplasmic part of its substrates [14]. Compared to normal culture conditions, cytoplasmic SMAD3 was noticeably reduced after serum-free treatment for 24 and 48 h, but the level of SMAD3 in the nucleus was intact at 24 h, and slightly decreased at 48 h (Fig. ​(Fig.2B),2B), indicating that the decrease of SMAD3 after serum-free treatment mainly results from the reduction of its cytoplasmic part, similar to the CMA substrate protein degradation pattern. In addition, we inhibited the activity of lysosomal hydrolase by combining ammonium chloride (NH4Cl) and leupeptin under the condition of serum deprivation. The decrease of cytoplasmic SMAD3 was inhibited by the combined treatment in a dose-dependent manner (Figs. ​(Figs.2C,2C, S1A, and S1B). LAMP1 and H3 served as the cytoplasmic and nuclear markers, respectively. Furthermore, immunofluorescent co-localization of LAMP1, a lysosome marker, and SMAD3 revealed that under serum deprivation, SMAD3 remained in the cytoplasm and co-localized with lysosomes following the combined treatment of NH4Cl plus leupeptin, when compared with the untreated groups (Figs. ​(Figs.2D2D and S1C). Taken together, these findings indicated that SMAD3 can be degraded by the lysosomal pathway.

In order to clarify whether SMAD3 is degraded by macro-autophagy, we applied 3-MA, a specific and effective macro-autophagy inhibitor, to HeLa cells under normal culture conditions (Fig. ​(Fig.2E).2E). Compared to the control group, SMAD3 did not notably change at the protein level after 3-MA administration, whereas SQSTM1, a substrate of macro-autophagy, skyrocketed. Then, we administered 3-MA to the cells under serum-free conditions. Also, 3-MA failed to alter the SMAD3 protein level (Fig. ​(Fig.2F).2F). These results indicated that SMAD3 degradation is independent of macro-autophagy.

It has been reported that SMAD3 is degraded by the proteasome pathway [35, 36]. We applied epoxomicin, a proteasome inhibitor, to HeLa cells under serum-free conditions. When the cells were treated with epoxomicin for 6 h, the level of SMAD3 started to recover and reached a peak at 12 h versus 0 h (Fig. ​(Fig.2G,2G, n = 3, P <0.001), indicating that proteasomes contribute to the degradation of SMAD3, consistent with previous studies. However, it was exciting to find that when we treated the cells with either lysosome inhibitors (HCQ and BF), or a proteasome inhibitor MG132, the degradation of SMAD3 was partially blocked (Fig. ​(Fig.2H).2H). Taken together, these results suggested that SMAD3 is degraded by both the lysosome and proteasome pathways.

The Degradation of SMAD3 is Dependent on the CMA-Lysosome Pathway

It is well known that ~30% of soluble proteins that bear a five-pentapeptide motif biochemically called “KFERQ” are recognized and brought to the surface of the lysosome by HSC70 [14, 15]. Then, these specific substrates bind to the membrane receptor named LAMP2A and are eventually engulfed into the lysosomal cavity. HSC70 and LAMP2A are key rate-limiting factors in CMA activity. When LAMP2A was overexpressed in HeLa and U251 cells to activate CMA by plasmid overexpression, the SMAD3 protein level gradually decreased in a time-dependent manner; it was most evident at 36 and 48 h, when compared to the vector (PC) group (Figs. ​(Figs.3A,3A, S2A, and S2B). When inhibiting the activity of lysosomes in both cell lines by using NH4Cl and leupeptin, the decrease of SMAD3 induced by LAMP2A overexpression was significantly blocked in comparison with the vector (PC) group (Figs. ​(Figs.3B,3B, S2C, and S2D). In addition, immunofluorescence staining showed that the co-localization of LAMP1 and SMAD3 was significantly increased in the LAMP2A-overexpression group after NH4Cl plus leupeptin treatment or in the combined inhibitor treatment group versus the overexpression group alone (Figs. ​(Figs.3C3C and S2E). On the contrary, silencing LAMP2A by the siRNA method significantly up-regulated the level of SMAD3 in both tumor cell lines at different time points when compared to the control group (Figs. ​(Figs.3D,3D, ​D,E,E, and S2F). These results suggested that LAMP2A mediates SMAD3 degradation in lysosomes.

We further considered that HSC70 may act as the chaperone that guides SMAD3 to contact LAMP2A at the surface of lysosomes. Therefore, we applied co-immunoprecipitation of SMAD3 and HSC70 to HeLa cells, through overexpressing Flag-SMAD3 and pcDNA3.1 under normal culture conditions. The results showed that both the endogenous SMAD3 and exogenous Flag-SMAD3 bound to HSC70 (Fig. ​(Fig.3F,3F, ​F,G).G). When overexpressing LAMP2A to enhance CMA activity in the cells, the amount of SMAD3 that bound to HSC70 was significantly reduced (Fig. ​(Fig.3H),3H), indicating that CMA activation decreases the interaction between SMAD3 and HSC70 in HeLa cells. Briefly, the above results strongly suggested that SMAD3 is a direct substrate of CMA and can be regulated by the CMA-lysosome pathway.

SMAD3 Degradation Depends on KFERQ-like Motifs in the CMA-lysosome Pathway

It has been demonstrated that the CMA substrates bear a five-pentapeptide motif referred to as the “KFERQ” motif, which can bind to HSC70 [15, 32]. Thus, we analyzed the sequence of SMAD3 and found that it contains four KFERQ-like motifs (DGRLQ, QRVET, QWLDK, and KVLTQ). Subsequently, we constructed four mutant SMAD3 plasmids according to these predicted sites; they were mutated at the 75th (M75, DGRLQ), 128th (M128, QRVET), 405th (M405, QWLDK), and 412th (M412, KVLTQ) amino-acid residues. Under serum-deprived conditions, when cells were transfected with the WT, M75, or M128 SMAD3 mutants, the SMAD3 level was decreased less in the M57 and M128 groups than in the WT group (P <0.001; Fig. ​Fig.4A).4A). Furthermore, the decrease of SMAD3 in cells transfected with M75 and M128 SMAD3 mutants could not be reversed by NH4Cl plus leupeptin treatment under serum deprivation, whereas the reduction of SMAD3 in the WT group was significantly rescued (P <0.01; Fig. ​Fig.4B).4B). These results suggested that the M75 and M128 mutations are capable of resisting SMAD3 degradation in tumor cells with serum-free treatment. Finally, the co-immunoprecipitation analysis showed that SMAD3 was hardly bound to HSC70 in the M75 or M128 mutation group when compared to the WT group in HeLa cells (Fig. ​(Fig.4C),4C), indicating that the binding is blocked by the M75 and M128 mutations. In addition, we explored the change of the M405 and M412 SMAD3 mutants under serum deprivation. The results showed that the extent of SMAD3 in cells expressing the M405 or M412 SMAD3 mutants was the same as that in the WT group, suggesting that SMAD3 M405 and M412 mutation cannot resist the degradation induced by serum deprivation.

Moreover, the lysosome binding and uptake experiments were performed in vitro, which is the classical experiment to detect the change of CMA substrates entering lysosomes [15, 32]. The liver tissue collected from Sprague–Dawley rats was ground and ultra-centrifuged to stratify lysosomes and their binding proteins. The results showed that the first pellet (P1) contained the purest lysosomes, distinguished by different markers, including immunoglobulin heavy chain-binding protein (BIP), an endoplasmic reticulum marker, and voltage-dependent anion channel 1 (VDAC), a mitochondrial marker (Fig. ​(Fig.4D).4D). Therefore, the lysosomes in P1 were used for the subsequent experiments. Flag-SMAD3 (WT) and mutant SMAD3 plasmids (M75, M128, M405, and M412) were purified by co-precipitation and then incubated with P1 lysosomes. The result showed that Flag-SMAD3 (WT) was taken into lysosomes for degradation, and not only bound to lysosomes but was also ingested by them, while the M75 mutant was not (Fig. ​(Fig.4E).4E). Further experiments showed that the binding of M128 and M75 mutations was notably weaker than the WT and other mutations (Fig. ​(Fig.4F).4F). From the above, the “KFERQ” motifs, especially amino-acid residues 75 and 128 in SMAD3 are important for its degradation through the CMA-lysosome pathway (Fig. ​(Fig.4E,4E, ​E,FF).

Down-regulating SMAD3 Through CMA Enhances the Proliferation and Invasion of Glioma Cells

Based on the above phenomena and mechanisms, we demonstrated that SMAD3 is degraded by CMA. We constructed a series of cell lines, stably expressed by knock-down negative control (NC), LAMP2A-knockdown (shLAMP2A), SMAD3-knockdown (shSMAD3), SMAD3-LAMP2A-knockdown (shSMAD3 + shLAMP2A), overexpression of positive control (PC), pcDNA3.1, LAMP2A, SMAD3, and LAMP2A + SMAD3 lentivirus in U251 cells. CCK-8 assays were applied to assess the viability of these cell lines. Compared to the NC cells, both the shSMAD3 and shSMAD3 + shLAMP2A groups showed a significant increase in viability (each P <0.05), whereas the viability of the shLAMP2A group was decreased (P <0.05) (Fig. ​(Fig.5A).5A). On the contrary, the number of cells in the tumor invasion assay was significantly increased in the LAMP2A overexpression group and reversed by overexpression of SMAD3 when compared to overexpression of the PC group (each P <0.05) (Fig. ​(Fig.5B,5B, ​B,C).C). These results suggested that, in terms of glioma invasion and proliferation, LAMP2A has a regulatory effect on SMAD3. Sequentially, we used a wound-healing assay to access the effects of SMAD3 on glioma cell migration. The shSMAD3 + shLAMP2A group showed a significant narrowing of the injury at 24 and 48 h, whereas shLAMP2A did not show significant narrowing until 48 h (Fig. ​(Fig.5D,5D, ​D,E),E), which further supported the conclusion that SMAD3 knock-down and SMAD3 + LAMP2A knock-down promote tumor migration.

Discussion

Both CMA and SMAD3 have been linked to cancer progression in recent years, but their relationship in the pathology of cancer is still not thoroughly clarified, especially in gliomas [13, 37, 38]. We found that SMAD3 is a newly-discovered substrate of CMA, and revealed the specific mechanisms of its degradation by the CMA-lysosome pathway, as well as its effects on the proliferation, migration, and invasion of glioma cells. It is well known that the TGF-β pathway has dichotomous effects on tumorigenesis. On one hand, it can inhibit tumor growth; on the other hand, it can also facilitate oncogenic development [39]. SMAD3, a member of the SMAD protein superfamily, plays a core role in launching such dichotomous effects in the TGF-β pathway [40]. The SMAD3 MH1 domain at the N-terminus is a DNA-binding area containing a couple of serine phosphorylated sites, and the MH2 domain at the C-terminus is a transcriptional factor interaction area containing serval threonine phosphorylated sites [8–10]. They are linked by a linker region which has a few serine/threonine residues that can be phosphorylated by proteins with kinase activity. In the canonical TGF-β pathway, SMAD3 is phosphorylated at the serine sites at its C-terminus by TGF-β receptor I, then forms a complex with SMAD2 and SMAD4, sequentially translocates into the nucleus, regulates target-gene transcription, and finally suppresses tumor growth [41]. Meanwhile, in the non-canonical TGF-β pathway, SMAD3 is phosphorylated by molecules such as CDK4/2, JNK, p38, and ERK1/2 at the phosphorylation sites in the linker region and switches to inhibit the tumor growth-suppression action of the canonical TGF-β pathway, consequently facilitating the proliferation, migration, and invasion of tumor cells [42, 43].

Here, we found that the level of SMAD3 was notably decreased in human glioma tissue, especially in high-grade gliomas. The SMAD3 level is also dramatically reduced in human hepatocellular carcinoma [37, 38]. Furthermore, our results indicated that SMAD3 knockdown enhanced the proliferation and invasion of glioma cells, suggesting that the canonical TGF-β/SMAD3 pathway is mainly involved in the oncogenesis and progress of glioma. Non-coding RNA-mediated reduction of SMAD3 mRNA [44], mutation of phosphorylation sites at the C-terminus of SMAD3 [45, 46], as well as the acceleration of SMAD3 degradation [43, 47] might contribute to the reduction of SMAD3 in glioma.

Therefore, maintaining a high level of SMAD3 in the glioma by up-regulating the expression of SMAD3 or inhibiting its degradation is a promising strategy in glioma treatment. It is well known that SMAD3 is degraded in proteasomes. Here, we discovered that SMAD3 degradation is also involved in the CMA-lysosome pathway. We demonstrated that SMAD3 was recognized by the molecular chaperone HSC70 in tumor cells, and interacted with LAMP2A, the CMA receptor, on the lysosomal membrane. Thereby, SMAD3 entered the lysosome lumen for degradation. The KFERQ motifs containing amino-acid residues 75 and 128 of SMAD3 are vital for SMAD3 degradation in this pathway. Our findings deepen the understanding of the SMAD3 degradation pathway in glioma and provide a new potential target for its treatment.

Our results showed that SMAD3 plus LAMP2A knockdown had more notable effects on facilitating the proliferation, migration, and invasion of glioma cells when compared to SMAD3 single knockdown, although LAMP2A single knockdown also inhibited tumor cell development. We propose a possible explanation might be that with SMAD3-knockdown, further knocking down LAMP2A activates other pathways, like the ubiquitination protease pathway, which then eventually accelerates the proliferation of tumor cells.

Autophagy plays vital roles in oncogenesis and deterioration. Some studies have reported that it is a robust tumor suppressor, while others have noted that it is a tumor promoter. Such controversial conclusions may be due to the dynamic functions of autophagy in different pathophysiological circumstances and different disease stages [48]. Here, we found that the level of LAMP2A, a crucial indicator of autophagy, was greatly increased in high-grade human glioma tissue, especially in GBM, which had not been previously reported to our knowledge. Although it is well known that LAMP2A is strongly expressed in placenta, lung, breast, and liver cancer tissues [49, 50], its expression was still unclear in brain tumors. Our results showed that the LAMP2A expression level was greatly increased in human glioma tissue, and the tumor grade was positively correlated with the level of LAMP2A. These results suggested that the basal level of autophagy may be enhanced in glioma, and LAMP2A is involved in the occurrence and development of glioma. However, the upstream mechanism is still unknown and needs to be further explored [51].

Taken together, we identified that SMAD3 is a novel CMA substrate mediating the development of malignant glioma. CMA promotes the rapid proliferation of glioma cells via accelerating the degradation of SMAD3. Also, the expression of LAMP2A increases significantly in high-grade glioma tissue. Thus, SMAD3 and LAMP2A are not only promising therapeutic targets but also act as useful detection markers for glioma precancerosis to GBM development. However, there are some limitations in the present study. First, we have not yet validated our results in animal models of glioma, although we identified the phenomenon in samples from glioma patients and revealed the underlying mechanism of the CMA-SMAD3 pathway in human glioma cell lines in vitro. Second, we did not explore the phosphorylation level and phosphorylated sites of SMAD3 in the progression of glioma, since the function of SMAD3 is closely related to its phosphorylation status and sites. Further studies are needed to address these concerns.
