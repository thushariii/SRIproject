Abstract

It is well-established that, through canonical functions in transcription and DNA repair, the tumor suppressor p53 plays a central role in safeguarding cells from the consequences of DNA damage. Recent data retrieved in tumor and stem cells demonstrated that p53 also carries out non-canonical functions when interacting with the translesion synthesis (TLS) polymerase iota (POLι) at DNA replication forks. This protein complex triggers a DNA damage tolerance (DDT) mechanism controlling the DNA replication rate. Given that the levels of p53 trigger non-binary rheostat-like functions in response to stress or during differentiation, we explore the relevance of the p53 levels for its DDT functions at the fork. We show that subtle changes in p53 levels modulate the contribution of some DDT factors including POLι, POLη, POLζ, REV1, PCNA, PRIMPOL, HLTF and ZRANB3 to the DNA replication rate. Our results suggest that the levels of p53 are central to coordinate the balance between DDT pathways including (i) fork-deceleration by the ZRANB3-mediated fork reversal factor, (ii) POLι-p53-mediated fork-slowing, (iii) POLι- and POLη-mediated TLS and (iv) PRIMPOL-mediated fork-acceleration. Collectively, our study reveals the relevance of p53 protein levels for the DDT pathway choice in replicating cells.

Introduction

In recent years, there has been growing interest in the relevance that non-binary changes in the levels of key cellular components may have for the cell fate. Hence, by exploring the impact that subtle modulations in the levels of key proteins may have on biologically relevant outputs, we may better grasp the complexity in their regulation. Recently, the importance of p53 protein levels, rather than just its presence or absence has been highlighted by the analysis of stem cell differentiation programs and the dynamics of transcriptional responses after genotoxic treatments (1,2). p53, known as a tumor suppressor and guardian of genome integrity, plays a central role in protecting cells from DNA damage (3,4). So far, mostly canonical functions of transcriptionally regulating DNA repair, cell cycle and pro-apoptotic genes through sequence-specific DNA binding (5–8) have been causally linked with these genome stabilizing functions. Yet in parallel, accumulating evidence also carved out non-canonical roles of p53 in DNA repair, recombination and replication, operating independently of its canonical functions (9–16).

Introduction of damage into the DNA is a recurrent event, and the DNA repair efficiency is not sufficient to fully prevent the collision of replication forks with residual DNA lesions (17). Once damaged DNA is encountered by replicative DNA polymerases, they stall as they are unable to accommodate damaged DNA within their active sites (18). Evolution has developed multiple so-called DNA damage tolerance (DDT) mechanisms to override these obstacles (19). A DDT pathway described in eukaryotes already in the eighties (20) relies on specialized DNA polymerases with the unique ability to execute translesion DNA synthesis (TLS) across the damaged regions, guaranteeing the uninterrupted progression of DNA replication (18). TLS-polymerases are diverse, subdivided in different families and play critical roles in TLS across specific types of DNA lesions as compared to the less specialized recombination-mediated bypass (21). Within the Y-family of DNA polymerases, Polymerase eta (POLη) has traditionally been considered specialized on TLS across UV-induced DNA lesions (22), while a broader role in overcoming replication stress was recognized later on (23). The Y-family member REV1 is central in orchestrating the switch from replicative to TLS polymerases at replication forks encountering DNA lesions (24,25). REV1 also serves as loading platform during the TLS process itself. Similarly, the proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) recruits different TLS polymerases and increases the processivity of POLη and REV1 after PCNA mono-ubiquitination (mUb) (26,27). POL zeta (ζ), originating from the B-family and consisting of the catalytic REV3 and accessory REV7 subunits, extends tracks initiated by other specialized polymerases with a comparatively high processivity. Besides, PRIMPOL as a TLS and repriming polymerase promotes DNA replication-restart downstream of DNA lesions even during replication traverse of DNA interstrand crosslinks (28–31). The least processive and most error-prone Y-polymerase POLι was typically considered a backup enzyme of its binding partner POLη with a potential role in base excision repair (32). Though POLι was discovered over two decades ago, has been linked with genomic instability in cancer cells and represents an interesting target for anti-cancer therapies (33–35), its cellular function is still not fully understood. However, more recent works provided evidence for POLι–p53 complex formation at the replication fork introducing a new dimension in p53’s genome-protecting functions (36–38). This interaction gives rise to a DDT pathway that permits the progress of replication forks by a recombination-mediated bypass of obstacles on the template strand. The participation of the POLι–p53 complex in nascent DNA replication decelerates DNA synthesis in a manner depending on p53′s exonuclease activity (36) suggesting formation of a so-called idling complex, known to serve as a kinetic barrier to TLS through iterative nucleotide insertion and degradation steps (39). The outcome of this iteration is the activation at ongoing forks of HLTF, an E3 ligase, promoting the accumulation of poly-ubiquitinated (pUb) PCNA (40,41), which is recognized by the translocase ZRANB3 capable of catalyzing fork reversal for recombination-mediated bypass (36,42), thereafter slowing nascent DNA synthesis (36,38,43). This highly regulated DDT pathway not only protects the cells against DNA replication-associated damage (36,38,43) but also improves the accuracy of DNA replication (44), which provides a biological meaning for why this pathway predominates in human embryonal and adult stem cells as compared to their differentiated counterparts (38). Moreover, using DNA fiber assays in combination with S1 nuclease, recent work demonstrated that inactivation of this DDT pathway by POLι depletion blocks PRIMPOL-dependent re-priming with consequent accumulation of post-replicative single-stranded DNA gaps (43).

In the light of the relevance that the context such as differentiation status, species or strain may have for the POLι-p53 DDT pathway choice (38,45) and because of p53′s remarkable capability to adapt to cellular insults through rapid changes in its level (1), we sought to explore the impact that changes in p53 levels may have on the DDT pathway choices during unperturbed growth, i.e. without exogenous DNA damage treatment. Assessment of DNA replication dynamics at the single molecule level combined with the analyses of protein-protein interactions demonstrate that the p53 level is instrumental for the choice of at least four different DDT pathways. Our data suggest that at an optimum level p53 forms a complex with POLι to screen endogenous DNA replication impediments, while alternative mechanisms gain importance in the absence of such complex.

Materials and methods

Biological resources and cell culture

The Human non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cell line H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] contains a chromosomally integrated tet-off system for controlled expression of either p53(WT) as described in Ahn et al. (46). It was cultivated in DMEM (Gibco by Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), supplemented with 10% FBS (Merck Millipore Darmstadt), puromycin (2 μg/ml; Sigma-Aldrich by Merck), G418 (300 μg/ml, Sigma-Aldrich) and tetracycline (4500 ng/ml; Sigma-Aldrich). Before the start of experiments, cells were washed two times, split and cultivated in medium with indicated tetracycline doses for tetracycline-regulated expression of defined p53 levels. Twenty-four hours later, cells were washed again and re-incubated in medium with respective tetracycline-doses for an additional 24 h, after which experiments were performed. U2OS cells were cultured in DMEM Medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin–streptomycin–glutamine (Gibco by Thermo Fisher Scientific). Before experiments, U2OS cells were split and cultivated in medium without penicillin–streptomycin–glutamine treatment for 48 h. K562[HR-EGFP/3'EGFP] cells were cultivated in RPMI (Gibco by Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), supplemented with 15% FBS (Merck Millipore Darmstadt) as previously in Hampp et al. (36). Cell cultures were regularly tested for mycoplasma contamination by PCR.

Viability assay

For the assessment of cell viability, the colorimetric 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay was used following manufacturer′s instructions. The assay was performed 48 h after the respective treatment (hydroxyurea, doxorubicin). Cells were incubated in 0.5 mg/ml MTT for 3.5 h, lysed in 0.04 N HCl diluted in isopropanol and light absorbance was measured at 570 nm with an ELISA reader. Each data set was corrected by the corresponding solvent control value. Cell viability curves were plotted and IC50-values were calculated using GraphPad Prism 8 or 9 software (La Jolla, CA). Statistically significant differences between IC50-values of p53-expressing cell lines and control cell lines were calculated using Extra sum of squares F-test of log IC50 values.

Cell cycle distribution

For the analysis of the distribution in cell-cycle phases, H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells were trypsinized, washed once with PBS, resuspended with 0.5 ml PBS and fixed drop-wise in 4.5 ml fixing solution (1:1-mixture of acetone and 80% ethanol; storage at −20°C) during gentle mixing. After 15 min of incubation on ice, the fixed cells were washed twice with ice-cold PBS, resuspended in 200 ml propidium iodide (PI) staining solution (50 μg/ml RNase A was freshly added, 50 μg/ml PI from Sigma-Aldrich in PBS) and incubated for 30 min in the dark. Subsequently, the cell suspension was diluted with 100 ml 0.2% PBS–EDTA and analyzed in a FACS Calibur flow cytometer (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA).

Knockdown experiments

HiPerFect (Qiagen) was used for siRNA transfection for silencing selected targets in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] and U2OS cells. For siRNA-based gene silencing FlexiTube GeneSolution siRNAs (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) with pools of four target gene specific siRNAs were used to silence p53 (GS7157) (36), POLι (GS11201) (47), POLη (GS5429), REV3L (GS5980), REV1 (GS51455), PRIMPOL ({"type":"entrez-nucleotide","attrs":{"text":"GS201973","term_id":"255858626","term_text":"GS201973"}}GS201973), HLTF (GS6596), SMARCAL1 (GS50485) and ZRANB3 (GS84083) (43). For negative controls and fill-up to reach the same amount of RNA per transfection sample we engaged non-silencing (ns) siRNA (Qiagen).

DNA fiber spreading assay

To measure replication dynamics the DNA fiber spreading technique was performed as detailed in (36). Briefly, CldU (5-Chloro-2-deoxyuridine, Sigma-Aldrich by Merck) was incorporated for 20 min in case of H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] and 10 min in case of U2OS cells. After washing, IdU (5-Iodo-2-deoxyuridine, Sigma-Aldrich by Merck) was incorporated in a 10 times higher concentration for another 20 and 30 min, respectively. RAD51 inhibitor (B02) was used at a final concentration of 25 μM during IdU incorporation for Supplementary Figure S5C,D. Then, cells were washed, harvested, and resuspended in PBS. 2500 cells were put on a slide, lysed with 6 μL of 0.5% SDS, 200 mM Tris–HCl, pH7.4, 50 mM EDTA, and incubated at room temperature for 6 min. Afterwards, they were tilted about 20° to allow DNA spreading via gravity. Subsequently, slides were covered with aluminium foil, air-dried for 6 min, fixed for 5 min with newly prepared 3:1 methanol:acetic acid, air-dried for 7 min, and stored in 70% ethanol at 4°C. Twenty-four hours later, the slides were processed for denaturation/deproteination in 2.5 N HCl for 1 h, followed by immunofluorescence staining. To measure ongoing replication, the track lengths of bi-colored forks were measured but for clarity, only the red tracks are shown. For fork asymmetry analysis, the ratio of longer versus shorter lengths of red IdU tracks within three-colored red-green-red replication structures was calculated and graphically plotted to assess replication fork stalling.

Immunofluorescence staining after DNA fiber spreading assays was performed after blocking with 5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) for 45 min. The following primary antibodies were used: anti-BrdU for detection of IdU (mouse, mAb, clone B44, #347580 BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, New Jersey, USA) and anti-BrdU for detection of CldU (rat, mAb, clone BU1/75 [ICR1] Abcam #ab6326 Cambridge, UK) and incubated at 4°C over-night. As secondary antibodies, AlexaFluor555 (anti-mouse, Invitrogen by Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) or AlexaFluor488 (anti-rat, Invitrogen by Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, Massachusetts, USA) were used (incubation time: 1 h, room temperature). Subsequently, for Figure ​Figure2D2D counterstaining of ssDNA was performed with primary antibody anti-ssDNA (mouse, mAb, AB_10805144 DSHB, Iowa, USA) and incubated at 4°C over-night. Secondary antibody AlexaFluor647 (anti-mouse, Invitrogen by Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) was used (incubation time: 1 h, room temperature). DNA fibers were imaged with a Keyence BZ-9000 microscope (Keyence, Neu-Isenburg, Germany). DNA fiber track lengths were measured with BZ-II Analyzer software or using Fiji (ImageJ) software (ImageJ, U.S. National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA).

In situ proximity ligation assay (PLA) and γH2AX foci analysis

H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells were seeded on chamber slides (Falcon® Culture Slides 4-well, OMNILAB, Bremen, Germany) with a tetracycline-containing medium for regulated p53-expression. Twenty-four hours later, cells were washed and re-incubated in medium without/with tetracycline for an additional 24 h. Then, for Mitomycin C (MMC) treatment cells were washed, incubated with MMC (Sigma-Aldrich) containing medium at a final concentration of 3 μM or solvent H2O for 45 min, washed and re-incubated with fresh medium for an additional 3 h. Before fixing the cells with 3.6% formaldehyde in PBS (10 min), pre-extraction was performed for 1 min with pre-extraction buffer (300 mM Sucrose, 50 mM NaCl, 20 mM HEPES (pH 7.4), 3 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 0.5% (v/v) Triton X-100 in water). Blocking was performed using Duolink Blocking solution (Sigma-Aldrich) for 1 h at 37°C. Then cells were double-labeled with the primary antibodies anti-DNA Polymerase ι (mouse, mAb, OTI3B5, ThermoFisher #MA5-26503) and anti-p53pSer15 (rabbit, polyclonal, Cell Signalling #9284) or anti-DNA Polymerase ι (rabbit, polyclonal, Thermofisher, PA5-29442) and anti-PCNA (mouse mAb PC10, Abcam #ab29). PLA-stain was then performed using Duolink® PLA (Proximity Ligation assay to detect protein interaction) technology from Sigma-Aldrich. Therefore, glass slides were stained with Duolink® In Situ PLA® Probe Anti-Rabbit PLUS (Affinity purified Donkey anti-Rabbit IgG (H + L), Sigma, DUO92002) and Duolink® In Situ PLA® Probe Anti-Mouse MINUS (Affinity purified Donkey anti-Mouse IgG (H + L), Sigma, DUO92004) for 1 h at 37°C. After washing the samples were then incubated with the Ligation-Ligase solution for 30 min at 37°C to hybridize oligonucleotides tagged on probes. After two short washing steps, the glass slides were incubated with the Amplification-Polymerase solution for 100 min at 37°C to amplify hybridized oligonucleotides and fluorescently label the amplification products. Ligation and Amplification were performed with Duolink® In Situ Detection Reagents Orange (Sigma, DUO92007). Then, glass slides were covered with coverslips using Duolink® In Situ Mounting Medium with DAPI (Sigma, DUO82040). Imaging was performed using the Keyence BZ-9000 microscope (Keyence, Cologne, Germany). Automated quantification of PLA-foci was carried out with BZ-II Analyser software.

For γH2AX foci analysis H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells were grown on coverslips. Pre-extraction was performed for 1 min in 300 mM Sucrose, 50 mM NaCl, 20 mM HEPES (pH 7.4), 3 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 0.5% (v/v) Triton X-100 in water. Subsequently, cells were fixed in 3.7% (v/v) formaldehyde in PBS and permeabilized with 0.5% (v/v) Triton X-100 (Sigma-Aldrich by Merck) for 12 min at room temperature. Afterwards, slides were incubated for 45 min at 37°C with secondary antibodies AlexaFluor488 (anti-mouse from Invitrogen by ThermoFisher Scientific). Slides were mounted with Vectashield containing DAPI (Vector Laboratories, Burlingame, California, USA), followed by automated quantification of nuclear foci using a Keyence BZ-9000 microscope and BZ-II Analyser software. Intensity threshold and minimal focus size were maintained throughout one set of simultaneously treated and processed samples.

Immunoprecipitation (IP) experiments

For chromatin-prepared crosslink Co-IP (ChIP) of PCNA, cells were incubated for 12 min with cytoskeletal (CSK, 250 mM Sucrose, 25 mM KCl, 1 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 1 mM EGTA, 1 mM MgCl2, H2O) buffer with protease-inhibitors (1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF), 1 mM Na3VO4, 0.1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT), protease inhibitor cocktail [Roche, Basel, Switzerland]) to isolate nuclear extracts. Cross-linking was performed by adding 1% MeOH-free formaldehyde (Thermo Fisher Scientific) in PBS for 10 min at room temperature. This reaction was stopped by adding 10 mM ice-cold glycine. Cells were lysed for 15 min on ice in RIPA buffer (10 mM Tris–HCl [pH 7.5], 25 mM sodium fluoride, 20 mM sodium chloride, 1% (v/v) sodium-deoxycholate, 1% (v/v) Nonidet P40, 0.1% SDS, H2O; freshly added inhibitors: 1 mM PMSF, 1 mM Na3VO4, 0.1 mM DTT, protease inhibitor cocktail [Roche, Basel, Switzerland]). Thereafter cells were harvested and sonified in a sonification water bath Sonorex (Bandelin electronic GmbH & Co. KG, Berlin, Germany) at high power three-times each 7.5 min: 30 s on, 30 s off. After centrifugation, the supernatant was used for immunoprecipitation of PCNA (anti-PCNA, mouse, ab29, Abcam, Cambridge, UK). Isotype control was performed by using mouse IgG (sc-2025, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Dallas, TX, USA). After preclearing protein extracts with Protein G Sepharose beads (PGS, GE Healthcare, Munich, Germany) overnight, extracts were transferred to PGS beads with pre-bound antibodies and incubated for another 4 h at 4°C.

For immunoprecipitation of POLι, cells were lysed in 50 mM Tris, pH8; 150 mM NaCl; 1% NP40; protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche). These whole cell extracts were used for immunoprecipitation engaging monoclonal mouse POLι-antibody (sc-101026 Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Dallas, TX, USA) or as control mouse IgG (Santa Cruz). After spin-down, precipitated proteins were washed five times with lysis buffer and dissolved in SDS-PAGE sample buffer.

Recombination measurements

Recombination frequencies were determined via Flow Cytometry (FACSCaliburTM, BD Biosciences) as described (36). Briefly, K562 cells with chromosomally integrated recombination substrate (K562[HR-EGFP/3'EGFP]) were transfected via electroporation with expression plasmids for p53(WT) or empty vector (E.V.) (36). Each measurement was paralleled by the same co-transfection including a wild-type EGFP (wtEGFP) control plasmid instead of pBS plasmid for determination of transfection efficiency. Recombination frequencies were measured 72 h after transfection by quantification of EGFP-positive cells within the live cell population (SSC/FSC gate) and were individually corrected for transfection efficiencies.

ELISA

ELISA analysis was performed following the protocol of the manufacturer (ab171571 Abcam, Cambridge, UK). ELISA reader (Tecan Sunrise Microplate Photometer ST/TC, Crailsheim, Germany) at 450 nm was used to detect OD and calculate p53 concentrations.

Results

Differential effect of p53 level on protein expression and cell cycle control

The speed of DNA replication is affected by wild-type p53, an effect that is lost in oncogenic hotspot mutants and cancer-associated alternative isoforms of p53 (36,37,49,50). Given that human p53 differentially affects the speed of nascent DNA synthesis in stem cells and their differentiated counterparts expressing low and high p53 levels, respectively (38), we wondered whether p53 levels are the source of the change in replication speed. Therefore, we moved into a simplified model that allowed us to selectively focus on the modulation of the p53 levels by means of the expression of a tetracycline (tet)-repressed wild-type p53 in the non-small cell lung carcinoma H1299 (H1299[tet-off p53(WT)]) cells enabling analyses of the consequences of gradual changes of p53 levels (46). As a p53-negative control, resembling culture maintenance conditions, we applied the maximum concentration of tetracycline (4500 ng/ml) minimizing p53 expression down to a level precluding its detection in western blots except in overexposed images when compared to p53-negative H1299 (Figure ​(Figure1A,1A, Supplementary Figure S1A). For maximum expression, still in the physiological range (51), tetracycline was removed (0 ng/ml). According to Figure ​Figure1A1A and B, we additionally engaged tetracycline concentrations of 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2 and 4 ng/ml to cover the intermediate range of p53 levels in western blot and ELISA experiments. Expression of the major p53 target gene product p21 (52) closely followed p53 level changes (Figure ​(Figure1C,1C, ​,D)D) resulting in a highly significant correlation (rs= 0.96; P= 0.003). TLS-polymerase η (POLη), a key component of DDT mechanisms and a target gene product of p53 (53) showed a significant decrease only at the highest tetracycline concentration, i.e. at the minimum p53 level. Similarly, levels of the p53 target MDM2 (54) were significantly reduced when comparing high to minimum p53 expression levels (0 and 0.25 versus 4500 ng/ml). MDMX was also reduced when comparing maximum to minimum p53 levels (0 versus 4500 ng/ml) (Supplementary Figure S1B). Regarding expression of POLι, the polymerase loading factor PCNA or the repriming DNA polymerase PRIMPOL no significant level changes were observed (Figure ​(Figure1C,1C, ​,D,D, Supplementary Figure S1B). Such results are aligned with the fact that neither POLι nor PCNA nor PRIMPOL are known as p53 target gene products. Nevertheless, it is important to mention that POLι forms a mutually stabilizing complex with p53, when triggering fork remodeling (36,37).

To explore how these gradual changes in p53 expression impacted on the cell cycle distribution, we performed DNA content analysis by FACS (Supplementary Figure S1C). Similar accumulation of on average 70% of the cells in G1-phase was observed for tetracycline concentrations ≤4 ng/ml but a decline down to 59% upon exposure to 4500 ng/ml. Concomitantly, the S-phase fraction rose from ∼10% for tetracycline concentrations ≤2 ng/ml up to 14% and 22% for 4 and 4500 ng/ml, respectively. Moreover, the G2-phase percentage was significantly reduced above tetracycline concentrations of 0.5 ng/ml. Percentages of cells with subG1-DNA content were negligibly low (<1%) and did not differ significantly. We conclude that G1 arrest was maintained as long as p53 expression was readily detectable, while S- and particularly G2-phase changes were noticeable already when the p53 level was moderately altered.

The specific level of p53 determines DNA replication dynamics

To investigate the dynamics of nascent DNA synthesis as a function of gradual p53 expression changes, we performed DNA fiber spreading assays monitoring sequential incorporation of 5-chloro-2-deoxyuridine (CldU: green) and 5-iodo-2-deoxyuridine (IdU: red) in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells exposed to different tetracycline concentrations (36). The lengthening of the fiber tracks roughly correlated with the dose of tetracycline, so that there was an inverse correlation between p53 levels and track lengths (Figure ​(Figure2A).2A). Plotting the mean IdU track lengths revealed an almost linear augmentation of tracks, except for an intermediate peak and subsequent dip between tetracycline concentrations of 0.25 and 2 ng/ml suggesting the participation of more than a single DNA synthesis regulatory component (Figure ​(Figure2B).2B). To explore if stalling of DNA replication forks played a role in the observed DNA replication dynamics, we calculated the ratios between long and short tracks from bicolored green–red fibers (55,56). Despite observing significant changes in replication speed, we did not find any significant differences between track ratios (Figure ​(Figure2C).2C). To reveal fork stalling by an alternative method (57), we quantified asymmetries in the progression of sister forks emanating from the same three-colored red–green–red origins. Again, no significant differences were detected between the means obtained for different tetracycline concentrations, largely excluding the possibility that DNA track shortening occurs due to replication fork stalling (Supplementary Figure S2A). As previously reported (58,59), a shortening of replication tracks might also be the consequence of increased origin firing. When we calculated the fraction of three-colored origins among the total number of replication structures (55), we found significantly elevated origin firing only upon complete withdrawal of tetracycline (Supplementary Figure S2B). From this we conclude that replication track changes in the presence of different tetracycline concentrations (≥0.25 ng/ml) cannot be explained by altered origin firing. Additionally, we verified the integrity of DNA fibers by DNA counterstaining (Figure ​(Figure2D)2D) and excluded an effect of tetracycline on track lengths by performing DNA fiber assays in H1299 devoid of any p53 (Figure ​(Figure2E,2E, Supplementary Figure S1A).

Next, we wanted to determine if the modulation of the levels of p53 in a cellular model that expresses endogenous p53 also reveals changes in the replication tracks. We therefore titrated endogenous p53 in U2OS cells by using increasing concentrations of siRNA targeting the TP53 transcript [si(p53)] (Supplementary Figure S2C). As seen with H1299[tet-off p53(WT)], DNA replication tracks were longest in cells with the lowest p53 levels [8 nM and 10 nM si(p53)] and were shorter in cells with maximum p53 expression [0 nM si(p53) plus 10 nM nsRNA] (Supplementary Figure S2D). Interestingly, in cells with intermediate p53 levels we noticed that track lengths shortened until 4 nM and lengthened again until 10 nM si(p53). As seen in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells, fork stalling was unable to explain shortening of tracks in U2OS as well (Supplementary Figure S2E). Therefore, p53 modulates nascent DNA track lengths in a level-dependent manner both in cells expressing endogenous and exogenous p53. To understand whether observed changes in replication dynamics at distinct p53 levels impact on the accumulation of DNA damage, we proceeded with the analysis of the damage markers γH2AX and P-RPA and downstream effects on cell viability.

Impact of p53 level on DNA replication stress and cell viability

In human cells the selection of DDT pathways impacts on the level of replication stress and is crucial to promote cell survival (27,60,61). γH2AX, a phosphorylated form of the histone H2AX at Ser139, serves as a marker of DNA damage at sites of replication stress (62). To examine the impact of p53 levels on DNA damage responses, we analysed γH2AX foci formation focusing on four concentrations of tetracycline, covering a range from the maximum to minimum p53 expression levels (0 ng/ml and 4500 ng/ml) including two intermediate concentrations, namely intermediate high (0.25 ng/ml) and intermediate low (2 ng/ml) (Figure ​(Figure3A).3A). Significant differences were observed when comparing samples with no p53 (4500 ng/ml) with those expressing high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml), namely a decline of nuclear γH2AX foci numbers from 48 to on average 35. Additionally, we evaluated accumulation of RPA32 phosphorylated on Ser33 (P-RPA), another marker of replication stress (63). Consistent with γH2AX foci formation, P-RPA relative to total RPA levels increased in samples with low p53 expression (2 ng/ml and 4500 ng/ml tetracycline; 3.3- and 2.6-fold), providing further evidence for replication stress (Figure ​(Figure3B).3B). Collectively, our findings suggest that reduced p53 levels lead to imperfect bypass of replication barriers, resulting in elevated replication stress, which prolongs S-phase (Supplementary Figure S1C).

Hydroxyurea (HU) is a potent inhibitor of ribonucleotide reductase, a key enzyme involved in the synthesis of deoxyribonucleotide triphosphates (dNTPs). Consequently, HU administration leads to a significant depletion of available dNTPs, subsequently resulting in a deceleration of the DNA replication process (64). To assess cell viability under enforced replication stress conditions, we performed MTT assay 48 h after HU treatment. p53 expression caused a significant increase in cell viability (compare the samples with high p53 levels—0 and 0.25 ng/ml, and the lowest p53 level, 4500 ng/ml, Figure ​Figure3C).3C). When treating with the chemotherapeutic agent doxorubicin, which interferes with topoisomerase action generating DNA double-strand breaks (65,66), the opposite phenotype was observed. In this case, p53 levels at 0, 0.25 and 2 ng/ml promoted cell death when compared to samples expressing the lowest p53 level at 4500 ng/ml (Figure ​(Figure3D).3D). These findings are reminiscent of the concept that p53 acts in DNA replication to promote survival, while canonical functions in transcription and apoptosis are induced in response to severe DNA damage such as DSBs (50). Our results support the idea that the cell fate is determined not solely by the presence or absence of p53, but rather by the levels of p53 expression. This delicate balance influences cell destiny, activating checkpoint control or even cell death, depending on the extent and nature of DNA damage in combination with the specific p53 level. In the light of these findings and the previous chapter showing that DNA replication fork stalling (Figure ​(Figure2C,2C, Supplementary Figure S2A, E) or changes in the frequency of origin firing (Supplementary Figure S2B) are unlikely to fully explain changes in DNA track length, we moved forward to explore the relevance of p53 levels for the DDT pathway choice that controls track lengthening.

POLι silencing abrogates the changes in track length caused by the expression of increasing levels of p53

Previously, we demonstrated regulatory roles of p53 in DNA replication dynamics in a manner depending on the differentiation status of human cells (36–38). Slow-down of DNA synthesis in stem cells was demonstrated to rely on the physical interactions between POLι and p53 (36,38). Conversely, p53-dependent DNA replication speed-up was shown to involve association of POLι and PCNA, but not p53, with the replication fork (38). Given the relevance of POLι for the p53-dependent control of DNA synthesis, we downregulated POLι in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] samples with increasing expression of p53, at the four tetracycline concentrations 0, 0.25, 2 and 4500 ng/ml. Indeed, POLι knockdown caused elongation of fiber tracks at the two higher p53 levels (0, 0.25 ng/ml tetracycline). To the contrary, POLι knockdown triggered fiber shortening at 2 ng/ml tetracycline (intermediate low). Finally, no effect of POLι silencing was detected at 4500 ng/ml tetracycline (Figure ​(Figure4A,4A, ​,B)B) which efficiently suppressed p53 expression. Regardless of the tetracycline concentration significant changes of track ratios and fork asymmetries were not detected, largely excluding involvement of fork stalling in the changes of track lengths caused by p53 expression (Figure ​(Figure4C,4C, ​,D).D). Assaying cell viability at four different p53 levels in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells revealed lowest viability at its minimum level (4500 ng/ml tetracycline) (Supplementary Figure S3A) matching the increase in replication stress signals seen before (Figure ​(Figure3A,3A, ​,B).B). Knockdown of POLι reduced cell viabilities under all four p53 expression conditions by up to 39% relative to the corresponding transfection-control sample (nsRNA), demonstrating a protective role of POLι.

Strikingly, when we examined the role of POLι under conditions of differential expression of endogenous p53 in U2OS, we observed almost the same contribution of POLι to track lengths as judged from the changes caused by its knockdown. At the two highest p53 levels [0 and 4 nM si(p53)] POLι knockdown resulted in track lengthening, while in cells with intermediate low p53 levels [8 nM si(p53)] POLι silencing had the opposite effect (Supplementary Figure S3B, C). However, at maximum downregulation of endogenous p53 [20 nM si(p53)], we observed replication track elongation after POLι knockdown. POLι knockdown caused stalling at the two higher levels of p53 [0 and 4 nM si(p53)], and the opposite with intermediate low p53 [8 nM si(p53)] (Supplementary Figure S3D). As stalling was associated with longer tracks after POLι silencing at 0 and 4 nM si(p53), it might be caused by the detrimental effect of excessively high replication speed in this cellular model system (67,68). From these observations, we concluded that, albeit different DDT hierarchies could be established in different cells expressing p53, the levels of p53 and the expression of POLι rule on the DDT pathway choice.

The level of p53 controls formation of specific POLι complexes

In order to understand the underlying mechanism, we investigated whether specific p53 levels differentially modulate formation of protein complexes involved in the observed replication phenotypes. To this end, we analysed the in situ association of POLι with the serine 15 phosphorylated form of p53, p53(pSer15), known to be active in the regulation of DNA replication (36–38). When performing proximity ligation assay (PLA) for POLι and p53(pSer15), we observed that only cells expressing intermediate high levels of p53 (0.25 ng/ml tetracycline) showed a significant augmentation of PLA foci numbers over the 4500 ng/ml tetracycline sample (Figure ​(Figure5A).5A). To verify that PLA foci reflect POLι-p53 complex formation, we performed co-immunoprecipitation experiments. Indeed, p53 was prominently detected in POLι co-precipitates under POLι-p53 PLA-positive conditions (0.25 ng/ml tetracycline), and to a lesser extent at maximum p53 expression without tetracycline (Figure ​(Figure5B).5B). Hence, the POLι-p53 complexes may trigger replication fork deceleration at intermediate high p53 level (0.25 ng/ml tetracycline) with a less prominent contribution at the highest p53 level (0 ng/ml tetracycline).

Secondly, we examined proximity between POLι and the replication clamp PCNA. We observed a two-fold increase in POLι-PCNA PLA foci at intermediate low p53 level (2 ng/ml tetracycline) compared with all other samples (Figure ​(Figure5C).5C). A smaller, 1.6-fold increase of POLι-PCNA PLA foci was also detectable in samples at minimum (4500 ng/ml) versus maximum (0 ng/ml) p53 expression. POLι co-precipitated with PCNA in the samples with intermediate low p53 level (2 ng/ml tetracycline) and was undetectable in pull-downs from the other samples (Figure ​(Figure5D).5D). Hence, POLι-dependent track lengthening observed at intermediate low p53 (2 ng/ml tetracycline; see Figure ​Figure4A)4A) correlates with increased POLι-PCNA complex formation but not POLι–p53 complex formation.

POLι–p53 complexes were previously demonstrated to trigger a replication decelerating DDT pathway involving recombinative fork remodeling (36), while POLι–PCNA complexes were found to play a role in the replication accelerating DDT pathway TLS (38). Therefore, we wondered whether the DDT pathway switchboard, PCNA (69,70), was differentially ubiquitinated under conditions of differential p53 expression. Remarkably, we detected a significant 2- to 3-fold rise of polyubiquitinated/monoubiquitinated (pUb/mUb) PCNA when comparing 0 ng/ml tetracycline to the increasing levels of tetracycline (Figure ​(Figure5E).5E). Specificity of pUb bands was verified by knockdown of the E3 ligase HLTF (Figure ​(Figure5F).5F). This result suggested that at its highest level p53 may promote deceleration of replication forks by engaging DDT events triggered by PCNA pUb. Altogether, these results suggest that p53 may promote different DDT pathways to overcome the barriers in a manner that depends on the p53 levels, predominantly promoting pUb-triggered DDT at the highest p53 levels (0 ng/ml tetracycline), POLι-p53-mediated DDT at intermediate high p53 levels (0.25 ng/ml tetracycline) and POLι–PCNA-mediated DDT at intermediate low p53 levels (2 ng/ml tetracycline).

p53 levels regulate the role of different DDT effector molecules on nascent DNA synthesis

Given that POLι negatively or positively affects track lengths in a manner that depends on p53 levels, we explored the contribution of key factors involved in TLS, fork reversal and repriming under conditions of increasing p53 levels. We firstly performed knockdown of POLη, a transcriptional p53 target (53) with a well characterized role in TLS at DNA lesions including cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (UV-induced CPDs) and in the duplication of difficult to replicate regions such as common fragile sites (CFS) (71–73). Secondly and thirdly, we downregulated REV3L, the catalytic subunit of POLζ, which is highly proficient in primer extension after TLS polymerases′ activation, as well as REV1, a scaffold for the other TLS polymerases and a dCMP inserter itself (74,75). Knockdown of each of these TLS polymerases (Figure ​(Figure6A)6A) induced shortening of fiber track lengths at the minimum level of p53 (4500 ng/ml tetracycline) suggesting they all participate in track elongation in these samples. POLη and REV3L knockdown also modified track lengths at intermediate low p53 level (2 ng/ml) while REV3L but not POLη knockdown shortened tracks at intermediate high p53 level (0.25 ng/ml) (Figure ​(Figure6A).6A). Neither POLη nor REV3L contributed to nascent DNA synthesis when p53 levels were highest (0 ng/ml). Under these experimental conditions, neither track ratio nor fork asymmetry analyses revealed significant differences between knockdown and control values (Figure ​(Figure6B,6B, Supplementary Figure S4A). These results suggested that knockdown-induced track shortening was due to a slow-down of fork speed rather than stalling.

Distinct from POLη and REV3L, downregulation of REV1 revealed a positive contribution to track lengthening, which was observed when p53 was markedly expressed (0–2 ng/ml tetracycline). REV1 knockdown-induced lengthening of fiber tracks, was coupled with elevated track ratios. These results reveal that p53 expressed at a distinct level (intermediate low, 2 ng/ml) enables opposed contributions of TLS polymerases to nascent DNA synthesis, promoting POLη-, REV3L-dependent lengthening and REV1-dependent shortening of the replication tracks, a complexity that was not anticipated by the current understanding of the DDT by TLS polymerases. Conversely, under conditions of minimum p53 expression, all three TLS polymerases (POLη, REV3L and REV1) canonically contribute to DNA synthesis by accelerating the DNA replication speed.

Next, we assessed the contribution of the repriming DNA polymerase, PRIMPOL, which has been reported to play an important role in bypassing secondary DNA structures particularly during unperturbed DNA replication (76,77). Knockdown of PRIMPOL unveiled a dual role depending on the levels of p53 expression: At high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml) PRIMPOL knockdown caused lengthening of replication tracks (Figure ​(Figure6A).6A). Notably, evidence of fork stalling was revealed when p53 levels were intermediate high (0.25 ng/ml) and PRIMPOL was downregulated (Figure ​(Figure6B,6B, Supplementary Figure S4A). Under conditions of minimum p53 expression (4500 ng/ml) PRIMPOL knockdown shortened track lengths. In conclusion, as for other specialized DNA polymerases, p53 levels regulate whether PRIMPOL promotes or opposes nascent DNA track lengthening.

Given that previous evidence showed that the fork reversal inducing enzymes HLTF and ZRANB3 are functionally associated with p53 during nascent DNA synthesis (36,38), we examined their impact on track lengths as a function of the p53 level. Knockdown of HLTF and ZRANB3 resulted in the same pattern of fiber track changes (Figure ​(Figure6A),6A), namely increased track lengths at the maximum p53 level (0 ng/ml) and track shortening at intermediate low and minimum p53 levels (2 ng/ml, 4500 ng/ml). When focusing on track ratios, in the case of ZRANB3 knockdown, elevated values were revealed when p53 levels were intermediate low or when p53 was minimally expressed (2, 4500 ng/ml). In the case of HLTF knockdown, values were elevated when p53 levels were at their maximum (0 ng/ml). This trend was also seen after HLTF knockdown at intermediate p53 levels (0.25 ng/ml: P= 0.0643 and 2 ng/ml, P= 0.0540) without fully reaching statistical significance (Figure ​(Figure6B,6B, Supplementary Figure S4A). For comparison, knockdown of the translocase SMARCAL1 did not influence the DNA fiber track length at high p53 levels (0, 0.25 ng/ml) (Supplementary Figure S4B, C). SMARCAL1 has a specific polarity for reversing forks with RPA-coated leading strands gaps in vitro, while ZRANB3 was reported not to show a preference for regressing either lagging or leading strand gap forks (78,79), arguing against a substrate specificity for RPA coated leading strand gaps at these p53 levels.

In order to investigate the impact of the p53 level on fork remodeling, we previously made use of a reporter cell line enabling measurements of replication-associated recombination under unperturbed growth conditions (36). Consequently, we used these p53-negative K562 reporter cells to measure recombination as a function of p53 expression (Supplementary Figure S5A, B). The results show that recombination increases with increasing p53 level, which supports the concept of elevated fork remodeling at maximum p53 level in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells (0 ng/ml tetracycline). Moreover, we performed DNA fiber spreading assays with and without inhibition of RAD51 (Supplementary Figure S5, D), which is known to promote different types of fork remodeling ranging from fork reversal to homologous recombination (80). As was expected from the involvement of PRIMPOL and TLS POLs in track elongation at lower p53 levels (2, 4500 ng/ml), inhibition of RAD51 did not affect track lengths under these conditions. However, at higher p53 levels (0, 0.25 ng/ml), inhibition of RAD51 further reduced track lengths due to fork stalling, which indicates a protective role at replication forks during remodeling. Taken together, the fork reversal factors HLTF and ZRANB3 prevented excessive replication speed at maximum p53 expression, most likely through their canonical role in fork reversal. At low p53 expression levels, when the basal DNA replication speed is high, the depletion of these fork reversal factors slows down DNA replication, most likely due to fork stalling.

Double depletion experiments unveil functional crosstalk between POLι, POLη and PRIMPOL at different levels of p53

In recent years, several studies (81–84) found pieces of evidence proposing a hierarchical framework for selection of DDT pathways. To elucidate the role that the changes in p53 levels have on such an interplay between key DDT factors, we performed double-knockdown experiments targeting POLι, POLη and PRIMPOL in combination with the modulation of p53 levels.

Combined downregulation of POLι and PRIMPOL resulted in track lengthening at the two highest p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml tetracycline) (Figure ​(Figure7A),7A), a result that correlates with those obtained after single knockdown of either POLι or PRIMPOL. At lower p53 levels, the individual contributions of each knockdown were revealed. Specifically, there was a reduction in track lengths at intermediate low p53 levels (2 ng/ml tetracycline), which is reminiscent of the effect seen in the individual POLι knockdown. At 4500 ng/ml tetracycline, track lengths were similar to those from the individual PRIMPOL knockdown phenotype. Fork stalling was only noticed at maximum p53 level (0 ng/ml tetracycline) suggesting that, when p53 levels are the highest forks decelerate in the presence of POLι and PRIMPOL but they stall when both proteins are absent (Figure ​(Figure7B).7B). These results suggest that POLι and PRIMPOL can individually contribute to nascent DNA synthesis with no indications for interference of one with another.

When simultaneously depleting POLι and POLη, a combination of the individual knockdown effects was observed, namely elongation at high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml tetracycline), as seen for the individual POLι knockdown, and shortening at intermediate low p53 level (2 ng/ml tetracycline) as seen for individual knockdowns of both POLι or POLη. At the minimum level of p53 (4500 ng/ml tetracycline) the double POLι and POLη knockdown recapitulated the single POLη knockdown (Figure ​(Figure7A).7A). There was no evidence of fork stalling in these scenarios (Figure ​(Figure7B).7B). In conclusion, also in this case of the combination of POLι and POLη knockdown, the individual contributions of each DNA polymerase to track shortening or lengthening did not cause any interference with the function of the other.

Lastly, we performed DNA fiber analysis after POLη and PRIMPOL downregulation. Surprisingly, nascent DNA track shortening was revealed in all samples regardless of the levels of p53 expression (Figure ​(Figure7A).7A). Track ratios remained unaffected, indicating that tracks shorten as a consequence of decelerated fork progression rather than because of replication fork stalling (Figure ​(Figure7B).7B). While it is possible that the reduced replication speed documented at the two lowest p53 levels is the consequence of the phenotypes observed after individual knockdown of POLη and PRIMPOL, the deceleration observed at elevated p53 levels is puzzling as POLη alone did not have an impact on fiber length, whereas the depletion of PRIMPOL alone resulted in elongation of fiber tracks. From these observations, we conclude that when p53 is highly expressed and PRIMPOL absent track elongation is dependent on POLη.

Collectively, these findings unveil an unpredicted complexity in the contribution of DDT factors to nascent DNA elongation as a function of the p53 level. While the double knockdown experiments in Figure ​Figure77 suggest that specialized DNA polymerases can provide independent contributions to nascent DNA elongation, expression of p53 can cause a diametrically opposed effect on nascent DNA synthesis by the same factors. In fact, our results reveal that the levels of p53 are central in establishing the hierarchies between DDTs pathways, favouring fork reversal mediated DDT when p53 is at its highest level, POLι-p53 complex-mediated DDT when p53 levels are intermediate high and TLS-mediated DDT when p53 levels are low.

Discussion

We have previously shown that wild-type p53 together with POLι triggers a DDT pathway characterized by a replication slow-down, which ensures genome integrity and mediates a pro-survival effect (36,38). In this study, we sought to understand the impact of the p53 expression level on the DDT pathway selection. Our findings reveal the coexistence of different phenotypes whereby even minor fluctuations in p53 levels can shift the equilibrium towards emergence of one or the other mechanism in such a continuum. The highest p53 levels increasingly favor the POLι-p53 pathway via stages of idling and downstream HLTF and ZRANB3 activities. Conversely, at intermediate low to minimum p53 expression, a delicate equilibrium between multiple DDT mechanisms appears to govern the choice favoring TLS and repriming, respectively (Figure ​(Figure88 and Graphical Abstract). These results underscore the critical role of p53 levels in determining the DDT pathway choice providing valuable insights into the molecular basis of pathway selection.

Model for p53 level-dependent DDT pathway choices. Identified by key molecular components as outlined in the text. Briefly, high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml) slow down DNA replication by complex formation with POLι and idling (36) triggering HLTF- and ZRANB3- mediated fork remodeling at the highest p53 level (0 ng/ml). Low p53 levels (2.5 ng/ml) unleash fast DNA synthesis through TLS activities e.g. by POLι or POLη. At minimal p53 levels (4500 ng/ml) and reduced level of its target gene product POLη, PRIMPOL plays a key role in elongating DNA replication tracks. Such unrestrained repriming entails DNA replication stress and sensitizes cells to exogenous replication stress. An additional role of PRIMPOL in fork slow-down at high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml) may involve repression of fast TLS such as initiated by POLη and/or gap formation promoting recombination-mediated template switch (92). REV1 as TLS-POL loader, REV3L as TLS extender of TLS-POLs (27) and gap filler post-PRIMPOL (83,84) as well as HLTF promoting mono- and poly-ubiquitination of PCNA (41,90,96,97) are epistatic with multiple activities.

The p53 level controls DNA replication dynamics through the formation of different POLι complexes

In 2016 (36), we proposed that p53 forms an idling complex with POLι, slowing down replication and, thus, generating a window of time for PCNA to accumulate pUb marks, which trigger an error-free fork remodeling mechanism (85). This model was validated in subsequent studies, not only in various cancer cell lines (37,49) but also in different types of primary human stem cells (38). In our present investigation, we provide evidence for an even much more complex hierarchy of DDT pathway usage under the control of p53, unveiled by gradual changes of the p53 level in H1299 and U2OS cancer cell models by tet-off-controlled expression of exogenous p53 or siRNA-mediated depletion of endogenous p53. We observed that high levels of p53 impaired DNA elongation in a POLι-dependent manner in both cell lines. Interestingly, intermediate low levels of p53 appear to enable POLι to function independently of the p53 complex, possibly in its previously characterized TLS polymerase role. Under these conditions, POLι depletion impairs DNA replication elongation in both cell lines. Yet, we notice that results obtained after p53 induction do not fully overlap with results obtained after downregulation of endogenous p53. At minimum p53 level, replication speed becomes independent of POLι in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells but not in U2OS cells. The lowest p53 levels reached in both cellular models may not be the only differences, as the expression of molecular DDT components and/or acquisition of cancer stem cell-like features in any of these cells may also be different (38,86). Despite such uncertainties, our data clearly show that gradual changes in both the exogenous and endogenous level of p53 are decisive for the rate of nascent DNA synthesis. This choreography fully depends on POLι at least in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)] cells, as POLι depletion neutralizes p53-dependent changes in the DNA replication dynamics.

Complex formation of POLι with p53 was observed only at higher p53 levels. Contrarily, the POLι-PCNA complex gained significance when p53 levels were lower, which could be interpreted to simply reflect the stoichiometry between the complex partners POLι and p53. Arguing against this one-dimensional explanation, PLA and immunoprecipitation experiments revealed maximum complex formation of POLι with p53 at a concentration of 0.25 ng/ml tetracycline in H1299[tet-off p53(WT)]. At 0 ng/ml tetracycline, i.e. the maximum level of p53, such a complex was no longer detectable by PLA and only visible as a very faint band after immunoprecipitation. From these results, we conclude that POLι-p53 complexes are formed and remain stable at an optimum, non-maximal, p53 level. Since DNA lesions and the cell cycle distribution were similar at the two highest p53 levels, we speculate that checkpoint signaling is unlikely to play a role. More likely, the biochemical properties of these factors at replication forks may be decisive for POLι-p53 complex stability. For example, the oligomerisation state of p53 within the complex (37), the posttranslational modifications of complex components and/or the structural changes in the recognized DNA at the fork may differ at different p53 levels. Our analysis at maximum p53 level indicates a significant increase in PCNA pUb, known to trigger fork reversal (42,85). Our immunoprecipitation and PLA experiments may therefore provide snapshots of different stages of the POLι-p53 pathway as previously reported (38). The pathway was reported to involve an initial step involving formation of the idling complex (36) (a step that may be enriched at 0.25 ng/ml tetracycline) that creates a time-window promoting PCNA pUb at stalled forks followed by fork remodeling and p53 dissociation (a step that may be enriched at 0 ng/ml tetracycline). Interestingly also, PLA and immunoprecipitation detected POLι-PCNA complexes at 2 ng/ml but not at 4500 ng/ml, i.e. the lowest p53 level. This indicates that an intermediate low level of p53 is required for POLι-PCNA TLS complex formation but not enough to generate the POLι-p53 idling complex. Since p53 is not a component of the TLS complex, canonical functions of p53 in transcriptional transactivation may play a role towards TLS, such as modulation of PCNA Ub (38,87,88) or expression of POLη (53). This strengthens the idea that more than one pathway switch occurs along the range of different p53 expression levels in the cell.

Complex crosstalk of DDT pathway-specific factors at different levels of p53

Previously, different factors involved in the POLι-p53 DDT pathway were identified (36–38). In this study, we dissected the hierarchy between these factors along different p53 levels. Due to our results of DNA fiber analyses subsequent to the depletion of TLS factors—specifically POLη, POLζ (REV3L) and REV1—we can conclude that TLS takes center stage at intermediate low p53 levels. Noteworthy however, REV1 plays a dual role depending on the p53 concentration: at intermediate low levels of p53, when TLS is predominant, REV1 potentiates TLS either as an inserter of nucleotides or as a hub for the recruitment of other TLS polymerases (75). Conversely, at high p53 where the POLι–p53 DDT pathway is predominant, REV1 may support nascent DNA synthesis deceleration by POLι–p53. Track ratio analysis revealed increased values only when REV1 depletion unleashed excessively fast replication, causing non-classical replication stress (67,68). Interestingly, POLζ down-regulation decelerated DNA synthesis not only at lower levels of p53 (2, 4500 ng/ml) but up to 0.25 ng/ml tetracycline, where the POLι–p53 complex was most prominent. Based on these results, we postulate that fork remodeling and TLS pathways act simultaneously at 0.25 and 2 ng/ml tetracycline. This highlights that DNA replication speed integrates contributions from different DDT pathways that can co-occur and compete with one another at intermediate p53 levels.

When depleting the fork remodeling factors HLTF and ZRANB3, replication track elongation was detectable only at the highest p53 level (0 ng/ml), not at both higher levels (0, 0.25 ng/ml) as seen after POLι knock-down. In the light of these and our results from POLι-p53 interaction analyses, it is possible that at the two highest p53 levels replication slow-down is caused by different processes. At 0 ng/ml tetracycline, it is driven through fork reversal by HLTF and ZRANB3 (42,89). At 0.25 ng/ml, when the impact of HLTF and ZRANB3 on DNA replication dynamics was no longer significant, but the POLι–p53 complex prominent, fork slowing may rely on idling (36). It cannot be excluded that canonical functions of p53 may also contribute to this scenario, as sequestration of p53 at sites of transcriptional transactivation may constrain its availability for POLι interactions.

Of note, after HLTF and ZRANB3 knockdown at lower p53 levels (2, 4500 ng/ml) shortening of replication tracks was documented. Importantly however, down-regulation of ZRANB3 at lower p53 levels generated a unique scenario, characterized by increased track ratios combined with fork slowing. Hence, in such particular conditions, the shortening of fibers can be explained by classical fork stalling rather than by slow-down of DNA synthesis. In opposition, knockdown of HLTF also entailed a significant increase of fork asymmetry at the highest p53 level (0 ng/ml), but in this case, likely due to excessively fast replication. Therefore, similarly to REV1, HLTF plays a dual role in DNA replication elongation which may be related to the role of HLTF in the induction of both mUb and pUb of PCNA (40,41,90,91). Thus, its depletion may not only reduce pUb marks on PCNA read by ZRANB3 for fork reversal (42), but also mUb marks on PCNA, necessary to recruit alternative POLs for TLS (90).

Recent studies unveiled opposing hierarchical relationships between PRIMPOL-mediated repriming and fork remodeling depending on the availability of specific DDT pathway components (81,83,92). In our study, knockdown experiments carved out a bimodal role of PRIMPOL, namely the expected repriming function accelerating DNA replication at undetectable p53 levels (4500 ng/ml) and, like POLι, a role in fiber shortening at the two higher p53 levels (0, 0.25 ng/ml). Simultaneous depletion of POLι and PRIMPOL reflected the individual replication phenotypes at lower p53 levels, i.e. replication accelerating activities by POLι-mediated TLS at 2 ng/ml and PRIMPOL-mediated repriming at 4500 ng/ml. At higher p53 levels (0, 0.25 ng/ml) double depletion suggested an epistatic relationship between POLι and PRIMPOL. Interestingly, earlier work from both yeast (92) and human cells (93) showed that repriming precedes template switching at the fork, as the first choice before fork reversal or TLS. From this we suggest that at higher p53 levels PRIMPOL could promote recombination-mediated template switching in line with the POLι-p53 complex and coexisting with HLTF- and ZRANB3-dependent fork reversal at the highest p53 level (0 ng/ml). In support of this idea, the POLι–p53 pathway was originally discovered as mechanism mediating recombination-mediated bypass of replication barriers (36). Therefore, depending on the window of p53 expression, POLι which forms a complex with PCNA, may compete with PRIMPOL to prevent DNA damage by excessively fast DNA replication or forms a complex with p53 and cooperates with PRIMPOL to ensure error-free bypass of replication barriers by fork remodeling, improving the resilience towards exogenous replication stress. In future work it will be interesting to analyze the potential formation of complexes of PRIMPOL and/or REV1 with p53 or POLι given that DNA fiber phenotypes were similar after knockdown of either POLι, PRIMPOL or REV1 at the highest p53 levels (compare Figure ​Figure4A4A with Figure ​Figure6A).6A). Future work may also address gap formation by methods such as electron microscopy, a technique that allows to determine the localization of single-stranded DNA gaps at or behind replication fork junctions, their size and number as well as the distribution on the two parental strands (94,95).

Which factor is responsible for accelerated DNA replication in the absence of POLι or absence of PRIMPOL at high p53 levels (0 ng/ml, 0.25 ng/ml)? To answer this question we performed further double knockdown experiments. POLι and POLη double depletion generated a pattern reflecting the combination of the individual knockdown effects at all tetracycline concentrations excluding the possibility that POLη is the only polymerase accelerating DNA replication in absence of POLι. Strikingly, when depleting PRIMPOL together with POLη, fiber analysis reflected the individual depletions at 2 ng/ml and 4500 ng/ml, but a new phenotype compared to single depletions, namely fiber shortening, at 0 and 0.25 ng/ml. This suggests that the depletion of PRIMPOL unleashes the TLS activities of POLη. In conclusion, PRIMPOL could play an important role in the p53-POLι DDT pathway by supporting the choice of fork remodeling over TLS.

Conclusions

Altogether, we postulate that different p53 levels promote the choice of different DDT pathways in charge of DNA replication. At high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml), DNA replication is slowed down through complex formation with POLι. At the highest p53 level (0 ng/ml), HLTF- and ZRANB3-mediated fork remodeling becomes the predominant DDT pathway. Conversely, at low p53 levels (2 ng/ml), DNA synthesis is accelerated by POLι- and POLη-mediated TLS. Only when p53 levels are hardly detectable (4500 ng/ml), and POLη, a p53 target, downregulated, PRIMPOL plays a role in DNA replication elongation. Conversely, PRIMPOL contributes to the deceleration of forks at high p53 levels (0 and 0.25 ng/ml) by repressing fast POLη-dependent TLS and possibly also by generating recombinogenic replication intermediates. Additional factors perform epistatic functions, namely REV1 acting as a TLS polymerase loader, REV3L as a TLS extender and gap filler post-repriming, and HLTF as a PCNA mUb and pUb E3 ligase (Figure ​(Figure8).8). In conclusion, prevalence of a certain replication phenotype involves many DNA replication factors that in part can play bimodal roles, whereby coexisting pathways establish a complex equilibrium determining the outcome. This work reveals the existence of a dynamic balance between DDT pathways finely tuned by a p53-mediated equilibrium. Slight perturbations can shift the pathway choice, highlighting the vulnerability of these control mechanisms safeguarding genome integrity.
