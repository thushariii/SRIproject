The function of carnosic acid in lipopolysaccharides-induced hepatic and intestinal inflammation in poultry

Abstract

Inflammatory processes are often accompanied by oxidative stress and lipid peroxidation, which might lead to cellular and organ damage. Carnosic acid (CA), an active component found in rosemary, exhibits pharmacological properties including antioxidative, anti-inflammatory, and antiviral effects. The aim of this research was to investigate whether CA can mitigate lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced oxidative stress and inflammatory responses in poultry and to understand its underlying mechanisms. We administered CA to broiler chickens via oral gavage and treated them with LPS, followed by analysis of the effects of different dosages of CA on body weight, antioxidative capacity, and inflammatory factors. Carnosic acid had no significant impact on the body weight of broiler chickens. However, serum analysis indicated that the middle dose of CA effectively enhanced the antioxidative capacity and reduced levels of oxidative stress and inflammation-related factors. Moreover, in the liver, CA demonstrated the ability to regulate the expression of proteins such as heat shock protein 60 (HSP60), heat shock protein 70 (HSP70), and P38 mitogen-activated protein kinase (P38), suggesting its protective role against liver damage induced by LPS. In the intestinal tract of broiler chickens, CA regulated the expression and localization of proteins including HSP60, HSP70, NFE2 like bZIP transcription factor 2 (Nrf2), and P38, while also influencing the expression of inflammatory markers such as protein tyrosine phosphatase receptor type C (CD45), and connexin (Cx). These findings revealed the potential protective mechanisms of CA in alleviating oxidative stress and inflammatory damage induced by LPS in poultry. Carnosic acid notably enhanced the chickens' antioxidative capacity by modulating the expression of key proteins, thereby reducing oxidative stress and inflammatory response levels. This study provides a deeper comprehension of the protective mechanisms of CA and its potential impact on avian health.

Key words: lipopolysaccharide model, inflammation, oxidative stress, carnosic acid, NFE2 like bZIP transcription factor 2 pathway

INTRODUCTION

In the intensive cultivation of poultry, several factors, such as environmental stress, pathogen infection, and noise, could affect the poultry growth rate or even lead to sudden death, which cause economic losses worldwide (Chloupek et al., 2009; Bilal et al., 2021; Hafez et al., 2021; Korver, 2023). Research suggests that different stressors or pathogen infections might act via similar pathways, including oxidative stress and inflammation signaling pathway (Mishra and Jha, 2019; Surai et al., 2019; Basiouni et al., 2023). Therefore, selecting feed additives with antioxidative and anti-inflammatory abilities is important. Antibiotics have been used as feed additives to prevent various diseases of broilers. However, several countries have forbidden the use of antibiotics in feed additives, including China (since 2020). In addition, selecting a feed additive with antipathogen activities is also very important. Consequently, plant extracts have garnered considerable attention because of their advantages of no residues, lack of resistance, and antioxidative properties. Carnosic acid (CA), identified as an active component within rosemary extracts, possesses robust pharmacological attributes encompassing powerful antioxidative, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, antitumor, and neuroprotective effects (Bahri et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2018; Park et al., 2019; Allegra et al., 2020; Zeb, 2020; Hasei et al., 2021; Habtemariam, 2023; Mirza et al., 2023). Carnosic acid has been used in human food to prevent fat oxidation (Hasei et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021), so its safety has been confirmed. In the present study, carnosic acid was used as a feed additive for broilers in vivo, using low (20 mg/kg), middle (40 mg/kg), and high (80 mg/kg) dosages. The broilers were further treated with lipopolysaccharide (LPS). Researches have confirmed that LPS can cause both oxidative stress and inflammation in vivo and in vitro, leading to large number of free radicals and the excessive accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) within the mitochondria in the animal's body (Zhou et al., 2010; Xu et al., 2022). In the present study, LPS was used to establish the damage model, allowing us to investigate which dosage of CA would be the best to relieve oxidative stress and inflammation damage in the intestine and liver of broilers.

Oxidative stress (OS) and inflammation could cause poultry intestinal injury, which could release several toxic items, such as LPS, bacteria, and other metabolic substances into the blood circulation. In particular, these toxins can reach the liver because of enterohepatic circulation, and may cause damage to several organs in poultry. When animal under oxidative stress, several antioxidative enzymes, such as intestinal superoxide dismutase (SOD) (Zhuang et al., 2019), glutathione peroxidase (GSH-Px) (Malyar et al., 2021) and the total antioxidant capacity (T-AOC) decrease in response to overloaded ROS. Tissue damage indicators, such as malondialdehyde (MDA) increased in the animal serum because of cellular damage. In addition, inflammatory factors, such as interleukin-1β (IL-1β) (Dinić et al., 2018), IL-4, and IL-6 (Liu et al., 2020), and tumor necrosis factor (TNF-α), further induce more severe mucosal injury (Tian et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020). Previous researches confirmed that carnosic acid exert its protective effects through multiple mechanisms, which involve oxidative stress and inflammation signaling pathways (Pérez-Fons et al., 2010; Xia et al., 2017). Therefore, in the present study, the above indicators were detected primarily in chicken serum and organs to determine CA's protective role.

Furthermore, inflammation and oxidative stress-related signaling pathways, such as the nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) (Xiang et al., 2013) and cell defense system Kelch like ECH associated protein 1 (Keap1)/NFE2 like bZIP transcription factor 2 (Nrf2) pathways were also detected in different treatment groups of broilers in vivo. To maintain the shape of the intestinal tract, gap junction proteins play a vital role in maintaining physiological functions in animals; therefore, in the present study, connexin (Cx) was also detected (Meşe et al., 2007).

When animals suffer different stresses, heat shock proteins (HSP) are widely induced to exert a protective role (Nawaz et al., 2021; Balakrishnan et al., 2023). Heat shock protein 70 (HSP70, molecular weight: 70 kD) functions to relieve oxidative stress-induced protein damage by inhibiting the aggregation of oxidized proteins or promoting their degradation. HSP70 Not only inducing the expression of key antioxidant enzymes like SOD1 and catalase (CAT) (Polla et al., 1996; Guo et al., 2007; Khomenko et al., 2007; Kalmar and Greensmith, 2009; Watanabe et al., 2014; Lubkowska et al., 2023; Scordino et al., 2023), but also can suppress the activation of the TNF-α-induced mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling pathway and NF-κB transcriptional activation (Luo et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2017; Lyu et al., 2020; Vostakolaei et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021). Heat shock protein60 (HSP60) also widely present in cellular mitochondria and is closely associated with oxidative stress damage (Lee et al., 2009; Min et al., 2022). Therefore, in the present study, to investigate CA's protective function from different aspects, the expression and location of HSP70 and HSP60 and their associated pathway proteins, such as MAPK and extracellular regulated kinase (ERK), were also detected.

In summary, the main idea of this study was to investigate whether CA can exert protective effects by alleviating LPS-induced inflammation and oxidative stress and to reveal its mechanisms of action in alleviating liver and intestinal injuries in broilers.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Experimental Design and Preparation of Test Subjects

For this experiment, 120 male AA broiler chickens, aged 18 d and sourced from Nanjing Tegeili Planting Cooperative (Nanjing, China), were individually weighed upon arrival. They were evenly allocated to cages based on their average weights, with their daily weight being recorded at specific times. The chickens were housed separately in cage-equipped rooms. The temperature was maintained at 27°C ± 3°C for the first week, then lowered to 22°C ± 3°C, while the indoor humidity was kept at 60 to 70%. Natural ventilation was introduced for 30 to 60 min daily, under a 16-h light and 8-h dark cycle, until the sampling day.

All experimental animals in this study were approved by the Ethics Committee of Nanjing Agricultural University.

Carnosic Acid and LPS Treatment

As shown in Table 1, the carnosic acid powder was dissolved in distilled water to prepare a solution, and divided into 3 different concentration doses. After a 1-wk adaptation period of regular feeding, the chicken groups were subjected to oral gavage with CA according to their respective groups. According to Table 1, the chickens were divided into 8 groups for administration. Following 1 wk of CA administration, an intraperitoneal injection of LPS was conducted using a solution created by dissolving LPS powder in phosphate-buffered saline to achieve a concentration of 1 mg/mL, with an injection dosage of 200 mg/kg. Sampling was carried out 12 h after injection.

Sample Collection

After euthanizing the broiler chickens via air vein injection, serum was collected and stored. Subsequently, one portion of organs with consistent size and location was preserved in a −80°C freezer, while another portion was fixed and stored in 10% formalin.

Instruments and Reagents

The 4% paraformaldehyde (22329929) and phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF) (20090716) were both purchased from Biosharp (Hefei, China); Lipopolysaccharides (L2880) and carnosic acid powder were obtained from Sigma (St. Louis, MO); Radioimmunoprecipitation assay lysis buffer (P0013B), primary antibody dilution buffer (P0023A), and protease inhibitor (P1045-2) were purchased from Beyotime Biotechnology.

The microplate reader (Infinite M200 pro) was obtained from Tecan (Männedorf, Switzerland; the microcentrifuge (Sorvall Legend Micro 17R) was from Thermo Fisher Scientific (Waltham, MA).

Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay

The levels of creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB), inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), malondialdehyde (MDA), IL-6, SOD, interleukin-1β (IL-1β), TNF-α, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), and total antioxidant capacity (T-AOC) were determined using Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay. The required assay kits were obtained from Nanjing Jiancheng Institute of Biotechnology (Nanjing, China) and used according to the supplier's instructions.

Hematoxylin and Eosin Staining

After euthanizing the animals via air vein injection, liver tissue samples were collected and processed following established protocols. Fresh specimens, not exceeding 2 mm in thickness and 5 × 5 mm in size, were obtained. These specimens were fixed in 10% formalin for 6 to 12 h, followed by thorough water washing and a series of meticulous dehydration steps. After paraffin embedding, the tissue is sectioned into thin slices, ranging from 3 to 5 μm in thickness. The sections were subsequently stained using hematoxylin and eosin (H&E), enabling observation under an optical microscope. Pathological changes were documented, providing key insights into the experiment's outcomes.

Immunohistochemical Assay

The paraffin-embedded sections underwent deparaffinization by incubating at 60°C for 1 h. After a series of hydration steps, the antigen retrieval was carried out using preheated sodium citrate buffer, followed by 3 × 3 min phosphate-buffered saline-Tween20 washes. To create a hydrophobic barrier, the tissue was circled with an immunohistochemistry pen, then treated with 3% hydrogen peroxide solution. Incubation in a humidified chamber at room temperature for 15 min was followed by 3 × 3 min phosphate-buffered saline-Tween20 washes. Goat serum blocking solution was applied dropwise, followed by a 10-min incubation in a humidified chamber at room temperature. The primary antibody dilution buffer (P0023A, Beyotime Institute of Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) was then applied and incubated overnight at 4°C.

Next day, the sections were incubated with the secondary antibody for 10 min, followed by the dropwise addition of avidin solution. 3,3′-Diaminobenzidine staining and hematoxylin counterstaining were performed, followed by dehydration and the dropwise addition of neutral gum seals after 10 min. Captured images by fluorescence microscope (Imager.A2, Zeiss, Germany) helped interpret the results: brownish-yellow indicated a positive reaction, and blue indicated the nuclei.

Western Blotting

Total proteins were extracted from jejunal tissue using radioimmunoprecipitation assay Lysis Buffer with PMSF (Biyuntian Bio-Technology Co., Shanghai, China). Protein concentrations were determined using a bicinchoninic acid assay kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and unified to the same concentration, added to 25 µL of SDS loading buffer, and denatured at 99°C for 10 min. Then, 5 to 6  μL of the above protein samples were separated using10% SDS-PAGE gel electrophoresis and then transferred to a polyvinylidene fluoride membrane in Tris-glycine buffer containing 20% methanol at 4°C for 90 min. The membrane was blocked using 5% nonfat milk at 37°C for 2 h and then incubated with antiglyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (1:10,000, Abcam, Cambridge, MA), anti-HSP70 (1:1,000, Enzo, Farmingdale, NY), anti-p38 MAPK (anti-P38) (1:1,000, Cell Signaling Technology (CST), Danvers, MA), anti-phospho-P38 (1:2,000, CST), antiextracellular regulated kinase (anti-ERK) (1:1,000, CST), anti-JUN N-Terminal Kinase (anti-JNK) (1:1,000, CST), antiprotein kinase C alpha (PKCα) (1:10,000, Abcam), and antiprotein kinase C beta (PKCβ) (1:1,000, Abcam) antibodies overnight at 4°C. Subsequently, the membranes were incubated with a horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody against rabbit/mouse IgG (1:5,000, ABclonal, Wuhan, China). The protein bands were visualized using an enhanced chemiluminescence kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The protein grayscale values were then determined using ImageJ software (NIH, Bethesda, MD), and the density of each band was normalized to that of its respective loading control. The obtained data were expressed as a ratio of the intensity of the protein in the experimental group to that of the corresponding protein in the control group.

Statistical Analysis and Interpretation of Results

Data analysis and graphical representation were performed using Image J and Graph Prism 9.0 software (GraphPad Inc., La Jolla, CA). The results are presented as the mean ± standard error. Intergroup differences were assessed using 1-way analysis of variance, followed by Tukey's post hoc test for pairwise comparisons. Statistical significance was defined at P < 0.05. Quantitative analysis of protein band grayscale in the Western blotting assays was conducted using Image J software. In comparison with the control group, * means a statistically significant difference (P < 0.05), ** indicates a highly significant difference (P < 0.01), *** indicates a very highly significant difference (P < 0.001), and **** indicates an extremely highly significant difference (P < 0.0001).

Effect of Carnosic Acid on Serum Markers of LPS-Induced Damage

After 1 wk of carnosic acid supplementation, broiler chickens were subjected to LPS intraperitoneal injection and euthanized 12 h later. Blood was collected, centrifuged to obtain serum, which was then used for ELISA to assess the changes in the levels of proinflammatory cytokines, including IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β; inflammation-related factor iNOS; antioxidants SOD and T-AOC; and CK-MB, MDA, LDH, and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) (also known as glutamic oxaloacetic transaminase (GOT) in the serum.

As shown in Figure 1, compared with the control group, the LPS-treated group significantly induced the expression of TNF-α and IL-1β (P < 0.01), while the level of IL-6 showed elevated trends without statistical significance. Carnosic acid intervention led to downregulation of IL-6, TNF-α, and IL-1β levels (P < 0.05), with the middle-dose CA group showing the most significant reduction (P < 0.01) and an extremely highly significant reduction in the case of TNF-α (P < 0.0001).

Under LPS stimulation, the level of iNOS was also significantly increased compared with that in the control group (P < 0.01), while no statistically significant differences were observed for SOD and T-AOC. However, after intervention with CA, the levels of iNOS, SOD, and T-AOC were all downregulated (P < 0.05), with all 3 dose groups significantly reducing iNOS (P < 0.01). The middle-dose CA group exhibited a significant increase in SOD and T-AOC levels (P < 0.05).

Compared with the control group, all carnosic acid groups demonstrated an extremely highly significant reduction in MDA levels (P < 0.0001), whereas the LPS group did not show statistical differences. Both the moderate and high doses of CA significantly attenuated the elevated levels induced by LPS (P < 0.0001).

Both creatine kinase-MB and aspartate aminotransferase exhibited an increasing trend under LPS induction compared with those in the control group (P < 0.05), while lactate dehydrogenase showed some elevation without reaching statistical significance. When compared with the LPS group, the moderate and high doses of CA intervention significantly decreased the LDH levels (P < 0.05), with the moderate dose of CA exhibiting the most pronounced downregulation for CK-MB (P < 0.05) and high-dose CA showing a highly significant reduction in AST (P < 0.01).

In summary, these results suggest that carnosic acid has the ability to reduce the levels of inflammation-related factors induced by LPS sensitization, such as IL-6 and IL-1β, while also increasing the content of antioxidant substances like SOD and T-AOC.

Protective Effect of Carnosic Acid on Lipopolysaccharides-Induced Liver Damage in Chickens

Results of Hematoxylin and Eosin Staining of Chicken Liver

After harvesting chicken liver tissues, hematoxylin and eosin staining was performed, as shown in Figure 2. In comparison with the control group, the LPS group exhibited more pronounced pathological changes: there was inflammatory cell infiltration in the portal vein area, disarrayed arrangement of hepatic cell cords, and sinusoidal dilation filled with red blood cells. Hepatocytes appeared enlarged, with condensed nuclei, irregular distribution, and some showed granular degeneration and vacuolar degeneration in the cytoplasm, accompanied by evident necrotic foci. In the groups treated with low or high doses of CA alone, a small amount of blood was observed in the hepatic sinusoids; however, the overall pathological changes were less prominent. In comparison with the LPS group, the groups treated with CA displayed fewer pathological symptoms. Among them, the middle-dose group exhibited relatively uniform distribution of hepatocytes with no noticeable necrosis or inflammatory cell infiltration.

Localization of Heat Shock and Inflammatory Pathway Proteins in Chicken Liver Tissues Under Carnosic Acid Intervention

We established an oxidative stress and inflammation model in broiler chickens via LPS induction, followed by a week-long administration of CA, and subsequent intraperitoneal LPS injection for 12 h, after which the chickens were humanely euthanized, and liver tissues were collected. Immunohistochemical methods were employed to assess alterations in the levels and locations of HSP60, HSP70, and Nrf2 proteins in chicken liver tissues. The results revealed that both experimental and control groups expressed HSP60, HSP70, and Nrf2. The positive signals for HSP60 and HSP70 were mainly located in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes, with minimal nuclear expression, whereas Nrf2 was predominantly found in the cytoplasm under normal conditions.

In the middle- and high-dose carnosic acid groups (Figure 2), the levels of HSP60 and HSP70 were significantly higher compared with those in the control group. In areas with pronounced degeneration and necrosis in the LPS group, the positive signals for HSP60 and HSP70 within liver hepatocytes increased. Following treatment with moderate-dose CA, the extent of damage was notably mitigated, and the expression of HSP60 and HSP70 within liver cells decreased. This suggests that under normal circumstances, moderate and high doses of CA can elevate the levels of HSP60 and HSP70 in chicken liver tissues, providing a protective effect. Upon lipopolysaccharides stimulation, HSP60 and HSP70 levels were notably elevated, which were subsequently reduced by CA intervention.

In the normal control group, NFE2 like bZIP transcription factor 2 positive signals (Figure 2) exhibited a cytoplasmic localization. However, the administration of CA alone had an insignificant impact on Nrf2 expression in chicken liver cells, with only slight nuclear entry of Nrf2 protein observed in the middle-dose CA group. After lipopolysaccharides treatment, NFE2 like bZIP transcription factor 2 was abundantly distributed in the cell nucleus, showing a significant increase in expression. In the LPS+CA group, the nuclear localization of Nrf2 was remarkably enhanced. Notably, in the moderate-dose group, both CA treatment and LPS+CA treatment induced a substantial expansion of Nrf2 protein translocation into the nucleus, accompanied by increased expression.

Modulation of Heat Shock Proteins and Inflammation-Related Pathways by Carnosic Acid in Lipopolysaccharides-Induced Oxidative Stress and Inflammation Model

To further investigate the inhibitory effects of CA on LPS-induced inflammation and oxidative stress in broiler chickens, we examined the levels of HSP60, HSP70, P38 MAPK (P38), extracellular regulated kinase (ERK), and c-Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK). As illustrated in Figure 3, compared with that in the control group, high doses of CA increased the levels of HSP70, P38, and ERK, whereas LPS significantly upregulates the levels of HSP70, P38, and ERK. By contrast, intervention with CA after LPS administration led to a significant reduction in the levels of HSP70, P38, and ERK (P < 0.05). Statistical analysis of grayscale values of ERK, HSP60, and JNK revealed significant interindividual variations among the groups, with no significant differences in data discrepancies (P > 0.05).

Protective Effect of Carnosic Acid on Lipopolysaccharides-Induced Intestinal Damage in Chickens

Results of Hematoxylin and Eosin Staining of Chicken Intestinal Tissue

The results of hematoxylin and eosin staining revealed that in the control group of chickens, the intestinal structure appeared intact with no edema. This is shown in Figure 4, villi were relatively long, well-aligned, and devoid of shedding, and there was no evidence of inflammatory cell infiltration. In contrast, the LPS-treated group exhibited distorted villus morphology with loss of the normal structure in the crypts. Villus length was reduced, and significant shedding and fracturing were observed, together with edema in the lamina propria, loosening of the submucosal layer, and structural laxity in the muscularis externa. Cellular degeneration and necrosis were evident, along with inflammatory cell infiltration.

However, intervention with CA in the LPS-exposed group alleviated the damage caused by LPS. Cells regained their normal morphology, and the inflammatory response was notably reduced. Moreover, both villus height and crypt depth showed significant improvement.

Localization of Heat Shock and Inflammatory Pathway Proteins in Chicken Intestinal Tissues Under Carnosic Acid Intervention

Immunohistochemical analysis was employed to assess the levels and locations of HSP60, HSP70, CD45, and connexin in chicken intestinal tissues. The results revealed that HSP60 was expressed in both control and experimental groups, as depicted in Figure 5. Administration of high-dose CA significantly enhanced the level of HSP60, with notable increases observed in both the cytoplasm and nucleus. In comparison, the LPS group exhibited increased HSP60 expression compared to the control group, but this effect was notably mitigated by moderate-dose CA intervention.

Figure 5 illustrates that HSP70 levels were relatively low in the control group intestines. However, carnosic acid administration led to a substantial increase in HSP70 levels. Lipopolysaccharides significantly upregulated HSP70 expression, which was decreased most prominently by moderate-dose CA intervention. Regarding connexin expression, as shown in Figure 5, both the control and LPS groups demonstrated limited expression of connexin. Nevertheless, both low-dose and moderate-dose CA administration induced an upregulation in Connexin levels to a certain extent. CD45 is widely present in inflammatory cells, and there was no positive signal in the control group under normal conditions. In the group administered with CA through gavage, the level of CD45 was low. In contrast, the LPS group, characterized by severe inflammatory reactions and oxidative stress, exhibited a significant increase in CD45 levels, as an indicator of inflammatory cells. However, the LPS-induced increase in CD45 was reduced by moderate-dose CA intervention, indicating its potential to alleviate intestinal inflammatory responses.

Exploring the Inhibitory Effects of Carnosic Acid on Lipopolysaccharides-Induced Inflammation and Oxidative Stress: Protein Expression result of Heat Shock Protein60, Heat Shock Protein70, P38 Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinases, and Extracellular Regulated Kinase

To further investigate the inhibitory effects of CA on LPS-induced inflammation and oxidative stress in broiler chickens, we examined the levels of HSP60, HSP70, P38, and ERK. As depicted in Figure 6, compared with that in the control group, the administration of CA alone did not induce a significant change in the levels of HSP60, HSP70, P38, and ERK (P > 0.05). Upon Lipopolysaccharides stimulation, there was a significant upregulation of HSP70 and HSP60 levels (P < 0.0001), and a nonsignificant increase in P38 and ERK levels (P > 0.05). Following carnosic acid intervention in the low-, medium-, and high-dose groups, the levels of HSP70 and HSP60 proteins were significantly downregulated (P < 0.0001). The low, medium, and high doses of CA also significantly suppressed the LPS-induced increase in P38 levels (P < 0.01), with the medium-dose group showing particularly significant attenuation of the LPS-induced ERK upregulation (P < 0.01).

DISCUSSION

According to our results, carnosic acid had no significant effect on the body weight of the broilers. Elevated levels of LPS in the liver led to hepatocellular injury, prompting the release of various proinflammatory factors from hepatic macrophages. Consequently, the levels of TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 increased. Carnosic acid administration reversed the elevation of inflammatory cytokine levels induced by LPS, with the most significant downregulation being observed in the group treated with the medium dose of CA. P38 mitogen-activated protein kinases participates in inflammatory signal transduction during the progression of atherosclerosis, regulating cell adhesion molecules and the production of inflammatory mediators such as IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α (Reustle and Torzewski, 2018), and enhances vascular sensitivity to vascular endothelial growth factor and ROS, thus activating the NF-κB pathway. Our results showed that the group receiving the middle dose of CA experienced a significant increase in ERK levels (P < 0.05). Upon CA intervention under LPS stimulation, extracellular regulated kinase expression exhibited a notable downtrend, with the low-dose group showing the most significant effect (P < 0.05). P38 mitogen-activated protein kinases was significantly elevated under LPS treatment compared with that in the control group (P < 0.01). Following intervention with various doses of CA, the P38 levels were significantly downregulated (P < 0.05), with the low-dose group exhibiting the most pronounced decrease (P < 0.01). CD45 is widely present in inflammatory cells and is commonly used as an inflammatory cell marker to indicate the extent of tissue damage caused by inflammation (Rogers et al., 2007). In this study, LPS significantly upregulated CD45 levels, while the intermediate-dose group of CA showed downregulated levels. When compared with the control group, the administration of CA alone also elevated CD45 levels, albeit to a lesser extent. This might be attributed to some degree of inflammatory damage caused by gavage or stress.

Therefore, carnosic acid could ease inflammation damage, possibly by inhibiting LPS-induced P38, ERK, and JNK activities, which induce cytokine expression.

Oxidative stress indicators were also investigated. Inducible nitric oxide synthase was detected in chicken serum, and after CA treatment in the low-, moderate-, and high-dose CA groups, iNOS levels were lower than in the LPS treatment group, as were MDA levels. Moreover, the levels of antioxidants, such as T-AOC and SOD, were also higher in the moderate-dose CA group, indicating that CA can enhance the body's antioxidative capacity. Furthermore, a comparative analysis revealed that the most pronounced effect was observed at a dosage of 40 mg/kg. The abundance of MDA is commonly employed to gauge the extent of lipid peroxidation within the body, indirectly reflecting the degree of tissue cell damage, and is often used in conjunction with SOD to assess tissue or cellular impairment. Other tissue or cellular damage indicators, such as CK-MB and AST, were also detected. Creatine kinase-MB is a commonly used molecular marker for myocardial damage, with higher serum CK-MB concentrations indicating more severe myocardial injury (Nigam, 2007; Chen and Deng, 2019). Aspartate aminotransferase is a critical enzyme within liver cells that is also present in the serum. Consistent with the results of oxidative damage markers, under LPS induction, the levels of CK-MB and AST increased significantly. Following intervention with CA, the levels of CK-MB and AST were both downregulated, indicating CA's protective role. Our analysis of pathological lesions further confirmed the protective role of CA. Hepatic tissue hematoxylin and eosin staining demonstrated that CA significantly reduced the inflammatory cell infiltration induced by LPS in the liver and alleviated various pathological damages to hepatocytes.

NFE2 like bZIP transcription factor 2 is a key protein that regulates oxidative stress. The activation of Nrf2 can protect the body from various inflammatory diseases, such as acute lung injury and acute liver failure (Lei et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018). Herein, we examined the level and location of Nrf2, which is normally expressed in the cytoplasm. However, LPS induced the translocation of Nrf2 into the nucleus. Additionally, CA further promoted the nuclear translocation of Nrf2 and suppressed LPS-induced inflammatory damage by upregulating the levels of antioxidant enzymes. The middle and high dosage groups of CA exhibited the most pronounced upregulation of Nrf2 levels.

The increasing evidence suggests that HSP60 and HSP70 can exert cellular protective functions (Althnaian et al., 2019; Önay Uçar et al., 2023). The overexpression of HSP70 could alleviate the extent of lesions and limit tissue damage within the lesions in a mouse model of brain ischemia (Olsson et al., 2004). In the present study, the levels of HSP60 and HSP70 in the liver were upregulated upon LPS induction, reflecting their roles as protective molecules to combat oxidative stress and inflammatory responses within the body. However, under the influence of CA, the levels of HSP60 and HSP70 both declined. This observation might indicate that the protective mechanism of CA activates only a portion of HSP60 and HSP70, thus restoring their expression to normal levels. Our findings showed that the levels of HSP60 and HSP70 in the intestines also increased significantly after LPS stimulation, underscoring their protective roles in countering intestinal damage. After carnosic acid intervention, the levels of both proteins were notably downregulated, indirectly supporting the protective function of CA on the intestines. We hypothesized that carnosic acid achieves its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects through mechanisms such as enhancing the activity of antioxidant enzymes and inhibiting the production of inflammatory factors. Therefore, further exploration of CA's protective mechanisms on other organs is warranted.

The integrity of the intestinal epithelium plays a crucial role in maintaining the gut barrier function. The intestinal mucosa is essential to resist infections and regulate immune responses triggered by external antigens. Any alterations in the integrity or function of the intestinal epithelium can have adverse effects on other organs. In this experiment, lipopolysaccharides induction led to pathological changes in intestinal epithelial cells, including degeneration, necrosis, inflammatory cell infiltration, and villous shedding. However, intervention with CA significantly suppressed the increased levels of P38 and ERK induced by LPS, aligning with the trends observed in the liver.

In this study, the levels of intestinal connexin were assessed. The addition of CA decreased the levels of P38 and ERK, and inhibited the upregulation of connexin induced by LPS. Among these effects, the action of CA in the high-dose group was most pronounced. However, the precise mechanisms underlying these interactions and the optimal dosage of CA application require further investigation.

Although the exact mechanisms underlying the protective effects of CA at the optimal dosage have not been completely determined, it is evident that poultry supplemented with an intermediate dose of CA exhibited enhanced resistance against LPS-induced oxidative stress and inflammatory injury. This provides a scientific basis for the development of CA and the selection of an appropriate dosage in poultry feed.

CONCLUSIONS

This study primarily investigated the protective mechanisms of CA against oxidative stress and inflammatory responses induced by LPS in the intestines and liver of poultry. The results indicated that the 3 doses of CA had no significant impact on the body weight of broiler chickens. Serum analysis suggested that the moderate dose of CA had the ability to enhance the body's antioxidative capacity and reduce oxidative stress and associated inflammatory factors.

When exploring the protective effects of CA on LPS-induced liver injury in chickens, we observed that CA modulated the levels of proteins such as HSP60, HSP70, and P38 in the liver. This provides a theoretical foundation to further investigate the expression of related proteins in other organs. Carnosic acid was found to protect against LPS-induced chicken intestinal damage, affecting the levels and locations of proteins including HSP60, HSP70, Nrf2, and P38 in the intestines. Notably, carnosic acid exhibited regulatory effects on LPS-induced inflammatory markers, such as CD45 and connexin, in the intestines. Taken together, these findings underscore the considerable potential of CA to alleviate oxidative stress and inflammatory injuries induced by LPS within the poultry body.
