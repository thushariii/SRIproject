Abstract

The genome of SARS-CoV-2 encodes for a helicase (nsp13) that is essential for viral replication and highly conserved across related viruses, making it an attractive antiviral target. Here we use nanopore tweezers, a high-resolution single-molecule technique, to gain detailed insight into how nsp13 turns ATP-hydrolysis into directed motion along nucleic acid strands. We measured nsp13 both as it translocates along single-stranded DNA or unwinds double-stranded DNA. Our data reveal nsp13’s single-nucleotide steps, translocating at ∼1000 nt/s or unwinding at ∼100 bp/s. Nanopore tweezers’ high spatiotemporal resolution enables detailed kinetic analysis of nsp13 motion. As a proof-of-principle for inhibition studies, we observed nsp13’s motion in the presence of the ATPase inhibitor ATPγS. We construct a detailed picture of inhibition in which ATPγS has multiple mechanisms of inhibition. The dominant mechanism of inhibition depends on the application of assisting force. This lays the groundwork for future single-molecule inhibition studies with viral helicases.

INTRODUCTION

The severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) responsible for the COVID-19 pandemic has spurred large-scale efforts to develop antiviral drugs against its viral replication cycle (1). Vaccines and new antiviral treatments such as Paxlovid, have been revolutionary, still COVID-19 claims thousands of lives per day. Further, vaccines are less effective for the immunocompromised, and Paxlovid cannot be taken by patients with kidney disease, even though this population is at higher risk of developing COVID-19 complications. Additional methods of prevention and treatment are needed. Taking a broader view, there have been three coronaviral outbreaks in the last 20 years (SARS in 2001, MERS in 2012 and SARS-2 in 2019). To head off the next pandemic, humanity needs better understanding of how coronaviruses function and needs to develop a broad arsenal of antiviral treatments.

Several SARS-CoV-2 proteins are attractive drug targets as they are essential for viral replication, key among them are non-structural protein 5 (nsp5), a viral protease; nsp12, the RNA dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp); and nsp13, an RNA helicase (2). Antivirals (Paxlovid, Molnupiravir and Remdesivir) currently approved to treat SARS-CoV-2 aim to inhibit viral replication by targeting nsp5 or nsp12, however, there are no presently approved antivirals which target nsp13. Studies of related coronaviruses (3) and other members of the order Nidovirales (4) have shown that the virus cannot replicate without a functional helicase. For this reason, nsp13 has been identified as a target for antiviral intervention. Moreover, unlike structural proteins, the amino acid sequence of nsp13 is one of the most conserved among related coronavirus species (e.g. SARS- and MERS-Nsp13) and variants of SARS-CoV-2 (including Omicron (5)). Thus, nsp13-targeting antivirals have the potential to be broad spectrum drugs that could address future coronaviral outbreaks as well. Since early in the pandemic, several compounds have been put forward as potentially inhibitory of nsp13 (6–12), however, many have high IC50, low solubility, and/or the potential for significant side effects. Learning more about how nsp13 functions could pave the way for new inhibitory molecules capable of disrupting nsp13 activity.

Based on structure and biochemical studies, nsp13 has been classified as a superfamily (SF) 1B helicase and an inchworm mechanism for translocation has been proposed (13–15). In this mechanism, 2A and 1A domains each bind nucleic acids (NA) in a 5′ → 3′ orientation respectively (13). Binding of a nucleotide-triphosphate, e.g. ATP, is followed by 2A and 1A domains coming together in a ‘closed’ conformation. During this conformational change, domain 2A advances a distance of one nucleotide in the 3′-direction along the NA strand. ATP hydrolysis then triggers a conformational change from ‘closed’ to ‘open,’ causing domain 1A to slide forward by a single nucleotide. This is followed by release of ADP and an inorganic phosphate to complete the cycle. Nsp13 can unwind both dsDNA and dsRNA in a 5′ → 3′ direction and is capable of hydrolyzing all deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs) and ribonucleoside triphosphates (NTPs) (16–19). Despite the RNA genome of SARS-CoV-2, structural data indicate that the nsp13 - nucleic-acid interactions are non-specific towards the ribose or nucleobase (13), and biochemical data show that the unwinding speed for dsDNA and dsRNA to be either similar or slightly faster for dsDNA (16,18,19). Reported unwinding speeds range from ∼50–200 bp/s. On its own, nsp13 appears to be a relatively weak helicase with relatively low processivity during unwinding (16,19). However, its processivity and unwinding speed are enhanced via destabilization of the DNA duplex using optical tweezers and interactions with RdRp (14,16,19). Translocation along ssNA is suspected since that is the mechanism by which nsp13 is thought to unwind NA duplexes but has not yet been observed.

Nucleic acid unwinding involves a series of chemical and physical steps each consisting of a number of interactions between several different molecules. Each of these interactions and steps is an opportunity for inhibition, however activity assays with Boolean output (it did or did not unwind) are not well suited to determine mechanism. Single-molecule techniques offer an attractive alternative as they allow for direct observation of individual molecules in action but the speed at which nsp13 walks along its NA substrate in addition to its small step size puts the fundamental steps of the nsp13 helicase beyond the resolution of traditional single-molecule techniques. Such resolution would enable detailed kinetic analysis of the underlying steps of nsp13’s inchworm mechanism and could also shed light on how inhibitory molecules affect its inner workings. We have developed a nanopore-based technique known as single-molecule picometer-resolution nanopore tweezers (SPRNT), which can measure steps of helicases and other molecular motors at high spatiotemporal resolution (20,21). Here we apply SPRNT to nsp13 of SARS-CoV-2 to reveal the detailed mechanism of nsp13’s motion on DNA and we demonstrate how SPRNT can be used to determine the mechanism of action of a helicase inhibitor.

In SPRNT, a single Mycobacterium smegmatis porin A (MspA) nanopore within a phospholipid bilayer serves as the sole electrical connection between two electrolyte-containing wells (Figure ​(Figure1A).1A). A voltage applied across this membrane causes an ion current to flow through the nanopore. The electric field of the nanopore also draws negatively-charged NA through the pore, blocking the ion current. Different NA bases within the nanopore's constriction cause unique ion current blockages which can be decoded into NA sequence as in nanopore sequencing (22). If a helicase molecule is bound to the captured NA strand, the helicase comes to rest on the outer-edge of the entry into the nanopore (henceforth, referred to as pore-rim) as the helicase's large size prohibits entry into the pore. From this point onward, the helicase steps along the NA causing motion of the NA through the nanopore, resulting in a sequence of ion current steps as the NA sequence in the pore changes. These ion current steps resolve single-nucleotide steps on sub-millisecond time scales, making it possible to observe the details of helicase motion along NA, while simultaneously determining the NA sequence of the substrate in the helicase. The force SPRNT applies on the enzyme/NA complex is proportional to the applied voltage. Depending on which end of the NA is threaded into the pore, this force can either oppose or assist the helicase motion.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

DNA constructs

DNA oligonucleotides were synthesized at Stanford University Protein and Nucleic Acid Facility and purified at their facility using Poly-Pak cartridges. The oligo sequences are shown schematically in Fig. 1B; exact sequences are in Supplementary Table 1. For nsp13 unwinding assays, DNA template and complement were annealed at relative molar concentrations of either 1:1.2 or 1:10 respectively.

Proteins

The M2-NNN-MspA (23) protein was custom ordered from GenScript. M2-NNN-MspA used in nanopore experiments is identical to wild-type MspA (AC: {"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"CAB56052.1","term_id":"5912275","term_text":"CAB56052.1"}}CAB56052.1) except for the following mutations: D90N/D91N/D93N/D118R/E139K/D134R. Nsp13 from SARS-CoV-2 (AC: {"type":"entrez-nucleotide","attrs":{"text":"MT121215.1","term_id":"1819735426","term_text":"MT121215.1"}}MT121215.1) was expressed and purified as reported previously (16).

Pore establishment

A single MspA nanopore was established in a bilayer as described previously (24). Briefly, a lipid bilayer is formed with a mixture of 1,2-di-O-phytanyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (Avanti Polar Lipids, SKU: 999984C) and n-hexadecane across a 20 μm diameter aperture that separates two ∼65 μl chambers (cisand trans) containing our operating buffers. A potential of 180 mV (unless otherwise specified for force titration experiments) is applied using an Axopatch 200B amplifier (Axon Instruments) and a National Instruments PCI-6251 DAQ.

Nanopore experiments

All experiments were run at asymmetric salt, cis 20 mM KCl, with 20 mM HEPES at pH 7.5, 5 mM MgCl2 and 1 mM TCEP. The trans buffer 500 mM KCl, 20 mM HEPES at pH 7.5. Cis buffer also contained ATP, ADP, or ATPγS at variable concentrations. Nsp13 was preincubated with the DNA construct in the absence of ATP by incubating at RT for 30 min at a concentration of 4 μM and 0.4 μM respectively in 124 mM KCl, 20 mM Hepes pH 7.5, 5 mM MgCl2 and 1 mM TCEP. Unwinding and translocation experiments were initiated by adding 1μl of this incubation to this cis well with nsp13 and DNA at final concentrations of 100nM and 10 nM, respectively. This final concentration is below Kd for formation of nsp13-DNA complexes, so we anticipate that only one nsp13 is bound to each DNA. In support of this, the vast majority of DNA strands that are pulled into the pore have no nsp13 bound. Cis buffer was reperfused every 5 min followed by readditon of nsp13-DNA incubation to maintain constant concentrations of ATP and prevent bulk accumulation of ADP and DNA. All experiments were recorded at RT (23 ± 1°C), with a bias of +180 mV (unless noted otherwise). ATP and TCEP was ordered from Sigma Aldrich and ATPγS were ordered from Sigma Aldrich or Tocris.

Data acquisition and analysis

Data were acquired with a sampling rate of 50 kHz and filtered at 10 kHz. Enzyme events were detected via thresholding: an event starts when ion current drops <80% of the open pore current and ends after the ion current has returned above 94% of the open pore current for more than eight datapoints. Candidate events are then evaluated by hand for processive ion-current leveling as in Figure ​Figure1C,1C, ​,D.D. A subset of these events with clear ion-current transitions was used to create an ion-current consensus (Figure ​(Figure1E).1E). By comparing this consensus to the ion currents expected for the template DNA strand, ion currents can be associated with an enzyme's sequence-specific location along the DNA strand (20,21,25). Ion current steps within each event were found automatically using a point-by-point alignment of raw ion-current traces to a consensus of ion current levels (Supplementary Figure S5, and associated discussion). Event counts and statistics are summarized in Supplementary Table 2.

Alignment of nanopore reads to map enzyme position

We used a modified dynamic programing algorithm similar to those we have used in the past (26). Because the ion current levels measured during SPRNT are known beforehand, the previously determined consensus of current levels (Figure ​(Figure1E)1E) can be used to detect the most likely timepoint for a step transition. The key innovation here is that instead of agnostically detecting steps in the data which are then aligned to the known consensus, all datapoints from the nanopore read are aligned to the known consensus levels. This enables detection of short-lived states which are below the 8-datapoint minimum typically used. Additionally, fewer step-types are allowed, only single forwards steps, single backwards steps, hold and ‘catch’ are allowed. Step penalties are also set to heavily favor hold, i.e. no change in position. As in other dynamic programming approaches to alignment, tuning of step penalties based on their actual probability of observation produces the best alignments. i.e. step penalties should reflect the per-datapoint likelihood of occurrence for hold, step, back, or catch. Hold is roughly 100x more likely than forward and 1000x more likely than back. Step penalties are simply the log probability of occurrence. However, the alignment still performs well on steps far shorter than the average step duration. This can be attributed to the relatively high signal-to-noise ratio of the data. There is really only one path through the similarity matrix which does not step through a region of cool colors given the allowed stepping rules. Alignment algorithms such as this provide a wholistic alignment of the data. The alignment path passing through one region of the similarity matrix requires that it can continue on through the subsequent regions. For example, a datapoint at 14 ms into the event matches reasonably well with consensus levels 13, 19, 24, 34, but is aligned to consensus level 13 not only because it matches well, but also because all the other datapoints before and after it match the other surrounding levels. Similar alignments are regularly used for biological sequence alignment such as alignment of DNA sequences and is guaranteed to find the optimal alignment of a read, X, to a reference, R, for the given set of rules (27). The ‘catch’ state is used by the algorithm to traverse transient datapoints that are otherwise inconsistent with the model and would otherwise poison some alignments. An example alignment is schematically shown in Figure ​Figure2.2. Initially, all datapoints within a read (top) are compared against the known progression of ion current levels which occur as the given DNA strand is passed through the nanopore (left). The so-called ‘similarity matrix’ (Figure ​(Figure2A)2A) represents the match score between each datapoint in the read and levels within the consensus (yellow = strong match, blue = poor match). Score values are calculated as Sn, m = –0.5 × ln(2π × V) + (Xm – Rn)/V, where V is the average current variance, Xm is the mth datapoint and Rn is the nth consensus current level. Alignment of the currents in X to the consensus values of R (shown in red), uses the similarity matrix along with the allowed step types shown in Figure ​Figure2B.2B. Steps within the alignment can then be used to determine the most likely location of an ion-current step (top: gray dashed lines) and to locate ion current levels within the raw ion current trace (top: black lines). Within the similarity matrix, an alignment would appear as a diagonal streak of yellow dots. For more detail on dynamic programming alignment algorithms see refs. (25–27). Automatic alignments performed in this way can then be briefly checked by eye before incorporation into the combined dataset. Some alignments are corrected manually using a custom GUI.

RESULTS

Because nsp13 is known to unwind DNA (16,19) and has a higher affinity for DNA (16), we designed a DNA substrate containing a 37-nucleotide pseudorandom sequence flanked by 30-nt poly-T overhangs on the 3′- and 5′ ends (Figure ​(Figure1B).1B). A cholesterol tag at the 3′ end of the DNA strand was used to increase the DNA concentration near the bilayer, thereby increasing the rate of capture into the pore. The DNA base sequence of the pseudorandom section was chosen to have varied sequence context and easily distinguishable ion current levels (Supplementary Figure S1). A 32-nucleotide complementary strand was hybridized to this strand for dsDNA experiments (Figure ​(Figure1B,1B, Supplementary Table S1, Supplementary Figure S2, Materials and Methods). Nsp13 loads onto the 5′ tail near the dsDNA-ssDNA junction and the free 5′ end of the DNA then threads into the nanopore (Figure ​(Figure1A1A and Supplementary Figure S3). We recorded a total of 2413 individual nsp13 translocation and unwinding events and a total of 27641 helicase steps (Table S2). Both ssDNA and dsDNA templates reveal 1-nt steps in a 5′ → 3′ direction with significantly slower nsp13 motion on dsDNA (Figures ​(Figures1C,1C, ​,D,D, Supplementary Figures S1, S2). At saturating [ATP] and ∼36 pN assisting force, we observe nsp13 ssDNA translocation speed of 1140 ± 40 nt/s (mean ± s.e.m.) and an average dsDNA unwinding speed of 120 ± 10 bp/s (mean ± s.e.m.) (Supplementary Figure S4). In both translocation and unwinding experiments, individual helicases exhibited a broader range of speeds than would be expected from statistical variation alone, suggesting a significant amount of static disorder affecting the helicases’ function. Even during the fast nsp13 translocation of 1500 nt/s, single-nucleotide steps are well resolved (Figure ​(Figure1C).1C). The remarkable speed of nsp13 during translocation presented an analysis challenge as steps within the data were at times difficult to resolve. To fully leverage the resolution available within the SPRNT data, we developed new analytical techniques (Figure ​(Figure2).2). This represents a significant step forward in single-molecule resolution (Supplementary Figure S5).

Our analysis of nanopore tweezers data previously involved automated level-finding using a change-point algorithm followed by alignment of the found levels to the ion-current consensus (20,21,28). In that analysis scheme, level-finding was agnostic to the underlying DNA sequence and detection of levels was achieved by looking for statistically significant steps within the data (29). Here, nsp13’s speed makes such step detection difficult or impossible as individual steps sometimes consist of just a few data points which is insufficient to agnostically determine whether a step took place. However, here the DNA sequence, and therefore the expected progression of ion current levels, is known (Figure ​(Figure1E)1E) and that information can be used to inform step-finding. By adapting our alignment algorithm so that raw data was aligned to the ion-current consensus, we were able to perform sequence-aware step finding (Figure ​(Figure22 and Supplementary Figures S6 and S7). In this scheme, the ion-current datapoints for a given read are aligned to consensus current values. The resulting alignment (Figure ​(Figure2A,2A, red line) yields what is essentially a global best-fit location of the nsp13 helicase as a function of time. We can then extract kinetic parameters such as step dwell-time, the location and frequency of enzyme backsteps, and the location of read start and read terminations. Such automated alignments can be readily verified by-eye, Supplemental Figures S6 and S7 show several representative alignments for ssDNA translocation and dsDNA unwinding, respectively.

Single-nucleotide ssDNA translocation

Figure ​Figure3A3A shows the dwell-time distribution for 4 representative locations along the DNA strand. Intriguingly, nsp13 steps over each of these locations at different rates. Such sequence-dependence has been reported for other helicases (21,30–32). At saturating conditions, the underlying dwell-time distributions of each nucleotide transition are well-fit by a single exponential distribution (Figure ​(Figure3A,3A, Supplementary Figure S8A), indicating that each step during nsp13 translocation is governed by a single rate-limiting step. Since processive helicases couple ATP hydrolysis to DNA translocation, we varied the ATP concentration to probe nsp13 translocation kinetics. The rate of translocation is dependent on [ATP] and is well described by Michaelis–Menten kinetics, with Vmax and KM varying significantly (Figure ​(Figure3B,3B, Supplementary Figure S9) depending on the underlying sequence context within nsp13 (Supplementary Figures S9 and S10). At low ATP, the event rate and enzyme processivity were significantly reduced complicating collection of data in the low ATP regime. Fits of Michaelis–Menten parameters were complicated by the lack of data at low [ATP] which could better constrain KM. The form of the Michaelis–Menten equation means there is significant covariance between KM and Vmax leading to relatively large uncertainties in both fit parameters. Still, error ellipses for these fits show significant differences from position to position and also differ significantly from a fit to a combined dataset in which all datapoints are pooled regardless of enzyme position (Supplementary Figure S10). Nsp13’s [ATP] dependence gives us confidence that we are observing ATP-driven translocation while the large variation in rate of translocation at different DNA positions suggests that the nucleic-acid base identity within nsp13 affects translocation kinetics.

Nsp13 translocation can begin and end anywhere on the template DNA, with 50% of reads terminating after nsp13 walks into the 3′ poly-dT section at the end of the DNA template (Supplementary Figure S11). The true processivity of nsp13, defined as the number of bases translocated before nsp13 disassociates, is estimated by fitting the observed read-lengths at varying [ATP] (Figure ​(Figure3C).3C). Processivity at saturating [ATP] is 26 ± 4 nt and drops to 7.9 ± 0.7 nt (mean ± sem) at [ATP] = 75 μM.

During SPRNT, the electrostatic force (∼36 pN at 180 mV) pulls on the NA via the electric field in the pore constriction and leads to a balancing contact force on the surface of the enzyme where it contacts the pore-rim. With the 5′ end of the NA threaded into the pore, the force on the NA acts to assist the enzyme's motion along the template strand (Figure ​(Figure1A1A and Supplementary Figure S3). Previous nanopore tweezers experiments have seen evidence for a one-dimensional diffusive step in which the applied force, rather than ATP-hydrolysis drives enzyme motion (21). To investigate the effect of assisting force on nsp13 translocation, we varied the force from ∼24 to ∼44 pN at saturating [ATP]. Because we are applying an assisting force, if force were to affect enzyme speed, we would expect the enzyme to speed up with increasing force. However, we found no significant change in average speed (Figure ​(Figure3D3D and Supplementary Figures S12, S13). Since the speed of translocation is independent of force in the range of forces we studied but dependent on [ATP], these data suggest that the observed nsp13 translocation is predominantly on-pathway ATP-hydrolysis-driven motion.

By definition, at saturating [ATP], ATP-binding is not rate-limiting. This, in combination with the lack of apparent force dependence, suggests that either (i) ATP-hydrolysis, (ii) domain 1A motion, or (iii) ADP & phosphate release are rate-limiting for nsp13 translocation. The apparent sequence dependence of this rate-limiting step suggests that it is domain 1A motion, but these observations do not strictly rule out effects of the DNA sequence on the rate of ATP hydrolysis or ADP release.

To further investigate the ADP-release portion of nsp13’s kinetic pathway we also ran the ssDNA assay in the presence of equimolar ATP and ADP ([ATP] = [ADP] = 600 μM). In these conditions, the rate of nsp13 translocation was slowed by approximately a factor of two (Supplementary Figure S14). This is similar to nanopore tweezers results with Hel308 helicase and PcrA helicase where ADP was seen to act as a competitive inhibitor to ATP-binding (21,30). Similar to force-assisting nanopore tweezers experiments with PcrA, ADP causes an increase in step dwell-time. ADP-binding does not appear to be associated with a physical step of the DNA through the pore.

Double stranded DNA unwinding

We next observed nsp13’s ability to unwind dsDNA. The mean dwell-times for duplex unwinding steps are similarly sequence-dependent and on average ∼8-fold longer than that of ssDNA translocation (Figure ​(Figure3E,3E, ​,F).F). This is similar to what would be expected for a ‘passive’ helicase (33,34).

While ssDNA reads start at a broad range of DNA positions, nearly 80% of the dsDNA reads start with the 5′ poly-dT within the pore constriction (Figure ​(Figure3G3G and Supplementary Figure S15). The reads that start within the duplex region are also comparable in speed to dsDNA unwinding (Supplementary Figure S16). This suggests the majority of the reads, including the partial dsDNA reads, are unwinding reads and that limited nsp13 unwinding occurs in the bulk solution.

Compared to ssDNA translocation, the processivity during dsDNA unwinding is shorter at 14 ± 1 nt (mean ± sem) (Figure ​(Figure3H),3H), which is consistent with previous results showing low processivity during unwinding (16). The underlying dwell-time distributions are single-exponential (Supplementary Figure S17), indicating a single, rate-limiting step. Because dsDNA unwinding is so much slower than ssDNA translocation, it stands to reason that the rate-limiting sub-step is the sub-step in which DNA unwinding occurs. Intriguingly, the dwell-times for dsDNA unwinding and ssDNA translocation as a function of DNA position are correlated (Figure ​(Figure3E,3E, Supplementary Figure S18). A similar effect was recently reported with the superfamily 1A helicase PcrA (21). In that case, it was deduced that such a correlation would occur if unwinding and translocation shared the same rate-limiting step. The presence of the DNA duplex acts as an additional barrier to that rate-limiting step. The duplex then modifies the rate-constants for the rate-limiting step rather than adding a new rate-limiting step. Put another way, unwinding happens within the context of enzyme translocation, energy barriers to translocation still affect the rate of unwinding. From this we can conclude that the advance of domain 1A following ATP-hydrolysis is rate-limiting in both translocation and unwinding and that unwinding occurs coincident with this step.

Based on these data and in light of insights from studying other SF1 and SF2 helicases using SPRNT (20,21), we propose an ATP hydrolysis model that places the steps of the inchworm mechanism in the context of the observed transitions during SPRNT (Figure ​(Figure4).4). In SPRNT, only steps in which DNA moves relative to the pore produce observable steps in the data. In the experiment shown here, domain 2A rests on the pore-rim, meaning that only the motion of DNA relative to domain 2A is observable (21). This is also the only kinetic step that can be modified by the applied force because this is the only place within the kinetic pathway in which the applied force affects the balance between two adjacent kinetic states (35). During this step, domain 2A is less tightly bound to the DNA and possibly domain 1A is more tightly bound. Domain 1A and domain 2A are pulled together by the high charge density of ATP in the ATP binding pocket aided by the forces applied by the nanopore: 1) tension along the DNA pulling on domain 1A (caused by the electrophoretic force on the DNA within the pore) and 2) pressure of the pore rim on domain 2A (Supplementary Figure S19). The application of force in this SPRNT experiment biases ATP-bound nsp13 into a closed conformation by increasing the rate of nsp13 closing and decreasing the rate of opening. Other kinetic sub-steps, along the rows of Figure ​Figure4,4, are inferred from the known steps of the inchworm mechanism and details of their kinetics can be inferred from observing changes in kinetics as experimental parameters are varied (30). For example, the presence of equimolar ADP slows nsp13 by encouraging binding of ADP, thereby backtracking nsp13 on its kinetic pathway from state I to state V. This does not result in DNA motion through the pore but does increase the step dwell-time. During steps other than the ATP-bound motion of domain 2A, the DNA tension and normal force of the pore rim on domain 2A are balanced within domain 2A and no DNA motion is observed for these steps (Supplementary Figure S19).

Nsp13 inhibition by ATPγS

There is significant interest in determining mechanisms by which nsp13 can be inhibited by small molecules (7–12). Because SPRNT can resolve individual ATPase turnovers, it can provide detailed insight into the mechanism of action of inhibitory molecules. As a proof of principle, we investigated inhibition of nsp13 by the slowly-hydrolysable ATP-analog ATPγS (36,37) which has been shown to inhibit nsp13 unwinding in bulk biochemical experiments (38). With ATPγS present, the average speed and processivity of nsp13 is significantly reduced relative to the ATP-only condition for translocation (Figure ​(Figure5A5A–E). Commercially available ATPγS regularly contains ADP as a significant contaminant comprising as much as 10% of the reagent, however control experiments with a mixture of 600μM ATP and 600μM ADP showed significantly less inhibition (Supplementary Figure S14B), suggesting that the observed inhibition was indeed due to ATPγS. To determine whether ATPγS alone was sufficient for ssDNA translocation, we attempted an experiment with [ATPγS] = 600 μM alone. We observed translocation with similar speed and processivity to the mixed condition with [ATPγS] = 300 μM and [ATP] = 600 μM (Figure ​(Figure5A,5A, ​,B,B, ​,E).E). This suggests that nsp13 is capable of hydrolyzing ATPγS and that the inhibitory effect of ATPγS saturates above 300μM suggesting that nsp13 has a higher affinity for ATPγS than for ATP. Dwell-time distributions for nsp13 translocation on ssDNA with ATPγS are well described by an exponential distribution suggesting the presence of a single rate-limiting step (Supplementary Figure S20). Intriguingly, the rate constants for each DNA position are different, suggesting that the DNA sequence within the nsp13 affects this rate-limiting step.

We also tested nsp13’s ability to unwind the DNA duplex in the presence of ATPγS. Unwinding speed and processivity are significantly reduced by the presence of ATPγS. Intriguingly, while significantly fewer bases are unwound by an individual nsp13 helicase, the total time spent on the DNA strand is significantly longer (Supplementary Figure S21). In ATPγS-only conditions, nearly 100% of unwinding reads begin with the 5′ poly-dT within the nanopore constriction, significantly greater than was observed in conditions that included ATP. Therefore, in the absence of ATP, there is no significant unwinding in bulk. This suggests that nsp13 can only unwind DNA using ATPγS in the presence of an assisting force provided by the nanopore.

We further investigated the role of this assisting force for ATPγS-associated motion by varying force from 24 to 44 pN at constant [ATP] (600 μM) and [ATPγS] (300 μM). At all applied forces, there was no significant change in average speed (Figure ​(Figure5F)5F) while processivity appeared to be unchanged or to decrease slightly with force (Figure ​(Figure5G).5G). As noted above, we expect the force exerted in this experiment to affect only the kinetic step in which domain 2A advances along the DNA following NTP binding. In sum, we make the following observations: (i) ATPγS slows the progression of nsp13 on ssDNA and dsDNA, (ii) inhibition by ATPγS saturates above ∼300 μM, (iii) there is little force dependence in the range 24–44 pN and (iv) with zero assisting force (bulk) ATPγS is insufficient for unwinding while with assisting force (on the nanopore) nsp13 can use ATPγS to unwind DNA.

Using these observations, we have constructed a model of ATPγS inhibition for nsp13 (Figure ​(Figure6).6). ATPγS inhibits nsp13 unwinding in two ways. First, at zero force, ATPγS inhibits the first conformational change of the inchworm mechanism. This could be either due to reducing the closing-rate of domain 1A and 2A to essentially zero or due to destabilizing the closed state so that it rapidly backsteps to State II before ATPγS can be hydrolyzed. Second, even in the presence of an assisting force, which helps to stabilize nsp13 in the closed conformation (State III), ATPγS-hydrolysis is slower than ATP-hydrolysis. We hypothesize that this is the principal reason why ATPγS slows nsp13 in the SPRNT assay. Alternative models in which ATPγS is not hydrolyzed are shown in Supplementary Figure S22, however only the model presented in Figure ​Figure66 is capable of describing all of our observations (Supplementary Figures S22 and S23).

(B) The assisting force applied in SPRNT overcomes the inhibitory effect of ATPγS seen in (A), however, hydrolysis of ATPγS is also slower as is seen in many other ATPases.

This model can also be used to make predictions for the combined effects of ATPγS and the presence of a DNA duplex on the rate of nsp13 progression. In other words we can use observations of dsDNA unwinding (Figure ​(Figure55orange, Supplementary Figure S17) and ATPγS-driven ssDNA translocation (Figure ​(Figure5dark5dark green, Supplementary Figure S20) to predict nsp13’s behavior while unwinding with ATPγS-alone (Figure ​(Figure55black, Supplementary Figure S24). In this model, ATPγS-hydrolysis and duplex unwinding are sequential steps and are a similar order of magnitude. Therefore, the mean step dwell-time for dsDNA unwinding with ATPγS-alone is given by the sum of the mean step dwell-time for unwinding and the mean step dwell-time for ATPγS-driven ssDNA translocation and the dwell-time histograms at each position should take the form of a convolution of the dwell-time histograms from Supplementary Figures S17 and S20. The data are in good agreement with this prediction (Supplementary Figure S24).

This model also sheds light on the apparent paradox between processivity and total event lifetime. As nsp13 progresses through the various states of its ATPase cycle, it is driven forward along the DNA strand by accompanying changes to its energy landscape (39). These changes in energy landscape manifest in-part due to changes in the strength of binding between the recA-like walker domains and the DNA backbone. With this in mind, it's reasonable to assume that different states within the ATPase cycle have different rates of dissociation from the DNA (Supplementary Figure S25). When there is only one or two rate limiting steps, nsp13 spends the vast majority of its time within one or two states. For example, during ATP-driven unwinding, nsp13 spends nearly all of its time in state IV, with ADP bound waiting for domain 1A to invade the dsDNA duplex. Or during ATPγS-driven unwinding nsp13 spends a significant amount of time in state III, waiting for ATPγS hydrolysis, and state IV. With such models, it's possible to derive expressions for observables such as dwell time and per-step probability of dissociation (28) as a function of the underlying kinetic rate constants (Supplementary Figure S26). Using these expressions, we performed maximum likelihood fitting to derive estimates for the rate of unwinding, kunwind = 213 ± 8 s−1, the rate of ATPγS hydrolysis, kh γS = 51 ± 8 s−1, the off rate in state III, koff III = 5 ± 1 s−1 and the off rate in state IV, koff IV = 22 ± 1 s−1. From these, it becomes clear how nsp13 can have both shorter processivity but spend more time bound to DNA during ATPγS-driven unwinding. While using ATPγS, nsp13 spends more time in state III, awaiting hydrolysis, which has a significantly lower off rate than state IV. This means on average a single nsp13 molecule will stay bound to the DNA considerably longer. Still in state III there is a non-zero chance of dissociation while the off rate in state IV remains unchanged, so the per-step probability of dissociation is higher, resulting in lower processivity.

DISCUSSION

While previous biochemical data show nsp13 ATPase rate is enhanced up to 25-fold in the presence of single-stranded nucleic acid (17), there has been no measurement of nsp13 translocation speed until now. Unwinding speed is ∼8-fold slower compared to translocation; this ratio is similar to that recently reported for SF1A helicase PcrA using SPRNT (∼4-fold), and consistent with in vitro studies of other passive helicases (21,40,41). Notably, the nsp13 unwinding speed measured here is similar to the observed speed of SARS-CoV-2 RdRp with which nsp13 forms a complex: ∼170 nt/s at 37°C as determined by single-molecule magnetic tweezers (42).

Our assisting-force unwinding data shows speeds about 10-fold greater than estimates of bulk unwinding speed. However, our average unwinding speed is comparable to the unwinding speeds achieved using optical tweezers to destabilize the RNA duplex (16). The manner by which force is applied with SPRNT differs significantly from how force was applied in the optical tweezers study (Supplementary Figure S27). In SPRNT, force affects the advance of domain 2A following ATP-binding whereas in the optical tweezers assay, force destabilizes the RNA duplex. It is possible that in the absence of force, domain 2A advance is rate-limiting as is apparent with ATPγS at zero force (bulk). Still, all other kinetic steps observed here are unaffected by the force applied in the SPRNT experiment because force can only affect steps in which the applied force performs work on the system. Within this framework, it is possible that nsp13 dissociation is affected by the applied force, but the effect is small in the range of forces sampled here (Figure ​(Figure5G).5G). It is also possible that the forces applied in our experiment distort nsp13’s structure in a way that affects underlying rate constants. Though whether such distortion would increase or decrease these rate constants is difficult to predict. Because nsp13 speed remains relatively constant across the range of forces used (Figures ​(Figures3D,3D, ​,5F),5F), we conclude such effects are small.

The application of an assisting force to nsp13 may have biological relevance. Interactions between nsp13 and the SARS-CoV-2 RdRp have been shown to play regulatory roles that affect unwinding activity (13,14,19,43,44). Depending on how RdRp and nsp13 function in vivo it's possible that RdRp applies some assisting force to nsp13 (16,43). Cryo-EM structures with nsp7, nsp8 and nsp12 show a pair of nsp13 associated with the Replication-Transcription Complex (RTC) in which one nsp13 is also bound to an RNA strand. In these structures, the RNA-bound nsp13 has a 1B-domain which is swiveled closer to domains 1A-2A while in the other the 1B domain is further apart from domains 1A-2A. The closed state is stabilized sterically by the second nsp13 molecule in the RTC. This suggests that interaction with the 1B-domain, such that it is in a closed state, can favor RNA binding and conceivably lead to a more processive helicase (13). Similarly, engineering the DNA groove to be in a closed conformation has been shown to increase unwinding activity for SF1A helicases such as PcrA and Rep (45). Contact between nsp13 and the nanopore rim may be playing a similar role. A similar study in which the conformation of the nsp13 1B-domain is constrained could shed further light on the role of the 1B-domain.

Unlike many single molecule techniques, SPRNT is uniquely capable of studying kinetics of translocation of a helicase in the absence of a duplex. While in this study, nsp13 translocation was measured only with assisting forces, reversing the orientation such that nsp13 is pulling DNA out of–rather than lowering it into–the pore would create an opposing force on domain 1A (21). This would directly mimic how the RTC complexes may apply force in vivo (13,43,44), enabling us to study how nsp13 works against an opposing force of a DNA duplex and to determine what might occur in a head-collision with the RdRp. SPRNT thus provides a valuable platform for investigating nsp13’s role within the SARS-CoV-2 RTC.

Both translocation and unwinding with nsp13 appears to be strongly dependent upon the underlying DNA sequence. Several kinetic steps of the inchworm mechanism appear to be affected by the underlying DNA sequence. For example, during dsDNA unwinding, the advance of domain 1A is rate limiting and sequence-dependent (Supplementary Figure S17), while during ssDNA translocation with ATPγS, hydrolysis of ATPγS is rate-limiting and sequence-dependent in a different manner (Supplementary Figure S20). Similar sequence-dependent behavior has been found in all other helicases measured with SPRNT (Hel308, Rep, PcrA) (21,31) and is evident in optical tweezers data with UvrD (32). With other helicases, we have found that nearly every kinetic rate constant within the ATPase cycle displays some sequence dependence. Similar mechanisms may be at work here. Cryo-EM structures of nsp13 with an ATP analog and RNA in concert with RdRP (PDB ID: 7RDY) shows no base-specific interactions between the protein and RNA (13). Another hypothesis by which a helicase could have sequence-dependent translocation kinetics may be that differing steric effects of bases (rather than their binding affinities) in the NA binding channel allosterically influence the helicase's conformational microstate thereby affecting various kinetic rates. Viral genomes are also highly structured such that particular sequence elements may play an important role in nsp13 regulation. SPRNT experiments with nsp13 on native SARS-CoV-2 sequences could shed light on such interactions.

The DNA bases displayed in Figure ​Figure1D1D are those within the pore constriction so bases responsible for nsp13 sequence dependence are some distance 3′ of the pore, likely 3–12 bases from the pore constriction (Supplementary Figure S3). The underlying cause of nsp13 sequence dependence appears to be complex and due to more than one single base at a single location within nsp13. A systematic study of nsp13 as it translocates over and unwinds several different sequence contexts should shed light on how different bases affect nsp13 kinetics (31).

SPRNT’s ability to determine how nsp13 works also sheds light on how it can be stopped from working. ATPγS is not a viable drug candidate for nsp13 because it is a general ATPase inhibitor, but we use ATPγS here to demonstrate the power of SPRNT in examining the mechanisms of helicase inhibition in exquisite detail. Within the inchworm mechanism, several steps could conceivably be affected by substitution of ATPγS for ATP: nucleotide binding, domain 2A motion after nucleotide has bound, and nucleotide hydrolysis could all conceivably be affected. Our measurements allowed us to determine that all three steps are affected: (i) ATPγS binding was faster than that of ATP, (ii) in the absence of force, ATPγS prevents domain 1A and 2A from coming together after nucleotide binding, acting as a competitive inhibitor and (iii) in the presence of a small assisting force, domains 1A and 2A can come together quickly but hydrolysis of ATPγS is slower than ATP hydrolysis. Similar studies with other general ATPase inhibitors such as AMP-PNP could highlight other mechanisms of inhibition or ways to enhance the inhibition seen here. Similar studies with promising drug candidates could be used to precisely determine the mechanism of inhibition and aid in development of more effective inhibitors.
