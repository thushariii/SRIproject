1. INTRODUCTION

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a common rheumatic autoimmune disease whose initial symptoms are symmetrical pain and swelling of the small joints. As RA progresses, patients experience joint deformation and serious disability, and these symptoms can seriously threaten their physical and mental health. A recent epidemiological survey reported the incidence of RA was 0.5% to 1.0%, and that RA was more common in women and the elderly.1 RA has a prolonged disease course and is associated with a high rate of disability and a wide range of complications that can affect multiple organ systems.

During the progression of RA, the slowly increasing imbalance of bone metabolism leads to irreversible destruction of bone and cartilage. Zhang et al 2 reported that mitogen activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling could restrict osteoclastogenesis to inhibit bone destruction and resorption. Receptor Activator of nuclear transcription factor-κB (NF-κB) ligand (RANKL) and receptor activator of NF-κB (RANK) are important regulators of osteoclastogenesis.3 Tumor necrosis factor receptor associated factor 6 (TRAF6) binds to RANKL and RANK, and this stimulates the MAPK signaling pathway and osteoclast formation.4,5 The p38α-MAPK pathway is one of the classic MAPK pathways, and the main pathway responsible for osteoclast formation.6 RANKL prevented osteoclast formation by down-regulating the expression of p38α and NF-κB.7 NF-κB-knockout mice have normal bone structure with no osteoporosis and few osteoclasts, indicating a close relationship between NF-κB and osteoclasts.8 NF-κB stimulated Nuclear Factor of Activated T Cells C1 (NFATc1) and the differentiation of osteoclasts.9

Adenosine regulates several signaling pathways by stimulating adenosine receptors, and participates in the regulation of various metabolic processes in vivo.10 Goldman et al 11 found that acupuncture altered the level of adenosine, and that alleviating the decomposition of adenosine prolonged the effect of acupuncture. In our previous study, we found that electroacupuncture (EA) delayed the decomposition of adenosine by regulating CD73 and adenosine deaminase (ADA).12 Jiang et al 13 reported that EA protected the knee joint by inhibiting inflammatory factors in rats with collagen-induced arthritis (CIA). Some researchers found that acupuncture relieved pain and improved the physiological functions of patients with RA.14,15 Our previous study of mice with CIA found that adenosine A2A receptor (A2AR) antagonists increased the expression of inflammatory factors.16 Mediero et al 17 showed that adenosine inhibited the RANKL-mediated differentiation of osteoclasts by binding to A2AR. Sauer et al 18 found an imbalance of RANK/RANKL and a decreased volume and number trabecular cells in mice that were A2AR-deficient.

Our recent study of rats with CIA suggested that EA increased the local adenosine concentration by activating the A2AR, and then the extracellular signal regulated kinase 1/2 (ERK1/2) pathway, thus inhibiting synovitis and osteoclast differentiation.12,19 Similarly, the results of Xu et al 20 implied that EA treatment of rats with CIA inhibited NF-κB and p38α-MAPK. The p38α-MAPK and ERK1/2-MAPK pathways are both classic MAPK pathways, but the p38α-MAPK pathway is more readily activated by stress responses, growth factors, and inflammatory cells.6 Thus, we studied mice with CIA and examined the effect of EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) on inhibition of osteoclastogenesis and the roles of A2AR and the p38α-MAPK signaling pathway on this effect.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1. Animals

Eighty male C57BL/6 mice [age: 4-5 weeks, (15 ± 2) g] were purchased from the Laboratory Animal Center of Silaike (Shanghai), kept in specific pathogen free grade, and provided with standard rodent chow and water according to established requirements for animal experiments (Number of the using of Laboratory Animal: SYXK 2015-0009). Ten mice were randomized into a Blank group; all other mice were subjected to induction of CIA. Finally, 44 mice were successfully. This study was approved by the Laboratory Animal Ethics Committee of Wenzhou Medical University and Laboratory Animal Centre of Wenzhou Medical University (No. WYDW2019-0962).

2.2. Induction of CIA

In a sterile environment, chicken type Ⅱ collagen (CⅡ) (20011, Chondrex Inc., Woodinville, WA, USA) was dissolved in 0.1 mmol/L of glacial acetic acid to prepare a solution with a concentration of 2 mg/mL. Then, this solution was mixed with 5 mg/mL complete Freund's adjuvant (CFA) (7023, Chondrex Inc., Woodinville, WA, USA) in equal volumes, and emulsified on ice. Multi-point intradermal injections of 0.1 mL emulsion, each containing 0.1 mg of CII and 0.25 mg of Mycobacterium tuberculosis were administered into the tail roots of mice. The mice received second injections 21 d later.

2.3. CIA assessment

According to “Protocol for the Successful Induction of Collagen-Induced Arthritis (CIA) in Mice” from Chondrex, Inc. (Woodinville, WA, USA), we used a qualitative scoring system used to assess arthritis. A score of 0 indicated a normal joint, 1 indicated slight redness and swelling in the paw that was limited to the toe, 2 indicated medium redness and swelling in the paw, 3 indicated serious redness and swelling in the paw, and 4 indicated serious redness and swelling in the paw and ankle. Each limb was graded in this way, giving a maximum score of 16 per mouse. Only mice with total scores of 4 or more were considered to have CIA and suitable for experiments.

2.4. Experimental groups

There were four experimental groups, with 10 mice per group. Mice in the “Blank group” did not have CIA; instead, they received injections of 0.01 M acetic acid (0.1 mL) (8187551000, Sigma, Shanghai, China) followed by daily headgear fixation for 30 min as described in detail previously.16 Mice in the “CIA-control group” received intraperitoneal injections of 10% Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) (200 µL) (D8370, Solarbio, Beijing, China) and were fixed with headgear for 30 min daily from day-35 for 14 d. Mice in the “CIA-EA group” received intraperitoneal injections of 10% DMSO (200 µL) and headgear fixation as described above. For EA, the Zusanli (ST36) and Sanyinjiao (SP6) were based on descriptions in previous publications.19 Zusanli (ST36) is located 3.5mm below the fibular head. Sanyinjiao (SP6) is located 5 mm above the apex of the medial malleolus. A special intradermal acupuncture needle (0.22 mm diameter, 5.0 mm length, Huatuo Brand Acupuncture Needles, Suzhou, China) was used. These needles were inserted approximately 2 mm deep at each point. An electrical machine (KWD-808I, Changcheng brand, Changzhou, China) was connected to the acupuncture needles at these points, and the mice were electrically stimulated with a 2 Hz current in a rectangular wave. The intensity (6-7 mA) was adjusted until local muscle contractions were seen. Stimulation was provided during headgear fixation for 30 min per day from day-35 to day-49. The left and right feet of the mice received electroacupuncture at the same time. Mice in the “CIA-EA-{"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"SCH58261","term_id":"1052882304","term_text":"SCH58261"}}SCH58261 Group” received intraperitoneal injections of {"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"SCH58261","term_id":"1052882304","term_text":"SCH58261"}}SCH58261 (S4568, Sigma, Shanghai, China), headgear fixation, and then EA (as in the CIA-EA group).

2.5. Immunohistochemical analysis

The sections were deparaffinized and hydrated in a graded series of alcohols. Endogenous peroxidase was quenched with 3% H2O2 for 10 min, and then trypsin (X1020, Solarbio, Beijing, China) was used for antigen retrieval. Goat serum (G9023, Sigma, Shanghai, China) was used as a blocking agent. The sections were incubated with the primary antibody overnight at 4 ℃ (1∶200 for anti-P38α [ab4822] and anti-NF-κB [ab131100]; 1∶300 for anti-NFATc1 [ab2796]; Abcam, Cambridge, UK). The secondary antibody (ab6721, ab6728, Abcam, Cambridge, UK) was diluted 1∶300 and incubated with the sections for 1 h at room temperature. The reaction complexes were visualized with diaminobenzidine and counterstained with hematoxylin.

2.6. Western blotting

Tissue samples from the ankle joints of mice were cut into small pieces with the bone removed. Four processed tissue samples of each mouse (20 mg) were added to a lysis solution consisting of Radio Immunoprecipitation Assay Lysis buffer (pH 7.4, 200 μL) with a protease inhibitor (phenylmethanesulfonyl fluoride, PMSF). Lysis was performed on ice for 20 min, and the sample was then centrifuged at 10 000 ×g at 4 ℃ for 20 min. The supernatant was collected for protein determination using the bicinchoninic acid method, and the proteins were then subjected to gel electrophoresis and transferred to a polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membrane.

The PVDF membrane was placed in a blocking solution (5% skim milk), sealed, and then subjected to slow shaking for 2 h. The membrane was incubated with the primary antibody overnight at 4 ℃ [1∶1000 for anti-RANKL (ab65024), anti-RANK (ab200369), anti-P38α (ab4822), anti-NF-κB (ab131100), and anti-NFATc1 (ab25916), and 1∶2000 for anti-TRAF6 (ab33915); Abcam, Cambridge, UK]. β-actin, the loading control, was measured using ab8227 (Abcam, Cambridge, UK). The membrane was then washed three times with Tris-buffered saline containing 0.05% Tween-20 (TBST, pH 7.6) with slow shaking during each 10 min rinse. Then, the membrane was incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP) -conjugated secondary antibody (1∶5000; ab6721, Abcam, Cambridge, UK) in a blocking buffer for 1 h, and the chromogen was added. Finally, images were visualized and Image J software was used for quantitative analysis.

2.7. Tartrate resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP) staining

Osteoclasts were identified using a TRAP staining kit (387A, Sigma, Shanghai, China) according to the manufacturer's protocol. A sample in which three or more nuclei had “red-wine” stains was considered TRAP-positive.

2.8. Statistical analysis

All data were analyzed using SPSS version 26.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). The data were expressed as means and standard deviations. Data were compared using a one-way analysis of variance. The Tukey’s honestly significant difference test was applied when the homogeneity of variances was uniform; otherwise, Dunnett's T3 test was used for post hoc multiple comparisons. P < 0.05 was considered significant.

4. DISCUSSION

In many studies on EA treatment of CIA, Zusanli (ST36) and Sanyinjiao (SP6) are the mainstream intervention points.21,⇓-23 Goldman et al 11 found acupuncture at Zusanli (ST36) up-regulated the level of adenosine in CIA mice and Chen et al 24 found acupuncture at Sanyinjiao (SP6) reduced the symptoms of RA. In our previous study, it was proved that EA intervention on Zusanli (ST36) and Sanyinjiao (SP6) can effectively reduce joint damage and inhibit the expression of pro-inflammatory factors.12,16,19 Our recent research found EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) impeded bone destruction by activating A2AR, and that this inhibited signaling through RANKL.19 RANKL plays a vital role in the initial stage of bone metabolism.5 RANKL binding to RANK directly activates a cascade of intracellular molecular events that culminate in osteoclast formation and activation.8,25 RANK, lacking intrinsic enzymatic activity, recruits different adaptor proteins for intracellular signal transduction, and the TRAF6 is the most critical protein.26 The combination of RANK and TRAF6 can activate downstream signaling pathways.26,27 Han et al 28 found EA inhibited the expression of TRAF6 in rats. The results of the present study indicated that EA inhibited the upregulation of RANKL, RANK, and TRAF6 that occurs in mice with CIA. However, when mice with CIA received EA and a selective A2AR antagonist ({"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"SCH58261","term_id":"1052882304","term_text":"SCH58261"}}SCH58261), the concentrations of these proteins increased. Our previous research found that EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) up-regulated adenosine and activated A2AR.12,16 Thus, our current results indicated that EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) activation of A2AR was responsible for the altered expression of RANKL, RANK, and TRAF6.

The binding of RANK and TRAF6 activates mitogen-activated kinases (MAPKs), especially p38-MAPK.8 The p38 family consists of four isozymes — p38α, p38β, P38γ, and p38δ.29 Matsumoto et al 30 found that p38α is the key enzyme responsible for osteoclast formation based on in vitro experiments with a p38α selective inhibitor. The experiments of Liu et al 31 suggested EA could suppress the activation of p38. The results from our immunohistochemical analysis of p38α indicated that this protein was highly expressed in places with damaged bone structure and high levels of inflammatory factors. Notably, EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) inhibited the expression of p38α and our western blotting results were consistent with these immunohistochemical results. In agreement, we found that EA treatment of mice with CIA led to down-regulation of p38α, and that {"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"SCH58261","term_id":"1052882304","term_text":"SCH58261"}}SCH58261 treatment reversed this effect. Thus, a synthesis of the present results with those of previous studies suggests that activation of A2AR inhibits p38α (which normally regulates osteoclast differentiation and function by downstream kinases and NF-κB6). The NF-κB measurements presented here are consistent with our recent publication,19 and suggest that EA reduced the formation of osteoclasts by down-regulating NF-κB. When NF-κB is activated, it is rapidly transferred from the cytoplasm to the nucleus. NFATc1 is a terminal transcription factor that regulates osteoclastogenesis.32 NFATc1 expression is dependent on NF-κb and c-FOS which are activated by the combination of RANK-TRAF6. The results of Chaweewannakorn et al 33 implied that p38-MAPK played a crucial role in RANKL-mediated osteoclastogenesis due to its modulation of NFATc1. Takatsuna et al 34 found NF-κB inhibited osteoclast formation by down-regulating the expression of NFATc1 in mice. This conclusion is also consistent with our current results. Immun-ohistochemical staining of NF-κB and NFATc1 indicated strong expression in chondrocytes, and an association with inflammatory factors, bone destruction, and synovial tissue in the CIA-control and CIA-EA-{"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"SCH58261","term_id":"1052882304","term_text":"SCH58261"}}SCH58261 groups. Importantly, EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) down-regulated the expression of NF-κB and NFATc1 and our immunohistochemical and Western blotting results were consistent, in that they indicated EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) inhibited production of NF-κB and NFATc1, but {"type":"entrez-protein","attrs":{"text":"SCH58261","term_id":"1052882304","term_text":"SCH58261"}}SCH58261 reversed this effect.

In summary, p38α-MAPK is one of the classic MAPK pathways, and is more susceptible to inflammatory factors and stress responses.6 The p38α-MAPK pathway affects RANKL-mediated osteoclast formation by regulating NFATc1.33 EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) inhibits osteoclastogenesis by activating A2AR.12,16,19 Thus, our present experiments suggest that EA stimulating Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6) activates A2AR, and then regulates the p38-MAPK signaling pathway to inhibit osteoclastogenesis. Our experiments found that EA affected chondrocytes. Because RA can cause damage to bone and cartilage, our future experiments will focus on the relationship between EA and chondrocytes.
