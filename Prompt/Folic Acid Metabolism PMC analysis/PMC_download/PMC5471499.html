Abstract

Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD) is the most common cause of mental disabilities in the western world. It has been quite established that acute alcohol exposure can dramatically affect astrocyte function. Because the effects of early alcohol exposure on cell physiology can persist into adulthood, we tested the hypothesis that ethanol exposure in ferrets during a period equivalent to the last months of human gestation leads to persistent changes in astrocyte secretome in vitro. Animals were treated with ethanol (3.5g/kg) or saline between post-natal day (P)10-30. At P31, astrocyte cultures were made and cells were submitted to stable isotope labeling by amino acids (SILAC). 24h-conditioned media of cells obtained from ethanol- or saline-treated animals (ET-CM or SAL-CM) were collected and analyzed by quantitative mass spectrometry in tandem with liquid chromatography. Here we show that 65 out of 280 quantifiable proteins displayed significant differences comparing ET-CM to SAL-CM. Among the 59 proteins that were found to be reduced in ET-CM we observed components of the extracellular matrix such as Laminin subunits α2, α4, β1, β2 and γ1 and the proteoglycans Biglycan, Heparin Sulfate Proteoglycan 2 and Lumican. Proteins with trophic function such as Insulin-Like Growth Factor Binding Protein 4, Pigment Epithelium-Derived Factor and Clusterin as well as proteins involved on modulation of proteolysis such as TIMP-1 and PAI-1 were also reduced. In contrast, pro-synaptogeneic proteins like Thrombospondin-1, Hevin as well as the modulator of extracelular matrix expression, Angiotensinogen, were found increased in ET-CM. The analysis of interactome maps through Ingenuity Pathway Analysis demonstrated that the Amyloid beta A4 protein precursor (APP), which was found reduced in ET-CM, was previously shown to interact with ten other proteins that exhibited significant changes in the ET-CM. Taken together our results strongly suggest that early exposure to teratogens such as alcohol may lead to an enduring change in astrocyte secretome.

INTRODUCTION

Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD) is an umbrella term that encompasses numerous congenital abnormalities arising from prenatal ethanol exposure. Individuals under these conditions often present some degree of cognitive impairment and sensory systems are particularly affected (Coles et al. 2002, Franklin et al. 2008, Manji et al. 2009, Mattson & Riley 1998). Although ethanol can potentially impact normal development at any point during pregnancy, its exposure in different stages of gestation can lead to distinct outcomes. The third-trimester of gestation in humans and the first postnatal weeks in rodents and ferrets is known as the “brain growth spurt” period, with marked formation and refinement of neuronal networks. Pronounced gliogenesis and glial differentiation are also observed (Tabata 2015). During this period ethanol exposure can increase apoptosis (Olney et al. 2000), alter the formation of neuronal networks (Galindo et al. 2005), disturb glial differentiation by inducing premature transformation of radial glial cells into astrocytes (Goodlett et al. 1993, Gressens et al. 1992, Miller & Robertson 1993, Rubert et al. 2006), impair astrogliogenesis from cultured progenitor cells (Valles et al. 1996, Vemuri & Chetty 2005) and reduce glial cells survival (Climent et al. 2002, Creeley et al. 2013, Holownia et al. 1997).

Astrocytes are involved in modulating and responding to synaptic activity by regulating turnover of neurotransmitters, release of growth factors, cytokines and components of the extracellular matrix (Araque et al. 2014, Santello et al. 2012, Wiese et al. 2012). In fact, the role of astrocytes as key modulators of neuronal plasticity in the brain has been well established (De Pitta et al. 2015, Fields et al. 2015, Perea et al. 2009).

Cortical neuronal plasticity is disrupted by developmental alcohol exposure (Medina 2011), which can contribute to the alteration of cortical networks seen in FASD (Krahe et al. 2009, Medina et al. 2005, Sowell et al. 2008). Because astrocytes modulate neuronal plasticity (Fields et al. 2015) and are affected by developmental alcohol exposure (Guizzetti et al. 2014), we recently tested the potential of these cells as targets to ameliorate plasticity deficits in FASD. We showed that overexpression of SRF (serum response factor), a plasticity-related transcription factor, in astrocytes restores neuronal plasticity in the visual cortex of a ferret model of FASD (Paul et al. 2010). Similarly, cultured astrocytes overexpressing SRF and implanted in the visual cortex of ferrets were also able to improve neuronal plasticity after early ethanol exposure (Paul & Medina 2012). These results support the use of molecules secreted by astrocytes as agents in the restoration of neuronal plasticity in FASD and suggest that the astrocytic secretome may be altered in this condition. In fact, astrocytes extracted from rats prenatally exposed to ethanol inhibit neuritogenesis of cortical neurons if they are grown together (Pascual & Guerri 2007). Similarly, conditioned media from astrocytes exposed to ethanol were shown to impair neuronal survival and neuritogenesis of serotoninergic neurons in culture (Lokhorst & Druse 1993).

Pioneering studies by Costa, Guerri and Guizzetti groups have demonstrated that application of alcohol in astrocyte cultures can dramatically affect astrocytic function and the quantity of some proteins secreted by these cells (Alfonso-Loeches et al. 2010, Giordano et al. 2011, Zhang et al. 2014a). Because many effects of early alcohol exposure on cell physiology can persist into adulthood, an important question that remains unanswered is whether the effects of ethanol on the astrocyte secretome can be seen even long after alcohol exposure.

Here we hypothesize that early ethanol exposure leads to a permanent change in the secretome profile of astrocytes, which may contribute to the alterations on neuronal plasticity and cortical development seen in FASD. In order to test this, we exposed ferrets to ethanol during a period equivalent to the “brain growth spurt” and then cultured cortical astrocytes from these animals. No ethanol was present in the media while the cells were cultured. Conditioned media were collected during the fourth week in culture after several cell divisions took place and then they were analyzed by quantitative mass spectrometry in tandem with liquid chromatography.

METHODS

Animals and Ethanol Treatment

All described procedures were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at University of Maryland, School of Medicine. For a summary of our experimental design, see Figure 1. Fourteen ferrets (procured from Marshall Farms, USA) from four different litters and from both sexes were used in this study. Briefly, ferrets were treated with 3.5g/kg ethanol ip. (25% solution in sterile saline) or saline (as control) every other day between postnatal day 10 to 30 (P10-30 - Fig. 1A). This period is roughly similar to the third trimester of human gestation (Medina et al. 2003, Medina et al. 2005). We have shown in previous studies that this type of exposure leads to a marked disruption of visual cortex plasticity and disrupts the functional architecture of orientation selectivity columns (Medina et al. 2003, Medina et al. 2005, Paul et al. 2010). This treatment leads to a blood alcohol level of ~250mg/dl for 1–5h after injection (Medina et al. 2003). The SILAC protocol (see below) allows for the comparison of conditioned media from cultures that were exposed to heavy or light lysine and in order to do so pair samples of experimental (ET-CM, treated with heavy lysine) and controls (SAL-CM, treated with light lysine) were combined. A total of 7 pairs were used. To minimize potential gender effects, only males were used in 4 pairs, whereas only females were used in two pairs. One pair was made from a saline treated male and an ethanol treated female.

Cell Cultures

The culture procedure is modified from the method described by McCarthy and de Vellis (McCarthy & de Vellis 1980). Briefly, ferrets at P31 were sacrificed and the occipital portion of their cortices immediately dissected, cleaned from its meninges and put in a petri dish containing Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS). The tissue block was minced and exposed to 0.05% Trypsin–EDTA solution for 10-20 min. The tissue pieces were then mechanically triturated and serum medium (DMEM/F12 + 10% dialyzed fetal bovine serum) was added to inactivate trypsin. The cell suspension was centrifuged at 1000rpm for 3min, resuspended in SILAC growth medium and plated in T-25 culture flasks (Falcon, BD). The flasks were kept in an incubator at 37°C and 5% CO2. Growth media were changed every 2-3 days. Between culture days 7 and 10 (C7-10), flasks were agitated on an orbital shaker (250 rpm, 37°C, 12–18h) in order to remove cells other than astrocytes, such as oligodendrocytes progenitors and microglia. An astrocyte purity of ~98% was achieved after supernatant removal (McCarthy & de Vellis 1980). Before reaching confluence, at around Cultured day (C)12-15, cells were detached with Accutase (Innovative Cell Technologies) and passed to T-75 flasks.

SILAC labeling

In order to quantify differential changes in the secretome of cultured astrocytes from saline-treated and ethanol-treated animals, cells were cultivated during all time in SILAC growth medium. Cells from saline-treated animals received regular L-Lysine-2HCl (light lysine) while cells from ethanol-treated animals received 13C6 L-Lysine-2HCl (heavy lysine). Stable isotope-labeled lysine is biochemically identical to its natural analog.

Astrocyte Conditioned Media collection and processing

At C21 were detached with Accutase again and 1×106 cells were plated in T-75 flasks. In order to substantially decrease the impact of protein contaminants from fetal bovine serum in the astrocytes conditioned medium (ACM), cells were washed three times with DPBS and then exposed to a chemically-defined medium (CD Medium) containing 50nM Hydrocortisone (Sigma), 500ng/ml Prostraglandin F2α (Sigma), 100nM Putrescine Dihydrochloride (Sigma), 5μg/ml insulin (Sigma) and 50μg /ml Fibroblast Growth Factor (Biolegend) diluted in SILAC DMEM medium (Thermo Scientific). Astrocytes grown under CD Medium present similar growth rates and markers as astrocytes grown in regular 10% FBS medium (Morrison & de Vellis 1981). After 48-72h cells were once more washed three times with DPBS and fresh SILAC CD Medium was replaced and kept for 24h. ACM were then collected, cooled to 4°C and centrifuged at 4000×g during 60min with a 3NMWL Amicon Ultra Centrifugal Filter (Millipore). The final product was normalized to a 10-fold concentrated sample and we performed a protein assay using Bradford based assay (Bradford 1976).

Mass Spectometry

Total protein concentrations did not differ between ET-CM and SAL-CM (Supplemental Fig. 1). A volume of conditioned media containing 10μg of protein from saline- or ethanol-treated groups were mixed in pairs and processed for proteomic protein profiling using the FASP procedure (Wisniewski et al. 2009). Processing and measuring samples from both groups together ensures a maximum of reproducibility and accuracy of quantitation avoiding false differences due to variations in enzymatic digestion between groups. Briefly astrocyte conditioned media were processed using Microcon YM-30 (Millipore) centrifugal filter units to effect a buffer exchange into 8M urea, 100mM triethylammonium bicarbonate (TEAB), pH 8.4, whereupon disulfide bonds were reduced with 10mM dithiothreitol and free thiols alkylated with 20mM iodoacetamide. The samples were washed 3 times and brought to a final volume of 40μl with 20mM TEAB, 8M urea, and enzymatically digested at 37°C with LysC (Wako) at 12.5ng/μl for 16 hr. After addition of 120μl of 20mM TEAB, proteins were further digested with Trypsin at 12.5ng/μl (Promega) for 4h.

Peptides were desalted using Stage Tips (Rappsilber et al. 2003) prior to first dimension fractionation on a PolyWAX LP column (PolyLC) in ERLIC mode. (Hao et al. 2013). One-minute fractions were collected during the 40min chromatographic run time and were concatenated into 5 fractions, dried down and resuspended in 0.1% formic acid prior to LC-MS/MS analysis.

The second dimension chromatographic separation was done using an Eksigent AS2 nano-flow pump connected to a Chromex trap column and a Chromex XP C18 (AB Sciex) separation column connected to the ion source of an AB Sciex TripleTOF 5600 mass spectrometer. The solvents used to develop a 90min gradient were 0.01% formic acid in water and 0.01% formic acid in acetonitrile. Mass spectra were produced and data analysis with Protein Pilot 5.0 was done at the Penn State College of Medicine Proteomics and Mass Spectrometry Facility. Conversion of raw data files to mzML format was done using MSConvert (Kessner et al. 2008). Additional data analysis was done using the Trans-Proteomic Pipeline (Institute for Systems Biology) (Deutsch et al. 2010) and UniProt database. The mass spectrometry proteomics data have been deposited to the ProteomeXchange Consortium (Vizcaino et al. 2014) via the PRIDE partner repository with the dataset identifier PXD002787.

Data Processing and Protein Ranking

Only proteins that presented spectral counts from at least two unique peptides with >95% identity confidence were considered for further analyzes. All identified proteins in the astrocyte conditioned media were analyzed for extracellular localization through the presence of a signal peptide in their sequences using SecretomeP (Bendtsen et al. 2004) and SignalP (Petersen et al. 2011). They were also inspected in the gene ontology database for extracellular functions (Ashburner et al. 2000). Proteins positive for extracellular localization in at least one of the previously described categorization methods were considered for further quantitative analyses (Supplementary Table 1).

Gene Ontology Analysis

Gene Ontology (GO) Annotations taken from the complete set of secreted proteins detected in the astrocyte conditioned media of both experimental groups were analyzed under the PANTHER Classification System (Mi et al. 2013). Analysis of overrepresented functional categories was carried out by grouping proteins into “Slim” biological functions. Since terms in the GO database frequently overlap, we used a ‘slim’ version of this database, which classifies the proteins from the astrocyte CM samples into a limited number of biological processes. Functional categories were considered overrepresented when the number of proteins in a given category was enriched relative to the same category in the whole Homo sapiens proteome using the binomial statistical test with Bonferroni correction. Significance was assumed at the level of p < 0.01. Statistical data for each individual category like fold enrichment and p value can be found in the Supplementary Table 3.

Pathway Analysis

UniProt Protein Identification numbers were uploaded to the Ingenuity Pathway Analysis software (IPA) to determine protein interactions. Using information from the Ingenuity Pathway knowledge base, the computational algorithm identified relationships among proteins and formed clusters of interaction.

Statistical Analysis

Proteins that presented spectral counts from at least two unique peptides with >95% identity confidence and detectable heavy/light lysine ratios in three or more samples were considered for quantitation. Heavy/Light lysine ratio values were transformed to percentage values in each paired sample. For a better visualization in the graph bars, values from ethanol group (heavy lysine labeled) were multiplied by (−1). Data on the graphs are reported as mean and standard error. Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS (IBM, Armonk, NY), and the Mann-Whitney test was used to compare relative values from both experimental groups. Significance was assumed at the level of p < 0.05. Statistical data for each individual protein like mean ratio, number of samples used and p value can be found in the Supplementary Table 2.

Immunocytochemistry

Cultured cells were washed with DPBS, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 10min at room temperature, washed three times with 0.1M PBS, permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100 for 10min, washed three times with 0.1M PBS, blocked with 10% Normal Goat Serum in PBS and then incubated with primary antibody rabbit anti-Vimentin (Abcam, ab92547) diluted 1:250 overnight at 4°C. On the following day cells were washed three times with 0.1M PBS and incubated with the secondary antibody goat anti-rabbit (Life Technologies) conjugated with Alexa Fluor 594 diluted 1:250 in PBS during 90min and protected from light. After being washed with 0.1M PBS three times, cells were stained with of 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) during 30s, washed three times with 0.1M PBS and immediately imaged under an Olympus microscope.

RESULTS

In order to confirm astrocyte purity in our cultures, we performed immunocytochemistry for the astrocyte marker Vimentin at C21. Virtually all cells were positive for Vimentin and presented astrocyte-like morphology in vitro (Fig. 2A).

In order to obtain a large-scale profile of the ferret astrocyte secretome, conditioned media from astrocyte cultures were collected between C24-C28, digested and submitted to LC/MS as detailed in Methods. Raw mass spectra were processed through Trans Proteomic Pipeline, which generated a list of detected proteins and their respective gi numbers. Additional searches in the UniProt database were made in order to obtain UniProtKB accession numbers, gene names and gene ontology (GO) information from all detected proteins. Of the 1005 proteins identified, 825 (82%) were found to be potentially secreted either by the presence of a signal peptide in their sequence or by being annotated with extracellular functions in the gene ontology database (Fig. 2B). An alphabetized list containing identifiers and mass spectra information for all 825 potentially secreted proteins is available in the Supplementary Table 1. An abundance ranking was also assigned to these proteins relative to their respective total independent spectra.

Additionally, we compared quantitative differences in the astrocyte secretome content between SAL-CM and ET-CM. For this quantification, we considered proteins which presented spectral counts from at least two unique peptides with >95% identity confidence and detectable heavy/light lysine ratios in three or more samples. Of the 825 secreted proteins, we were able to quantify differences between SAL-CM and ET-CM in 280 of them (34% of secreted proteins - Fig. 2B). The number of quantifiable proteins is small due to limitations in detectability of mass spectra from proteins present in small amounts in the samples. This is supported by the fact that about 80% of the quantifiable proteins are among the 300 most abundant ones in the protein ranking provided in Supplementary Table 1. Moreover, of the 280 quantifiable proteins, 65 proteins (23% of quantifiable proteins) showed significant differences in the ET-CM in relation to the SAL-CM (Fig. 2B; Supplementary Table 2). The fact that almost one quarter of the quantifiable secreted proteins are affected suggests that early ethanol exposure has a dramatic impact on the astrocyte secretome. All quantitative information is detailed in the Supplementary Table 2.

To better understand the biological importance of the astrocyte secretome, we turned to gene ontology analysis. Using the PANTHER classification system, we compared the categorization of the complete set of proteins detected in the conditioned media of both experimental groups versus a reference list of the human proteome using GO information (Fig. 2C). Since terms in the GO database frequently overlap, we used a ‘slim’ version of this database, which classifies the proteins from the astrocyte CM samples into a limited number of biological processes. Detailed statistics about the GO analysis of the astrocyte secretome can be found in the Supplementary Table 3.

No trend was observed between sexes. While the number of animals when separated by sex is not high enough to produce meaningful statistical comparisons, males and females showed the same direction of the effects (increase or decrease in expression when compared to controls) in 63 out of the 65 proteins that showed significant differences between ET-CM and SAL-CM. The two proteins that showed different trends when groups were separated by sex were Dihydropirimidinase-related protein 2 (DPYSL2) and Soluble malate dehydrogenase (MDH2) and both showed increased expression in ET-CM females compared to SAL-CM ones, but were decreased in ET-CM males compared to SAL-CM ones (Supplementary Table 4).

In order to understand what kind of biological processes can be potentially affected by early ethanol exposure in the astrocyte secretome, the proteins that exhibited significant changes in the ET-CM were assigned to five different categories based on those biological processes that were overrepresented in the astrocyte secretome. Even using the slim GO database, we obtained some overlapping terms, most of them encompassing four major processes (Fig. 2C). We therefore assigned all the quantified proteins into the following categories: Differentiation, Cell Growth & Survival; Extracellular Matrix & Adhesion; Protein Processing & Proteolysis; Metabolism. We also included another category (Others) for proteins that could not be classified in the major 4 processes (Others) (Fig. 3-​-7).7). Of the 65 proteins that are significantly different in the ET-CM, 59 (91%) showed a decrease in relation to the SAL-CM group. This trend is present in all five categories of proteins. We have not detected any protein that was exclusively found in one of the two experimental groups.

In the set of proteins assigned to the Differentiation, Cell Growth & Survival category (Fig. 3), three of them are among those which showed the largest decrease in the ET-CM group across all categories: Insulin-Like Growth Factor Binding Protein 4 (IGFBP4) (−89%), Pigment Epithelium-Derived Factor (PEDF) (−88%) and Clusterin (−85%). On the other hand, Secreted Frizzled-Related Protein 4 (SFRP4) was the only one in this category that was increased in the ET-CM (+174%).

The category Extracellular Matrix & Adhesion encompasses the largest set of proteins and some of the most interesting results (Fig. 4). Lumican (−75%), Cadherin-11 (−83%) and the subunit alpha-2 of Laminin (−86%) were the proteins that exhibited the highest levels of decrease in this category. Interestingly, 5 different subunits of Laminin were found to be decreased in the ET-CM suggesting that the impact of ethanol strongly affects the biology of Laminin. Like Lumican, other proteoglycans of the extracellular matrix like Biglycan (−43%) and Heparin Sulfate Proteoglycan 2 were also decreased in the ET-CM. On the other hand, the pro-synaptogenic proteins Hevin and Thrombospondin-1 were increased by 37% and 83% respectively in the ET-CM. Angiotensinogen is another protein that was found to be increased (by 89%) in this set. Taken together these results strongly suggest that early ethanol exposure can potentially unbalance the extracellular matrix composition.

Since the number of proteins secreted by astrocytes we identified being modulated by early ethanol exposure was considerably large, it is possible that some of these are being secondarily modulated by others that were directly affected by ethanol. Thus, in order to identify what kind of interactions were present in this set of proteins, we created interactome maps using the Ingenuity Pathway Analysis database. Two analyses were performed. In the first one, we analyzed the group of proteins that were increased in ET-CM against the group of proteins that were decreased in ET-CM (Fig. 8A). Interestingly, angiotensinogen was found to potentially upregulate some important targets like Biglycan, Laminin beta 1, Collagen Alpha-1 (Type VI), PAI-1 and TIMP-1, however these were in fact downregulated in ET-CM. TIMP-1 is also potentially upregulated by Thrombospondin-1 in other systems as well as the Low density lipoprotein receptor-related protein 1 (LRP1). No data was found when the IPA search was made in the opposite direction. In other words, the six proteins found to be upregulated in ET-CM were not previously described as being modulated by any of the fifty-nine proteins downregulated in ET-CM. In the second interactome map we analyzed the proteins within the group that was downregulated in ET-CM (Fig. 8B). Most interactions in this map were characterized by binding without modulation of expression. We were able to identify seven “hub proteins” exhibiting 5 or more interactions. Among them we highlight the Amyloid beta A4 protein precursor (APP), which was previously shown to interact with ten other proteins, rendering it suitable for a more accurate analysis about the effects of ethanol on its biology. We have not found any previously described interactions among the six proteins increased in ET-CM.

DISCUSSION

Here, we showed that developmental ethanol exposure leads to a permanent change in the astrocyte secretome. To the best of our knowledge this is the first LC-MS/MS study describing, the astrocyte secretome profile of a gyrencephalic animal. Almost all published studies describing the astrocyte secretome were done in mouse or rat cell models. The exception was a study published by Choi and colleagues describing the secretome profile of human astrocytes using an array capable of detecting 36 cytokines/chemokines (Choi et al. 2014). Since we used an approach based in LC-MS/MS, our data is pioneering in the study of large proteins secreted by astrocytes from gyrencephalic animals. In the past decade several studies have investigated the astrocyte secretome profile using different proteomic approaches (Greco et al. 2010, Han et al. 2014, Lafon-Cazal et al. 2003, Moore et al. 2009). Although the number of identified proteins varies between different papers, the identity of the major proteins detected in these studies was similar to what we found in our data set, thus indicating that astrocytes from rodents and higher mammals are able to secrete similar trophic factors, extracellular matrix components, and modulators of proteolytic activity.

Most studies describing the effects of early ethanol exposure in astrocytes were focused on acute application of ethanol and addressed short-term changes after ethanol exposure (Blanco et al. 2004, Zhang et al. 2014b). For instance, some components of the extracellular matrix like laminin, fibronectin, and plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1), that are secreted by astrocytes in response to carbachol, are reduced if these cells are exposed to ethanol (Guizzetti et al. 2010). Interestingly we found similar results in the ET-CM where several subunits of laminin and plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 are also reduced (Fig. 4 & 5). In addition, we found a trend in fibronectin to decrease (−40%; P=0.053) in ET-CM (Supplementary Table 2). Taken together these results suggest that the changes in astrocyte-secreted proteins caused by acute ethanol exposure may be permanent.

There is pronounced gliogenesis and glial proliferation during the timing of alcohol exposure used in the present study. Early in development, neural progenitors radial glial cells (RGs) preferentially generate neurons while in a later stage, right before birth in rodent models, RGs shift to a glia-restricted progenitor profile, acquiring the potential to differentiate into astrocytes and oligodendrocytes (Siddiqi et al. 2014). In RGs, the canonical Wnt pathway seems to be important for early neurogenesis (Munji et al. 2011), but it needs to be inhibited in order to generate glial cells (Kasai et al. 2005, Tang et al. 2002). Interestingly, we found that the Wnt signaling inhibitors Clusterin, Dickkopf-related protein 3 (DKK3), Insulin-like growth factor binding protein 4 (IGFBP4) and Pigment epithelium-derived factor (PEDF), are decreased in ET-CM (Fig. 3). On the other hand, Secreted frizzled-related protein 4 (SFRP4), another negative modulator of the Wnt signaling, is upregulated in ET-CM (Fig. 3). These results may suggest that early ethanol exposure could affect differentiation of the glial lineage by modulating Wnt signaling. In the cortex, ethanol can disturb glial differentiation by inducing premature transformation of RGs into astrocytes (Goodlett et al. 1993, Gressens et al. 1992, Miller & Robertson 1993, Rubert et al. 2006), and also impair astrogliogenesis from cultured progenitor cells (Valles et al. 1996, Vemuri & Chetty 2005). Moreover, ethanol can affect Wnt signaling in progenitor cells (Vangipuram & Lyman 2012).

The extracellular matrix provides mechanical and chemical cues to orchestrate cellular functions during Central Nervous System development and its composition is crucial for regulating processes like cell growth, differentiation, synaptogenesis and synaptic plasticity (Dityatev et al. 2010a, Dityatev et al. 2010b). Our results suggest that early ethanol exposure can permanently downregulate the astrocytic secretion of structural ECM components like proteoglycans (Biglycan, HSPG2 and Lumican), collagen fibers (Collagens alpha-1 types VI and XII) and five subunits of Laminin (Fig. 4). Interestingly, we found an increase in Angiotensinogen in ET-CM. As detected in the interactome map (Fig. 8A), this precursor molecule and its effector Angiotensin are usually found positively regulating components of the extracellular matrix in several different systems (Kagami et al. 1994, Murphy et al. 2015, Tamura et al. 2000, Weber et al. 1999). Since we found a global decrease in structural molecules of ECM in ET-CM, one possibility to be addressed in the future is that the increase in Angiotensinogen seen in ET-CM could be a physiological response attempting to restore normal ECM composition.

Additionally, molecules known to establish close interactions with structural components of ECM like Hevin, Thrombospondin-1 and the amyloid precursor protein (APP) were also affected by ethanol. Besides its importance in the physiology of the Alzheimer’s disease, APP and its cleavage products play an important role during development, such as bidirectional modulatiion of synaptogenesis and synaptic plasticity (Kirazov et al. 2001, Morimoto et al. 1998, Wang et al. 2009, Wang et al. 2012). Moreover, among the proteins that decreased in ET-CM, APP was found to be the one with the higher number of known interactions (mostly binding) in the interactome map (Fig. 8B). For all these reasons APP is an excellent candidate to play a role in the deficits seen in FASD models. In turn, Hevin and Thrombospondin-1, known prosynaptogenic molecules (Christopherson et al. 2005, Kucukdereli et al. 2011) were found increased in ET-CM. These results are compatible with disturbed brain plasticity seen in models of FASD. Corroborating this idea, TIMP-1, an inhibitor of MMP-2 and MMP-9, and Plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) were found decreased after early ethanol exposure (Fig. 5). Both TIMP-1 and PAI-1 regulate neuronal plasticity (Cho et al. 2013, Jourquin et al. 2005) and together with the other modulators of proteolysis that we found affected in ET-CM (Fig. 5) suggest that proteolytic activity can be globally disturbed in FASD models affecting normal brain development and ECM composition.

Global proteomic analyses of conditioned media result in the identification of not only secreted and extracellular proteins but non-secreted proteins as well, which in part can be attributed to cellular death. In order to avoid quantifying proteins that belonged to debris caused by cell death, we only quantified those proteins containing a signal peptide, those known to be non-classically secreted by their sequence or those with known extracellular functions in the GO database. Some of the proteins assigned to extracellular functions but non-classically secreted were previously known to be classical intracellular components and can also be found in exosomes and microvesicles. In fact, we found several Heat Shock Proteins relatively well ranked in our secretome dataset (Supplementary Table 1), and these proteins have been considered as exosome markers (Campanella et al. 2014), suggesting that these structures should be taken into account when analyzing the composition of the astrocyte secretome. Exosomes exhibit specific cell-type dependent content and have emerged as plasticity modulators and neuron-glia mediators (Chivet et al. 2013, Fruhbeis et al. 2013, Korkut et al. 2013). Interestingly, some proteins that can be found in exosomes, like Annexin-1, Complement factor H, Clusterin, Hexosaminidase subunit B, PEDF, Plectin and Vimentin (Leoni et al. 2015, Proia et al. 2008, Shin et al. 2013), were found decreased in ET-CM (Fig. 3-​-7),7), suggesting that the function and composition of these structures can be compromised by early ethanol exposure and should be considered in future investigations.

The interactome map of the proteins found changed in ET-CM (Fig. 8B) can potentially identify regulatory systems that could be further studied in the context of the FASD. Proteins with a high number of known interactions, also named hub proteins, can be used to determine these potential ethanol-modulated regulatory systems. We have found seven hub proteins, which two are important structural constituents of ECM (Laminin subunit gamma-1 and HSPG2), and two are modulators of protease activity (PAI-1 and LRP1). Together with the cell adhesion molecule Cadherin-1 and the integral membrane protein APP, all of them are well characterized by their closest interactions with ECM physiology. Interestingly, the majority of the interactions found in Figure 8B were described as binding with no modulation. Since several proteins found changed in ET-CM were structural components of ECM, this result strongly suggest that the network stability of ECM is possibly compromised in FASD models and should be considered a major target for future investigations. Surprisingly we also have found the intermediate filament Vimentin as a hub protein. In addition to its previously mentioned role in exossomes, extracellular Vimentin has been described to promote axonal growth through the insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor (Shigyo et al. 2015). Since ethanol can affect axon growth in several different models (Bearer et al. 1999, VanDemark et al. 2009, Lindsley et al. 2011), it would be interesting to test if ethanol-mediated downregulation of extracellular Vimentin is found in vivo and if it affects axon growth.

Our study triggers questions regarding the mechanisms underlying the long lasting changes in the astrocyte secretome after alcohol exposure. One possibility is an epignetic action of alcohol. Early ethanol exposure can induce epigenetic modifications by modulating DNA methylation, histone modifications and MicroRNA (miRNA) expression (Laufer et al. 2013, Kleiber et al. 2014a, Kleiber et al. 2014b). In fact, it has been described that ethanol can decrease global methylation in the hippocampus (Otero et al. 2012) and in cortical astroglial cultures it can specifically decrease DNA methylation of the tPA promoter thereby inducing an increase in tPA expression and release (Zhang et al. 2014b). On the other hand, since these epigenetics-miRNA regulatory processes can co-regulate each other in complex regulatory networks (Sato et al. 2011), ethanol-mediated miRNA regulation can be as critical as DNA methylation and histone modifications for gene expression. For instance, Zhang and colleagues showed a miRNA-dependent decrease in VGLUT2 content in the adult hippocampus of prenatally ethanol exposed animals despite an observed downregulation of DNA methylation and upregulation of histone H3 lysine methylation at the VGLUT2 promoter (Zhang et al. 2015). Thus, it would be interesting to test whether the decrease in proteins that we see in ethanol-exposed astrocyte conditioned media in this study could be explained by modulation of epigenetics-miRNA regulatory circuit. Another possibility lies on the fact that acute ethanol can modify Golgi complex morphology and reduce the number of secretory vesicles, perturbing the secretory pathway in astrocytes (Tomas et al. 2005). It would be interesting to test whether such effects could be implicated in the long-term changes induced by developmental ethanol exposure.

Another possible explanation for the effect of ethanol on astrocyte secretome would be a selective reduction or elimination of particular subtypes of astrocytes and glial progenitors by developmental ethanol exposure since these cells potentially exhibit different secretome profiles. Despite several subtypes of astrocytes have been described in the literature by morphology and localization within the brain (Tabata 2015), no markers are available for such identification. Additionally, once in vitro these cells present a completely different morphology than expected in vivo turning challenging the assessment to contributions of each astroglial subtype. In the current study our cell cultures were obtained from the occipital cortex and therefore predominantly from the gray matter, a region particularly enriched with protoplasmic astrocytes but not fibrous astrocytes (Miller & Raff 1984). Layer-1 astrocytes are also expected to be present in our preparation despite being outnumbered in relation to protoplasmic astrocytes in the cortex (Garcia-Marques & Lopez-Mascaraque 2013). On the other hand, intermediate glial progenitors like oligodendrocyte progenitors (OPCs) are expected to be removed as part of the astrocyte purification step, which should also avoid the presence of OPCs-derived type 2 astrocytes. Thus, an ethanol-mediated unbalance between the protoplasmic and layer-1 astrocytes numbers could explain the differences seen in the secretome profile in this study. No data is currently available about how specific astroglial subtypes could be affected by developmental ethanol exposure turning this topic relevant for further investigations.

In conclusion we showed that 23% of the quantifiable proteins in the astrocyte conditioned media are affected long-term by early ethanol exposure in vivo. These changes are undoubtedly long-term because they were detected in cells cultured for more than three weeks, after several cell division cycles had taken place. Our findings suggest that brief exposure to teratogens such as alcohol may lead to an enduring change in astrocyte secretome, which in turn may lead to permanent alterations in brain function.
