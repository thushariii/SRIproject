Abstract

Introduction

This study aimed to identify whether microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and/or intra‐amniotic inflammation in women with late preterm prelabor rupture of membranes (PPROM) was associated with changes in concentrations of soluble fms‐like tyrosine kinase‐1 (sFlt‐1), placental growth factor (PlGF) and its ratio in maternal serum, and whether placental features consistent with maternal vascular malperfusion further affect their concentrations.

Material and methods

This historical study included 154 women with singleton pregnancies complicated by PPROM between gestational ages 34+0 and 36+6 weeks. Transabdominal amniocentesis was performed as part of standard clinical management to evaluate the intra‐amniotic environment. Women were categorized into two subgroups based on the presence of microorganisms and/or their nucleic acids in amniotic fluid (determined by culturing and molecular biology method) and intra‐amniotic inflammation (by amniotic fluid interleukin‐6 concentration evaluation): (1) those with the presence of microorganisms and/or inflammation (at least one present) and (2) those with negative amniotic fluid for infection/inflammation (absence of both). Concentrations of sFlt‐1 and PlGF were assessed using the Elecsys® sFlt‐1 and Elecsys® PlGF immunoassays and converted into multiples of medians.

Results

Women with the presence of microorganisms and/or inflammation in amniotic fluid had lower serum concentrations of sFlt‐1 and sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios and higher concentrations of PlGF compared with those with negative amniotic fluid. (sFlt‐1: presence: median 1.0 multiples of the median (MoM), vs negative: median: 1.5 MoM, P = 0.003; PlGF: presence: median 0.7 MoM, vs negative: median 0.4 MoM, P = 0.02; sFlt‐1/PlGF: presence: median 8.9 vs negative 25.0, P = 0.001). Higher serum concentrations of sFlt‐1 and sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios as well as lower concentrations of PlGF were found in the subsets of women with maternal vascular malperfusion than in those without maternal vascular malperfusion.

Conclusions

Among women experiencing late PPROM, angiogenic imbalance in maternal serum is primarily observed in those without both microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and intra‐amniotic inflammation. Additionally, there is an association between angiogenic imbalance and the presence of maternal vascular malperfusion.

Key message

Late preterm prelabor rupture of membranesis is associated with angiogenic imbalance that is emphasized in cases without microorganism and inflammation in amniotic fluid and with the presence of placental features consistent with maternal vascular malperfusion.

1. INTRODUCTION

Preterm prelabor rupture of membranes (PPROM) is defined as rupture of the fetal membranes with leakage of amniotic fluid before the onset of regular uterine activity, prior to the 37th week of gestation. 1 , 2 PPROM remains a clinical conundrum due to its unpredictability, unpreventability and variable underlying pathologies. PPROM represents a heterogeneous syndrome with complex pathophysiology that may include inflammation, 3 infection, 4 senescence, 5 oxidative stress, 5 maternal anti‐fetal rejection, 6 excessive thrombin generation 7 and angiogenic imbalance. 8 , 9

PPROM can be classified as “early” or “late” according to the gestational age of manifestation. The most frequently used gestational age cut‐off is 34+0 weeks. 1 , 2 Early PPROM is associated with worse neonatal outcomes than late PPROM. 10 Late PPROM may be incorrectly considered a benign pregnancy complication, thus diminishing its relevance. Nevertheless, late PPROM still represents a serious clinical issue for the following reasons: (i) it is responsible for 40%–60% of all PPROM; 11 (ii) it has variable pathophysiology when compared with early PPROM; 8 , 12 (iii) it is associated with antepartum hemorrhage and/or clinical chorioamnionitis in pregnant women; 13 (iv) the presence of microorganisms and/or their nucleic acids, known as microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity, complicates approximately 20% of the cases; 11 , 14 (v) elevation of concentrations of inflammatory mediators in amniotic cavity, known as intra‐amniotic inflammation, is found in approximately 15% of cases; 11 , 14 and (vi) it is associated with an increased risk of neonatal early onset sepsis. 11 , 13

Both phenotypes of PPROM, along with other pregnancy complications such as preeclampsia, fetal growth restriction, preterm labor with intact membranes and stillbirth, have been shown to be associated with an imbalance of angiogenic/anti‐angiogenic factors in maternal blood serum. 8 , 9 Altered concentrations of the angiogenic factor (placental growth factor [PlGF]) and the anti‐angiogenic factor (soluble fms‐like tyrosine kinase‐1 [sFlt‐1], also known as soluble vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) receptor‐1), together with their ratio, were recently reported in pregnancies complicated by PPROM compared with uncomplicated pregnancies. 8 The changes characterized by a decrease of concentrations of PlGF and increase of concentrations of sFlt‐1 were more pronounced in a subset of PPROM pregnancies where the placental histopathology revealed features consistent with maternal vascular malperfusion (MVM). 8

Given the presence of microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and/or intra‐amniotic inflammation as conditions that are likely associated with placental features more consistent with acute inflammation than with MVM, 15 we hypothesize that this subset of PPROM could exhibit different maternal serum concentrations of angiogenic and anti‐angiogenic factors than remaining PPROM pregnancies. However, there is a paucity of relevant information on this field.

To fill this knowledge gap, we conducted a historical study on women with singleton pregnancies complicated by late PPROM with known intra‐amniotic environment with the following aims: (i) to compare concentrations of sFlt‐1, PlGF and the sFlt‐1/PlGF ratio in maternal serum between the subsets of late PPROM with or without microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and/or intra‐amniotic inflammation; and (ii) to assess whether the presence of MVM affects the concentrations of sFlt‐1, PlGF and the sFlt‐1/PlGF ratio in maternal serum in the subsets of late PPROM with or without microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and/or intra‐amniotic inflammation.

2. MATERIAL AND METHODS

This historical cohort study included pregnant women with singleton pregnancies complicated by PPROM between gestational ages of 34+0 and 36+6 weeks who were admitted to the Department of Obstetrics and Gynecology, University Hospital Hradec Kralove in the Czech Republic between January 2018 and September 2022. The exclusion criteria were: (i) pregnancy complications such as fetal growth restriction, gestational or pregestational diabetes, gestational or chronic hypertension, and preeclampsia; (ii) structural or chromosomal fetal abnormalities; (iii) signs of fetal hypoxia; (iv) significant vaginal bleeding; and (v) where amniocentesis was not performed. All women were white.

Gestational age was determined based on a first‐trimester ultrasound scan. The diagnosis of PPROM was made based on the visual confirmation of amniotic fluid pooling in the posterior vaginal fornix during the sterile speculum examination. When uncertainty persisted after speculum examination, amniotic fluid leakage was confirmed or excluded using a test to determine the presence of insulin‐like growth factor‐binding protein‐1 in the vaginal fluid (Actim PROM; Medix Biochemica, Kaunianine, Finland).

Transabdominal ultrasound‐guided amniocentesis was offered to all women admitted with a singleton pregnancy complicated by PPROM between gestational age 34+0 and 36+6 weeks to assess intra‐amniotic inflammation and microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity as a part of the standard clinical management of PPROM. Amniocentesis was performed at the time of admission before administration of corticosteroids and antibiotics. The samples of amniotic fluid were used for the assessment of the concentration of interleukin (IL)‐6, aerobic and anaerobic culture, PCR analysis of Ureaplasma species, Mycoplasma hominis and Chlamydia trachomatis, and sequencing of the 16S rRNA gene. Details of this procedure were described in our previous publications. 16 , 17

The women with PPROM were treated with antibiotics. Those with intra‐amniotic inflammation received intravenous clarithromycin for 7 days unless delivery occurred. Those without intra‐amniotic inflammation received intravenous benzylpenicillin (or clindamycin in case of penicillin allergy) for 7 days unless delivery occurred. A course of corticosteroid therapy to accelerate lung maturity was administered to the women with of gestational age 34+0 and 34+6 weeks. The women with PPROM were managed expectantly. If there was evidence of intra‐amniotic inflammation and/or microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity, the antimicrobial therapy was modified accordingly and induction of labor or cesarean section was scheduled within 24 hours.

A vaginal‐rectal swab to assess for the presence or absence of group B Streptococcus was obtained at the time of admission. Women with culture positive group B Streptococcus or with an unknown status of group B Streptococcus received intravenous benzylpenicillin (clindamycin, in case of penicillin allergy) during an active labor.

After delivery, the placenta, fetal membranes and umbilical cord were fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin and sent for histopathologic examination.

Late PPROM patients were chosen for two pragmatic reasons: (i) angiogenic imbalance is more commonly associated with this group than in patients with early PPROM, as placental features consistent with chronic inflammation and MVM are dominant in this phenotype; 18 , 19 (ii) a calculator was available to transform the concentrations of PlGF and sFlt‐1 in maternal serum into multiples of the median (MoM) for gestational ages of 34–36 weeks. 20 , 21

2.1. Maternal blood sampling

Maternal blood samples were collected at the time of admission before administering antibiotics and corticosteroids. Maternal blood was obtained via venipuncture of the cubital vein. The the maternal blood sample was sent to the laboratory for complete blood count assessment and C‐reactive protein concentration. The remaining blood was centrifuged and serum was aliquoted and stored at −80°C for further research use.

2.2. Analysis of IL‐6 concentrations in amniotic fluid

Amniotic fluid IL‐6 concentration measurement in the fresh amniotic fluid samples was performed via the automated electrochemiluminescence immunoassay method using the Cobas e602 immunoanalyzer, which is part of the Cobas 8000 platform (Roche Diagnostics, Basel, Switzerland). 22 The measurement range was 1.5–5000 pg/mL, which could be extended to 50 000 pg/mL by a 10‐fold dilution of the sample.

2.3. Identification of microorganisms and/or their nucleic acids in amniotic fluid

DNA was isolated from the amniotic fluid using a QIAamp DNA Mini Kit (QIAGEN, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer's instructions (using the protocol for isolating bacterial DNA from biological fluids). Real‐time PCR was performed on a Rotor‐Gene 6000 instrument (QIAGEN) using the commercial AmpliSens® C. trachomatis/Ureaplasma/M. hominis‐ FRT (Federal State Institution of Science, Central Research Institute of Epidemiology, Moscow, Russia) to detect DNA from Ureaplasma spp., Mycoplasma hominis and Chlamydia trachomatis using common polymerase chain reaction (PCR) tubes. As a control, a PCR run for beta‐actin, a housekeeping gene, was performed to assess the presence of inhibitors of the PCR.

Bacterial DNA was identified by PCR targeting 16S rRNA using the following primers: 5‐CCAGACTCCTACGGGAGGCAG‐3 (V3 region) and 5‐ACATTTCACAACACGAGC‐GACGA‐3 (V6 region). 23 Each reaction contained 3 μL of target DNA, 500 nM forward and reverse primers, and Q5 High‐Fidelity DNA Polymerase (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA) in a total volume of 25 μL. Amplification was performed using the 2720 Thermal Cycler (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). The products were visualized on an agarose gel. Positive reactions yielded 950‐bp products that were subsequently analyzed by sequencing. The 16S PCR products were purified and sequenced using the above‐mentioned primers and the BigDye Terminator v3.1 Cycle Sequencing Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). The bacteria were then typed using sequences obtained from BLAST® and SepsiTest™ BLAST. 24 These tests are routinely offered clinically at our medical center and do not represent research tests. 17

Amniotic fluid samples were cultured on Columbia agar with sheep blood, Gardnerella vaginalis selective medium, MacConkey agar, Neisseria‐selective medium (modified Thayer–Martin medium), Sabouraud agar and Schaedler anaerobe agar. The plates were cultured for 6 days and checked daily. Species were identified by matrix‐assisted laser desorption/ionization time‐of‐flight mass spectrometry using MALDI BIOTYPER software (Bruker Daltonics, Bremen, Germany).

2.4. Analysis of sFlt‐1 and PlGF concentrations in maternal serum

The Elecsys® sFlt‐1 and Elecsys® PlGF immunoassays (Roche Diagnostics) were used to assess sFlt‐1 and PlGF concentrations and sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios in the maternal serum samples with just one freeze–thaw cycle. Single measurements of the samples were performed for sFlt‐1 and PlGF using the fully automated Elecsys® sFlt‐1 and Elecsys® PlGF immunoassays. The measurement range of Elecsys® sFlt‐1 and Elecsys® PlGF was 10–85 000 pg/mL and 3–10 000 pg/mL, respectively.

The measured concentrations of sFlt‐1 and PlGF were converted into MoMs with adjustment for gestational age at sampling, maternal characteristics, medical history and the biochemistry machine used for making the measurements with the use of calculators available on the webpage of the Fetal Medicine Foundation (https://www.fetalmedicine.org/research/mom) for gestational ages 30+0 to 34+6 weeks and 35+0 to 37+6 weeks. 20 , 21

2.5. Placental pathology

Tissue samples were obtained from the placenta (at least two samples), fetal membranes (one sample from the free margin of the membranes, one from the central part of the membranes, and one from the marginal placental membranes), and umbilical cord (usually one sample) and routinely processed and embedded in paraffin. Sections of the tissue blocks were stained with hematoxylin and eosin. The samples were examined by two perinatal pathologists (HH and SJ) who were blinded to clinical diagnoses and outcomes.

MVM was diagnosed using the criteria established by the Perinatal Section of the Society for Pediatric Pathology and Amsterdam Placental Workshop Group consensus and included at least one of the following: (a) villous change, further subdivided into abrupt onset, gradual onset with intermediate duration or gradual onset with prolonged duration and (b) vascular lesion – (i) persistent muscularization of the basal plate arteries, (ii) mural hypertrophy of the decidual arterioles, (iii) acute atherosis of the basal plate arteries, (iv) fibrinoid necrosis of the spiral arteries, (v) persistence of endovascular trophoblast and (vi) spiral artery thrombosis. 25

2.6. Clinical definitions

Intra‐amniotic inflammation was defined as a concentration of IL‐6 in amniotic fluid of ≥3000 pg/mL. 22 Microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity was defined as the presence of microorganisms detected in amniotic fluid cell cultures and/or microbial nucleic acids in amniotic fluid. Intra‐amniotic infection was defined as the concomitant presence of microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and intra‐amniotic inflammation. 4 , 26 , 27 Sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation was defined as the presence of intra‐amniotic inflammation without microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity. Colonization of the amniotic cavity was defined as the presence of microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity without the concomitant presence of intra‐amniotic inflammation. Negative amniotic fluid for microorganisms and inflammation was defined as the absence of both intra‐amniotic inflammation and microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity.

2.7. Statistical analyses

The normality of the data was tested using the Anderson–Darling test. Continuous variables were compared using the nonparametric Mann–Whitney U or Kruskal–Wallis H tests, as appropriate, and are presented as medians (interquartile ranges [IQR]). Categorical variables were compared using chi‐square or Fisher's exact tests, as appropriate, and are presented as numbers (%). MoMs of PlGF and sFlt‐1, and the sFlt‐1/PlGF ratio were compared between the groups using the Mann–Whitney U test or Kruskal–Wallis H test, as appropriate, and are presented as medians (IQR). Differences were considered statistically significant at P < 0.05. All P‐values were obtained using two‐tailed tests. All statistical analyses were performed using GRAPHPAD PRISM for MacOS (version 9.3.1.), and Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS) (version 28.0.0.0) for Windows (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA).

3. RESULTS

3.1. Demographic and clinical characteristics of the study population

In total, 182 women with PPROM at gestational ages 34+0 and 36+6 weeks were included in the study. Twenty‐five women were excluded due to gestational diabetes mellitus (n = 13), diabetes mellitus (n = 2), gestational hypertension (n = 4), late‐onset preeclampsia (n = 3), fetal growth restriction (n = 2) or chronic hypertension (n = 1). In three women, amniocentesis was not performed. The remaining 154 women were included in the analysis.

Microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and intra‐amniotic inflammation were present in 16% (25/154) and 10% (15/154) of the women, respectively. When the women were divided into four groups based on the presence of microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and/or intra‐amniotic inflammation, intra‐amniotic infection, sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation, colonization of the amniotic cavity and negative amniotic fluid were found in 6% (9/154), 4% (6/154), 10% (16/154) and 80% (123/154), respectively. 4. DISCUSSION

The principal findings of this historical study performed on a cohort of women with singleton pregnancies complicated by late PPROM are as follows: (i) the concentrations of sFlt‐1, PlGF and their ratio in maternal serum differ among the groups of women with intra‐amniotic infection, sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation, colonization of the amniotic cavity and negative amniotic fluid; (ii) the concentrations of sFlt‐1, PlGF and their ratios in the groups are affected by the presence of MVM; (iii) angiogenic imbalance is most pronounced in women without microorganisms and inflammation in amniotic fluid, even in the absence of MVM.

These observations suggest that PPROM pregnancies without microorganisms and inflammation in amniotic fluid and without concurrent MVM: (i) are still associated with angiogenic imbalance, likely non‐placental in origin; and (ii) may have angiogenic imbalance as a major underlying pathophysiology leading to PPROM. From a clinical perspective, the fact that the presence of intra‐amniotic infection, sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation and colonization of the amniotic cavity in late PPROM is associated with low sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios may have a potential relevance. This observation suggests that sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios could be used as a tool for the identification of preeclampsia as well as a simple non‐invasive tool for prediction of the risk of the presence of intra‐amniotic complications (presence of microorganisms and/or inflammation) in women with late PPROM. Low sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios in late PPROM may indicate a high risk of these complications and vice versa.

PlGF and sFtl‐1 represent important angiogenic and anti‐angiogenic markers. They are mainly produced by the placenta, but can also have an extraplacental origin from fetal membranes, endothelial cells, monocytes, macrophages, neutrophils and decidual stromal and other cells. 28 , 29 , 30 PlGF and VEGF are potent angiogenic factors. 31 They promote angiogenesis through endothelial cell proliferation, migration and prevention of apoptosis of these cells. 9 They exert this action through binding on membrane‐bound specific receptors (eg Flt‐1 [VEGF receptor‐1] and VEGF receptor‐2). 9 Soluble forms of these receptors (sFlt‐1 and sVEGF receptor‐2) have anti‐angiogenic activity since they serve as decoy receptors and prevent PlGF and VEGF from binding to Flt‐1 and VEGF receptor‐2. 9

Angiogenic imbalance represents one of the underlying pathologies of PPROM. 28 The angiogenic imbalance in the maternal circulation of women who later develop PPROM can be observed even in the first trimester of the pregnancy 32 and is further pronounced in the second and third trimesters of pregnancy. 8 Romero et al. have recently shown that the earliest gestational age at which concentrations of PlGF, sFlt‐1 and their ratio become different from uncomplicated pregnancy are gestational ages 30, 31 and 27 weeks, respectively. 8 However, in a subset of PPROM pregnancies with MVM, these changes can manifest even earlier; at 26 weeks for PlGF, 28 weeks for sFlt‐1 and 25 weeks for their ratio. 8

In this study, the women with late PPROM were divided in four groups based on the presence or absence of microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and intra‐amniotic inflammation. Differences in concentrations of sFlt‐1 and PlGF, expressed as MoMs, and in sFlt‐1/PlGF ratio among the groups were observed. The highest angiogenic imbalance was seen in the group of women without microorganisms and inflammation in their amniotic fluid. To reach a larger sample size of women with intra‐amniotic inflammatory complications, the groups of the women with intra‐amniotic infection, sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation and colonization of the amniotic cavity were merged into a single group. When concentrations of sFlt‐1 and PlGF and the sFlt‐1/PlGF ratios in maternal serum were compared between this group and the group of women without microorganisms and inflammation in their amniotic fluid, the latter had a higher angiogenic imbalance.

Our observations suggest that angiogenic imbalance may play a role in the pathophysiology of late PPROM, predominantly in a subset of women without microorganisms and inflammation in their amniotic fluid. These results differ from those reported by Espinosa et al.,33 who did not find any difference in maternal plasma concentrations of sFlt‐1 between women with PPROM with and without microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity. However, there are some important differences between these studies: (i) methods to detect microorganisms in amniotic fluid (microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity in the study by Espinosa et al. was defined as a positive amniotic fluid culture for microorganisms);33 (ii) gestational age of PPROM pregnancies (women in their study had a lower gestational age, ranging between 21 and 34 weeks). 33

MVM are traditionally considered a pathologic hallmark of ischemic placental disease due to reduced perfusion of the intervillous space. 34 Kim et al. reported that PPROM and preterm labor with intact membranes had a higher degree of failure of physiologic transformation of the spiral arteries in the myometrial segment of the placental bed compared with uncomplicated delivery at term. 35 , 36 In addition, preterm labor with intact membranes had an even higher frequency of these changes in the decidual portion of spiral arteries. 36 PlGF, sFlt‐1 and the sFlt‐1/PlGF ratio represents specific biomarkers for the presence of these lesions. 8 Therefore, it is not surprising that the subset of women with MVM in the groups of those with colonization of the amniotic cavity, microorganisms and/or inflammation in amniotic fluid, and negative amniotic fluid had higher concentrations of sFlt‐1, higher sFlt‐1/PlGF ratio and lower concentrations of PlGF compared with those without MVM. This difference was not found in the group of the women with intra‐amniotic infection and sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation, likely due to very low sample size in the subgroups (2 and 7 women with intra‐amniotic infection and 4 and 2 women with sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation).

The main strength of this study is a well‐defined phenotype of spontaneous preterm delivery (late PPROM), in which the intra‐amniotic environment was assessed using a comprehensive approach combining nonspecific PCR (sequencing of the 16S rRNA gene); specific PCR for U. spp., M. hominis, and C. trachomatis; anaerobic and anaerobic cultivation; and evaluation of the IL‐6 concentration in amniotic fluid. This comprehensive approach allowed differentiation of the subsets with intra‐amniotic infection, sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation and colonization of the amniotic cavity. Concentrations of sFlt‐1 and PlGF in maternal serum were also assessed with clinically relevant methods broadly used in large biochemical laboratories. This approach provides an opportunity to compare the sFlt‐1/PlGF results with cut‐offs clinically used for preeclampsia.

However, this study also had some limitations. First, the study was performed on a subset of PPROM pregnancies with gestational ages between 34+0 and 36+6 weeks. It would be of interest to perform the same study on a cohort of the women with PPROM between gestational ages 24+0 and 33+6 weeks. Secondly, the sample size of the women with intra‐amniotic infection, sterile intra‐amniotic inflammation and colonization of the amniotic cavity was limited. Therefore, these three groups were merged into a single group to create a group with an appropriate sample size. Thirdly, an intra‐amniotic dye test was not used for the diagnosis of PPROM in women in whom uncertainty remained after sterile speculum examination. Lastly, concentrations of PlGF and sFlt‐1 were not assessed in fresh samples of maternal serum but in the samples that had undergone a single freeze/thaw cycle. However, when samples are stored at –80°C, serum PlGF and sFlt‐1 are stable. 37 , 38

5. CONCLUSION

Among women experiencing late PPROM, angiogenic imbalance in maternal serum is primarily observed in those without both microbial invasion of the amniotic cavity and intra‐amniotic inflammation. Additionally, there is an association between angiogenic imbalance and the presence of MVM.
